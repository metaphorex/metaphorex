The discipline of understanding wholes through the relationships among their
parts. As a source domain, systems thinking supplies a vocabulary of
feedback loops (reinforcing and balancing), stocks and flows, delays,
emergence, and leverage points. It originated in cybernetics (Wiener),
general systems theory (von Bertalanffy), and system dynamics (Forrester),
and entered business strategy through Senge's "The Fifth Discipline."
When we say a problem is "systemic," that an intervention hit a "leverage
point," or that a policy created "unintended consequences," we are drawing
on this frame. Its power lies in making interdependencies visible; its
danger lies in treating every problem as a system problem, which can
paralyze action with complexity.
