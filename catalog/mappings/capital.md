## What It Brings

Latin capitalis from caput -- "head." Wealth was measured by counting heads
of cattle. The same root gives us "chattel" (movable property), "cattle"
(the animals themselves), and "per capita" (per head). "Capitalism" is,
etymologically, "headism" -- an entire economic system named after the
practice of counting cows.

- **Wealth as countable units** -- the headcount metaphor imports the
  idea that wealth is discrete and enumerable. You can count heads of
  cattle the way you count coins or shares. This structural mapping
  persists in modern finance: capital is always quantified, always
  reducible to a number. The metaphor makes wealth legible by making
  it countable, and it makes it countable by mapping it onto bodies
  that can be lined up and tallied. "How much capital do you have?"
  is a question that makes sense only because the dead metaphor
  established wealth as something with a number attached.
- **Wealth as living, reproductive asset** -- cattle are not inert
  stores of value. They breed. A herd of twenty can become a herd of
  forty without the owner doing anything except waiting. The metaphor
  imports the expectation that capital should grow on its own -- that
  wealth properly managed is self-reproducing. "Return on capital,"
  "capital growth," and "capital gains" all carry this buried
  assumption: if your capital is not multiplying, something is wrong.
  The livestock origin makes the expectation of growth feel natural
  rather than ideological.
- **Wealth as mobile property** -- cattle move. Unlike land, which is
  fixed, a herd can be driven to market, relocated, divided, or
  combined. The metaphor maps this mobility onto financial capital:
  capital flows, capital moves across borders, capital is deployed
  and redeployed. "Chattel" (from the same root) meant movable
  property as opposed to real estate. The entire distinction between
  fixed and liquid capital recapitulates the distinction between land
  and livestock.

## Where It Breaks

- **The head was the unit, not the animal** -- modern "capital" has
  completely lost the counting-unit structure. When someone says
  "venture capital" or "human capital," no sense of enumeration
  remains. The metaphor died in a specific way: the living animal
  disappeared but the abstraction of accumulated wealth persisted.
  This is significant because the headcount metaphor originally
  constrained what counted as wealth -- only things you could count
  per head qualified. Once the metaphor died, "capital" expanded to
  cover anything of value: social capital, cultural capital,
  intellectual capital, political capital. Pierre Bourdieu's
  proliferation of capital types would be incoherent if the livestock
  metaphor were still alive. You cannot count heads of cultural
  capital.
- **Cattle were consumable** -- the ultimate purpose of livestock was
  slaughter. Cattle were wealth precisely because they could be
  converted into food, leather, and tallow. Capital-as-cattle implied
  that wealth exists to be consumed. Modern capital theory treats
  consumption of capital as a failure -- you are supposed to preserve
  and grow capital, not eat it. The dead metaphor underwent a
  complete reversal: the livestock origin says wealth is for using up;
  capitalist ideology says wealth is for accumulating. The word
  retained the "wealth" meaning but inverted the relationship to
  consumption.
- **The violence of the headcount** -- "capital punishment" preserves
  the head-as-unit metaphor in its most literal form: punishment
  applied to the head. The same root that gives us the blandest word
  in finance also gives us the word for state-sanctioned killing.
  "Capital" in economics and "capital" in "capital offense" are the
  same metaphor: what matters is counted by heads, and heads can be
  taken. The dead metaphor sanitizes this connection completely --
  nobody discussing capital allocation thinks about decapitation,
  but the etymology links accumulated wealth to bodily vulnerability.
- **"Human capital" resurrects the original grotesquerie** -- when
  economists speak of "human capital," the dead metaphor briefly
  flickers back to life and reveals something uncomfortable. The
  original capital was cattle -- property owned by someone, counted
  by heads, valued for productive output, and ultimately consumable.
  "Human capital" maps this entire structure onto people. The term
  treats people as livestock: countable, ownable, productive, and
  depletable. The dead metaphor's sanitization makes "human capital"
  sound technical rather than dehumanizing, but resurrecting the
  etymology shows it is the language of the stockyard applied to
  the workforce.

## Expressions

- "Capital investment" -- deploying accumulated wealth for productive
  return, where the livestock metaphor maps buying breeding stock onto
  funding ventures
- "Venture capital" -- wealth risked on new enterprises, where the
  headcount origin has been completely abstracted into financial risk
- "Capital gains" -- profit from the appreciation of an asset, where
  the reproductive nature of cattle (herds grow) maps onto the
  expectation that wealth should multiply
- "Human capital" -- people treated as economic assets, where the
  livestock origin is accidentally resurrected
- "Per capita" -- per head, one of the few expressions that preserves
  the literal counting-heads structure
- "Capital punishment" -- punishment applied to the head, preserving
  the caput root in its most violent sense
- "Capitalism" -- the economic system organized around accumulated
  wealth, named after counting cattle

## Origin Story

Latin caput ("head") generated capitalis ("of the head") and then
capitale ("wealth, property"), because Roman wealth was assessed by
counting heads of livestock. The same root branched into Old French
chatel (property), which became English "chattel" (movable property)
and, through a separate line, "cattle" (the animals). The convergence
is not coincidental: cattle were the original chattel, and both words
descend from the same metaphor of head-counting.

In medieval Europe, "capital" meant the principal sum of money as
distinct from interest -- the "head" of a debt from which interest
"grew" like offspring from livestock. This financial sense was well
established by the 13th century. By the 17th century, "capital" had
expanded to mean any accumulated wealth used for productive purposes.

Adam Smith used "capital" throughout *The Wealth of Nations* (1776)
without any awareness that he was deploying a livestock metaphor. By
his time, the word was fully dead -- a technical economic term with no
residual animal scent. Karl Marx adopted the same dead metaphor for
his critique: *Das Kapital* (1867) is, etymologically, "The Cattle."
The irony is considerable: Marx's entire analysis of how capital
treats workers as instruments of production is encoded in a word that
originally referred to treating animals as instruments of wealth.
