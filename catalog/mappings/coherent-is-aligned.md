## What It Brings

Things that make sense together are lined up. Coherence -- the property of
fitting together logically, narratively, or aesthetically -- is understood
through the bodily experience of spatial alignment: objects in a row, edges
that match, surfaces that are flush, components that face the same
direction. When ideas cohere, they are "in line" with each other. When a
plan is coherent, everything "lines up." The metaphor gives abstract
logical consistency a visible, spatial geometry.

Key structural parallels:

- **Logical consistency as alignment** -- "The facts line up." "Her story
  doesn't line up with the evidence." "Everything is in alignment." When
  claims, pieces of evidence, or elements of an argument are consistent
  with each other, they are spatially aligned -- arranged in the same
  direction, pointing the same way. Inconsistency is misalignment: things
  that should be parallel are at angles to each other.
- **Agreement as alignment** -- "We're all on the same page." "They're
  aligned on the key issues." "Let's get everyone in line." When people
  agree, they face the same direction. The metaphor maps interpersonal
  consensus onto the physical arrangement of objects sharing an
  orientation. Disagreement is being "at cross purposes" -- facing
  different directions.
- **Organizational coherence as structural alignment** -- "The team is
  well-aligned." "Strategy and execution are out of alignment." "We need
  to align our goals with our resources." Organizations cohere when their
  parts point the same way. Misalignment between departments, goals, or
  values is understood as components that are physically askew relative
  to each other.
- **Moral consistency as straightness** -- "He's a straight shooter."
  "She stayed on the straight and narrow." "That's a crooked deal." The
  alignment metaphor extends from logical to moral coherence: a person
  of integrity is straight (aligned with principles), while a dishonest
  person is crooked (misaligned). This sub-mapping connects COHERENT IS
  ALIGNED to the broader metaphor family of MORALITY IS STRAIGHTNESS.
- **Incoherence as disarray** -- "His argument is all over the place."
  "Nothing adds up." "The pieces don't fit together." When coherence
  fails, the spatial order collapses. The components scatter, face random
  directions, and no longer form a recognizable pattern. Incoherence is
  mess, and mess is the absence of alignment.

## Where It Breaks

- **Coherence is not always linear** -- the alignment metaphor privileges
  a particular kind of order: parallel lines, shared orientation, straight
  edges. But many forms of coherence are non-linear. A musical composition
  coheres through harmonic relationships that have no natural spatial
  analog. A novel coheres through thematic resonance, callback, and
  irony -- none of which look like things "lining up." The metaphor makes
  linear coherence the prototype and marginalizes other kinds.
- **The metaphor conflates agreement with correctness** -- "aligned"
  carries a positive valence. When people or ideas are aligned, the
  metaphor implies this is good. But alignment can be pathological:
  groupthink is perfect alignment, and it produces catastrophically bad
  decisions. The metaphor has no vocabulary for distinguishing productive
  alignment from conformist alignment.
- **Alignment assumes a reference axis** -- for things to be "in line,"
  there must be a line they are in relative to. But in abstract domains,
  the reference axis is often arbitrary or contested. "Aligning with the
  company's values" assumes the company's values provide the axis, but
  someone else might choose a different reference frame. The metaphor
  naturalizes whichever axis is taken as the standard.
- **The metaphor hides productive tension** -- in many creative and
  intellectual domains, coherence emerges from the interaction of elements
  that are not aligned but are in productive tension. A good argument may
  involve competing considerations held in balance, not ideas "lined up."
  The alignment metaphor makes dialectical coherence -- coherence through
  opposition -- nearly unthinkable.
- **Complexity requires more than alignment** -- a complex system can be
  highly coherent without any of its parts being aligned in any spatial
  sense. An ecosystem coheres through feedback loops, competition, and
  symbiosis. The metaphor cannot represent this kind of emergent coherence
  because it requires parts to share an orientation, not merely to
  interact productively.

## Expressions

- "The facts line up" -- logical consistency as spatial alignment
- "We need to get aligned on this" -- achieving agreement as arranging
  components in the same direction
- "Her story doesn't add up" -- incoherence as failure to form a
  consistent arrangement
- "Everything fell into place" -- coherence achieved as components
  reaching their correct positions
- "They're on the same page" -- agreement as co-location in a shared
  reference frame
- "That's out of line" -- behavior inconsistent with norms as spatial
  deviation from alignment
- "Straighten this out" -- resolving incoherence as correcting
  misalignment
- "The plan is all over the place" -- incoherence as spatial disorder
- "We're at cross purposes" -- disagreement as perpendicular orientations
- "Keep things in order" -- maintaining coherence as maintaining spatial
  arrangement

## Origin Story

COHERENT IS ALIGNED appears in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson
& Schwartz 1991). It belongs to a cluster of metaphors that map spatial
order onto abstract order -- related to MORALITY IS STRAIGHTNESS, RATIONAL
IS UP, and the broader orientational metaphor system. The experiential
grounding is in the bodily perception of alignment: from early childhood,
humans notice when things are "straight" versus "crooked," when edges
match versus when they do not, when objects face the same direction versus
when they are jumbled. This perceptual capacity for detecting spatial
alignment is recruited to evaluate abstract coherence -- the "fit" of
ideas, plans, values, and actions.

The metaphor is especially productive in organizational and strategic
discourse, where "alignment" has become a near-technical term. A
consultant who says "we need strategic alignment" is deploying this
conceptual metaphor, perhaps without realizing that the word "alignment"
began its life describing objects arranged in a physical row.
