## What It Brings

A problem is a place you find yourself in -- a swamp, a thicket, a
valley, a maze. This metaphor maps regions of a physical landscape onto
problems, so that having a problem is being located in difficult terrain
and solving it is finding your way out. Unlike the locked-container
metaphor, which makes problems into discrete objects you act on, this
metaphor makes problems into environments you inhabit and must navigate.

Key structural parallels:

- **The problem as a region you occupy** -- "We're stuck in a mess."
  "He found himself in a difficult situation." "She wandered into
  trouble." The problem is not something you look at from outside; it
  is a place you are inside. You entered it (willingly or not), you
  are surrounded by it, and you must find a way out.
- **Terrain features as problem characteristics** -- "It's a minefield."
  "We're in a swamp." "He's lost in a maze of regulations." Different
  kinds of landscape map onto different kinds of difficulty. Swamps are
  problems that slow and trap. Mazes are problems of complexity and
  disorientation. Minefields are problems where any step could be
  catastrophic. The landscape vocabulary provides a rich typology of
  difficulties.
- **Boundaries and exits** -- "We need to find a way out." "There's no
  exit from this situation." "She saw light at the edge of the forest."
  Every region has edges, and the metaphor maps these onto the
  transition from problem to solution. Solving the problem is reaching
  the boundary and crossing it. If there is no boundary, the problem
  is infinite -- a desert with no edge.
- **Orientation and disorientation** -- "I'm lost." "We need to get our
  bearings." "He can't see the forest for the trees." Being in a
  problem-region means potentially not knowing where you are or which
  direction leads out. The metaphor maps confusion and uncertainty
  onto spatial disorientation -- a deeply embodied, pre-linguistic
  experience.
- **Paths through the problem** -- "We need to find a way through this."
  "She charted a path out of the crisis." "There's no clear route
  forward." The landscape contains paths, or fails to. Some regions
  have well-worn trails; others are trackless wilderness. The
  availability of paths maps onto the availability of known methods
  for solving the problem.

## Where It Breaks

- **Landscapes are given; problems are often constructed** -- a physical
  landscape exists independently of the traveler. But many problems are
  not pre-existing terrain that you wander into; they are created by
  decisions, systems, and other people. The landscape metaphor makes
  problems feel natural and fixed, obscuring the fact that someone or
  something made them. A policy problem is not a swamp you stumbled
  into -- it is a swamp someone built.
- **Finding your way out is not always the right goal** -- the metaphor
  assumes the objective is to leave the problem-region. But some
  problems must be lived in rather than escaped. Chronic illness, grief,
  structural poverty -- these are regions you may never leave, and the
  metaphor's insistence on exit can make permanent residence feel like
  permanent failure.
- **The metaphor flattens the problem's internal structure** -- a region
  is a bounded area, but it does not have the articulated internal
  structure that many problems have. A legal dispute has parties,
  precedents, statutes, and procedures. Mapping it onto "a thicket" or
  "a maze" captures the felt experience of complexity but loses the
  specific logical structure that distinguishes one problem from another.
- **Scale is ambiguous** -- is the problem a puddle or a continent? The
  landscape metaphor provides no intrinsic scale. "We're in a mess"
  could mean a five-minute inconvenience or a decade-long institutional
  crisis. The metaphor vividly communicates the quality of difficulty
  (swampy, thorny, labyrinthine) but poorly communicates its magnitude.
- **The solver is alone in the landscape** -- the default image is a
  single person in difficult terrain. But most serious problems are
  collective: many people are in the same region, and coordination
  among them is part of the solution. The landscape metaphor makes
  problem-solving feel like individual pathfinding rather than
  collective action.

## Expressions

- "We're in a real mess" -- a problem as a chaotic region
- "He's lost in a maze of regulations" -- complexity as labyrinthine
  terrain
- "She found herself in a difficult situation" -- entering a problem
  as entering a region
- "We need to find a way out of this" -- solution as exit from a region
- "It's a minefield" -- a problem where any move is dangerous
- "They're stuck in a swamp of bureaucracy" -- slow, trapping difficulty
  as boggy terrain
- "I can't see the forest for the trees" -- disorientation within a
  problem-region
- "There's light at the end of the tunnel" -- approaching the boundary
  of the problem
- "We need to get our bearings" -- reorienting within a confusing problem
- "He wandered into trouble" -- entering a problem-region accidentally
- "Uncharted territory" -- a problem without known paths or methods

## Origin Story

The Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson, and Schwartz 1991) catalogs
A PROBLEM IS A REGION IN A LANDSCAPE alongside the body-of-water and
locked-container variants. Together, these three PROBLEM metaphors
illustrate how a single target domain (difficulty, problem-solving) can
be structured by multiple, non-competing source domains, each
highlighting different aspects of the experience.

The region-in-a-landscape variant is closely connected to the broader
Event Structure metaphor system, where STATES ARE LOCATIONS and ACTION
IS MOTION. If your current state is a location, and a problematic state
is a bad location, then a problem is simply a bad region in the
landscape of possible states. Solving the problem is moving to a better
region. This makes the PROBLEM IS A REGION metaphor a natural extension
of the spatial logic that pervades the entire Event Structure system.

The metaphor is especially prominent in military, exploration, and
therapeutic language. Military strategists speak of "difficult terrain"
both literally and metaphorically. Therapists help clients "navigate"
emotional landscapes. Explorers encounter "uncharted territory." In
each case, the spatial framing transforms abstract difficulty into
something that can be mapped, traversed, and ultimately left behind.
