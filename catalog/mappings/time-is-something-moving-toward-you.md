## What It Brings

One of the two fundamental ways English speakers conceptualize time's
relationship to the self. In this frame -- the "moving time" metaphor --
the observer stands still while time flows toward and past them, like objects
on a river or a parade passing a reviewing stand. The future approaches from
ahead, arrives at the present, and recedes into the past behind.

This is the complement of the "moving ego" metaphor (where the self travels
forward through a temporal landscape). Both are grounded in the same
underlying correlation between motion and temporal succession, but they
assign the roles differently: here, time is the mover and the experiencer
is stationary.

Key structural parallels:

- **Time as approaching entity** -- future events come toward the observer.
  "The deadline is approaching" and "Christmas is coming" treat temporal
  events as objects with trajectories, converging on a fixed self.
- **Arrival as present** -- when the moving time reaches the observer, it
  becomes the present moment. "The moment has arrived" and "when the time
  comes" mark the contact between approaching event and stationary self.
- **Passing as past** -- once the moment passes the observer, it recedes
  into the past. "That opportunity has passed" and "the crisis is behind
  us" treat the past as spatially behind the observer, where departed
  objects go.
- **Speed of approach** -- time moves at variable speeds. "Time flies" when
  events approach rapidly. "Time crawls" when the next event seems far away
  and slow to arrive. The metaphor imports velocity from physical motion.
- **Sequence as spatial order** -- events are ordered by their position in
  the approaching stream. "The next few weeks" places future events in a
  queue. "One thing after another" describes serial arrival.

The moving-time frame dominates in contexts of anticipation and patience.
When we wait, we imagine time coming to us, not ourselves traveling to it.

## Where It Breaks

- **The observer is not actually stationary** -- the metaphor places the
  self at a fixed point while time flows past. But lived experience includes
  agency: we move toward goals, pursue plans, rush to meet deadlines. The
  moving-time frame erases the experiencer's active role in shaping what
  happens, casting them as a passive recipient of arriving events.
- **Time has no trajectory** -- physical objects approaching you have a
  path, a speed, and a direction you can perceive. Future events have none
  of these. You cannot see the deadline "getting closer" the way you see a
  car approaching. The metaphor imports perceptual affordances (tracking,
  estimating arrival time) that temporal experience does not actually
  provide. This is why deadlines consistently surprise people -- the
  approach metaphor promises visibility that reality withholds.
- **Contradiction with the moving-ego frame** -- English speakers routinely
  switch between "the meeting was moved forward" (ambiguous between moving-
  time and moving-ego interpretations). Research by Boroditsky and Ramscar
  (2002) showed that which frame is active at a given moment depends on
  recent spatial experience: people who had just walked through a line
  adopted the moving-ego frame, while those who had pulled objects toward
  themselves adopted the moving-time frame. The two metaphors are
  incompatible in their spatial logic, yet speakers shift between them
  without noticing.
- **The metaphor hides causal structure** -- when events "come at you,"
  they seem to arrive from nowhere, as if generated by the future itself.
  The actual causes -- decisions, processes, systemic conditions -- are
  invisible. "A recession is coming" frames it as weather, not as the
  consequence of identifiable economic choices.
- **Cultural variation** -- the Aymara people of the Andes use a
  reverse spatial mapping: the past is in front (visible, known) and the
  future is behind (unseen). The moving-time metaphor with the future
  approaching from ahead is not universal but reflects a particular
  cultural choice about spatial orientation.

## Expressions

- "The deadline is approaching" -- temporal event as object with a
  trajectory aimed at the observer
- "Christmas is coming" -- a calendar date as an entity in motion
- "Time flies when you're having fun" -- temporal passage as high-speed
  flight past the self
- "The moment has arrived" -- present as the point of contact between
  moving time and stationary observer
- "That's all behind us now" -- the past as spatially behind, where
  departed entities recede
- "In the weeks ahead" -- future events as spatially ahead in the
  approaching stream
- "The opportunity has passed" -- a missed event as an object that moved
  past without being grasped
- "We're coming up on the anniversary" -- hybrid expression blending
  moving-time with moving-ego

## Origin Story

The moving-time metaphor is documented in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff,
Espenson & Schwartz, 1991) as one of two core spatial metaphors for time,
alongside TIME IS A LANDSCAPE WE MOVE THROUGH (the moving-ego variant).
Lakoff and Johnson discussed the duality in *Philosophy in the Flesh* (1999),
arguing that both arise from the same fundamental correlation between motion
and temporal change but assign figure and ground differently.

The experimental dissection came from Boroditsky and Ramscar (2002), who
demonstrated that the two frames are psychologically real and contextually
activated. Their classic "Wednesday's meeting" experiment showed that
people's interpretation of "the meeting has been moved forward two days"
depends on which spatial frame they are currently primed for -- moving-time
yields Friday, moving-ego yields Monday.
