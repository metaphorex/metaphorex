## What It Brings

In Aphorisms XXXIX and XLII of the *Novum Organum* (1620), Bacon
describes the second class of cognitive error: "For every one (besides
the errors common to human nature in general) has a cave or den of his
own, which refracts and discolors the light of nature." Where tribal
idols afflict the entire species, cave idols are personal -- shaped
by each individual's temperament, education, reading, habits, and the
authorities they happen to admire.

Bacon deliberately echoes Plato's cave allegory from the *Republic*,
but with a crucial twist: Plato describes *humanity* trapped in *one*
cave, watching shared shadows. Bacon personalizes the image -- each
person has their *own* cave, producing their *own* private distortions.
The problem is not universal illusion but individual idiosyncrasy
mistaken for objective perception.

Key structural parallels:

- **The cave as enclosure** -- the individual mind is a confined space.
  What reaches it is filtered, refracted, diminished. You cannot see
  what your cave's geometry blocks. Education shapes the cave's
  dimensions; temperament determines its orientation toward or away
  from the light.
- **Refraction, not blindness** -- the cave does not prevent perception;
  it distorts it. The person inside sees *something*, and what they see
  feels real. This makes cave idols harder to correct than simple
  ignorance, because the sufferer has evidence -- just bent evidence.
- **The authority figure as cave-architect** -- Bacon notes that "some
  men become attached to certain particular sciences and speculations,
  either because they fancy themselves the authors and inventors
  thereof, or because they have bestowed the greatest pains upon them."
  Your intellectual heroes shape your cave.
- **Solitude as reinforcement** -- the cave is private. Without
  exposure to other perspectives, its distortions compound. Bacon
  implicitly argues for collaborative inquiry as a corrective -- you
  need other people's caves to triangulate.

## Where It Breaks

- **The source frame is a natural cave, not a built structure.** The
  manifest assigns `architecture-and-building` as the source frame,
  but Bacon's metaphor is geological, not architectural. A cave is
  found, not constructed. This matters because it implies the
  individual's biases are natural formations rather than designed
  enclosures -- more like personality than like prison. The
  architecture frame suggests intentional construction and the
  possibility of renovation, which overstates the degree of control
  a person has over their own cognitive enclosure.
- **The metaphor underestimates how caves are socially constructed.**
  Bacon treats the cave as individual, but modern sociology of
  knowledge shows that "personal" biases are largely shaped by group
  membership -- class, profession, nationality, era. Your cave is
  not really yours; it was furnished by your social context. The
  individual framing obscures structural causes.
- **Knowing your cave does not let you leave it.** Bacon implies that
  awareness of personal bias is a step toward correcting it. But the
  Dunning-Kruger effect and the bias blind spot show that people are
  systematically poor at identifying their own distortions, even when
  they know distortions exist in general. The cave has no exit sign.
- **Bacon himself was in a cave.** His rejection of Copernican
  heliocentrism reflected his own formation -- his attachment to
  certain authorities and his temperamental preference for gradual
  reform over revolutionary revision. The man who named the cave idol
  was demonstrably inside one.
- **The metaphor's moral tone is counterproductive.** Calling someone's
  perspective a "cave" implies they are trapped and benighted. In
  practice, telling someone they are in a cave provokes defensiveness,
  not enlightenment. The metaphor is better at diagnosing others than
  at reforming oneself.

## Expressions

- "Filter bubble" -- the algorithmic cave: recommendation systems
  that show you more of what you already believe, personalizing the
  distortion exactly as Bacon described
- "Echo chamber" -- the social cave: communities where dissent is
  absent and agreement reinforces existing belief
- "Confirmation bias" -- overlaps with tribal idols, but the cave
  version is personal: seeking evidence that confirms *your specific*
  prior commitments
- "Curse of expertise" -- deep specialization narrows the cave.
  The expert sees everything through one lens and mistakes that lens
  for clear glass
- "Dunning-Kruger effect" -- the cave's cruelest property: the less
  you know, the less you can perceive how little you know
- "Motivated reasoning" -- the cave's lighting system: you see what
  you want to see, and the desire precedes the perception
- "Professional deformation" (*deformation professionnelle*) -- the
  French term for how one's profession shapes one's worldview, a
  direct descendant of Bacon's cave idol
- "When you have a hammer, everything looks like a nail" -- Maslow's
  formulation of the cave idol, the tool becoming the lens

## Origin Story

Bacon's Four Idols form a taxonomy of cognitive error organized by
scope: species-wide (Tribe), individual (Cave), social-linguistic
(Marketplace), and institutional (Theatre). The cave is second because
it is the most intimate -- the errors you carry with you regardless
of what language you speak or what tradition you belong to.

The Platonic allusion is explicit and deliberate. Bacon signals that
he is updating the classical tradition, not breaking with it. But
where Plato's cave allegory is about the human condition as such
(everyone shares one cave), Bacon's version is about individual
variation -- the recognition that cognitive distortion has both
universal and personal components. This distinction anticipates the
modern division between cognitive biases (universal heuristics) and
individual differences (personality, expertise, identity-based
perception).
