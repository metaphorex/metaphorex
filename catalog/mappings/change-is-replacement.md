## What It Brings

To change is to swap one thing for another. Where CHANGE IS MOTION treats
change as a single entity moving between locations, CHANGE IS REPLACEMENT
treats it as the removal of one entity and the installation of another in
its place. The old thing goes away; a new thing appears. Change is not
movement but substitution -- and the result is understood not as the same
thing in a new state but as a different thing altogether.

Key structural parallels:

- **Changed properties as replaced parts** -- "She got a new outlook on
  life." "He's a changed man -- practically a different person." "The
  neighborhood has been completely transformed; it's unrecognizable." When
  something changes, the old version is gone and the new version has taken
  its place. The metaphor treats properties, attitudes, and conditions as
  removable, replaceable components.
- **Improvement as upgrade** -- "They replaced the old system with a better
  one." "She traded up." "It's time for a change of leadership." Positive
  change is replacing an inferior component with a superior one. The old
  part was faulty, worn out, or obsolete; the new part is an improvement.
  This is the logic of consumer culture applied to states of affairs.
- **Deterioration as degradation of the replacement** -- "The replacement
  was worse than the original." "The cure was worse than the disease."
  "What they put in its place was no better." Negative change is a bad
  swap -- the new thing is worse than what it replaced. The metaphor
  makes evaluation easy: compare the old part with the new one.
- **Reversibility as re-replacement** -- "Can we go back to the old way?"
  "Restore the original settings." "Put things back the way they were."
  If change is replacement, then undoing change is swapping back. This
  creates an expectation of reversibility: if you kept the old part, you
  can reinstall it. The metaphor makes change feel less permanent than it
  often is.
- **Gradual change as piecemeal replacement** -- "They changed one thing
  at a time." "Bit by bit, everything was different." "The ship of Theseus"
  paradox. When change happens incrementally, the metaphor codes it as
  replacing parts one by one, raising the question: at what point has the
  whole thing been replaced?

## Where It Breaks

- **The metaphor assumes discrete, separable components** -- for replacement
  to work, you need identifiable parts that can be removed and swapped.
  But many changes are not like this. Emotional maturation, cultural drift,
  and the gradual evolution of a language do not involve the removal of one
  discrete thing and the installation of another. The metaphor imposes a
  modular structure on processes that are continuous and holistic.
- **Identity becomes paradoxical** -- if change is replacement, then
  anything that has changed enough is no longer the same thing. "She's a
  completely different person" -- taken literally, this denies personal
  continuity. The metaphor creates the Ship of Theseus problem wherever
  it is applied: if you replace every part, is it still the same entity?
  This is not a problem for CHANGE IS MOTION, where the moving entity
  persists through its displacement.
- **The metaphor hides the process of transformation** -- replacement is
  instantaneous: the old part comes out, the new part goes in. There is
  no in-between state. But real change often involves a messy, gradual
  process of transformation where old and new coexist, blend, and
  negotiate. The replacement metaphor skips the transition.
- **It implies an external agent** -- someone does the replacing. The
  metaphor works best when there is a designer, mechanic, or reformer
  who decides what to swap. But many changes are self-organizing, emergent,
  or unintended. When we say "democracy replaced monarchy," we are
  attributing agent-like behavior to a diffuse historical process.
- **The old part is discarded** -- in the replacement model, what was
  removed is gone. But in many real changes, the previous state is not
  erased; it persists as memory, sediment, or residue. Cultural change
  layers the new atop the old rather than replacing it. The metaphor
  cannot represent palimpsest -- only clean swaps.

## Expressions

- "She's a changed person" -- personal transformation as replacement of
  the old self
- "Time for a change" -- the current state as an old part ready for
  swapping
- "We need new leadership" -- changing leadership as replacing a component
- "The new policy replaced the old one" -- institutional change as
  substitution
- "Out with the old, in with the new" -- change as explicit removal and
  installation
- "He traded his old habits for new ones" -- behavioral change as
  exchanging parts
- "Put things back the way they were" -- reversing change as re-replacement
- "A change of scenery" -- altered environment as substituted backdrop
- "That changes everything" -- comprehensive impact as total replacement
- "Nothing can replace what was lost" -- irreversible change as a part
  that cannot be re-swapped

## Origin Story

CHANGE IS REPLACEMENT appears in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff,
Espenson & Schwartz 1991) as an alternative to CHANGE IS MOTION within
the Event Structure metaphor system. Where CHANGE IS MOTION belongs to
the location case of event structure (states are locations, change is
movement between them), CHANGE IS REPLACEMENT belongs to the object case
(properties are possessions, change is acquiring new ones and losing old
ones). The two metaphors provide complementary ways of understanding the
same phenomenon: motion emphasizes continuity through change, while
replacement emphasizes discontinuity.

The experiential grounding is in early object manipulation: a child removes
one block and puts another in its place. The correlation between the
physical act of substitution and the resulting difference in state provides
the embodied basis for understanding abstract change as replacement.
