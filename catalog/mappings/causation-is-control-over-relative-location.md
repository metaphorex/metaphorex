## What It Brings

To cause something is to put it where you want it. This metaphor maps the
bodily experience of physically positioning objects onto abstract causation:
the cause is an agent who controls where something ends up relative to
something else. A parent places a child in a chair. A gardener moves a plant
into sunlight. A chess player positions a piece to threaten the opponent's
king. In each case, the causal act is an act of spatial arrangement -- and
the effect is a new configuration of relative positions.

Key structural parallels:

- **Causing as positioning** -- "She put him in a difficult situation." "The
  policy placed the economy on a dangerous path." "He set things in order."
  The cause is an agent who moves an entity from one location to another.
  The effect is the new location. This is not just force (pushing) but
  deliberate placement -- the causer controls not just that something moves
  but where it ends up.
- **Preventing as keeping in place** -- "She held him back." "The regulation
  kept prices in check." "Nothing could keep him down." If causing is
  repositioning, then preventing is maintaining current position against
  forces that would displace. The causer exerts control by fixing relative
  location rather than changing it.
- **Enabling as clearing the way** -- "She opened up opportunities for him."
  "The reform removed barriers to entry." "That freed him to pursue his
  goals." Enabling is making a new relative location accessible -- removing
  obstacles between here and there so the entity can occupy the desired
  position.
- **Loss of control as displacement** -- "Things got out of hand." "The
  situation spiraled out of control." "Events overtook him." When the causal
  agent loses control over relative positioning, the entities move to
  unintended locations. Chaos is the failure of spatial arrangement.
- **Degree of causation as precision of placement** -- "She put him exactly
  where she wanted him." "The intervention was well-targeted." "The policy
  was a blunt instrument." Fine-grained causation is precise positioning;
  crude causation is rough placement. The metaphor gives causation a
  spatial resolution.

## Where It Breaks

- **Not all causation involves an agent with spatial intent** -- the metaphor
  requires someone who controls placement. But many causes are agentless
  processes: weather causes floods, mutations cause disease, market forces
  cause unemployment. The metaphor imports an assumption of intentional
  spatial arrangement that makes agentless causation hard to express. When
  we say "the recession put millions out of work," we are smuggling in an
  agent (the recession as a placer) that does not exist.
- **Relative location implies a fixed reference frame** -- the metaphor
  assumes there is a stable background against which positions are measured.
  But in complex causal systems, the reference frame itself shifts. A
  political revolution changes not just where things are but what "where"
  means. The metaphor cannot represent situations where the coordinate
  system itself is transformed by the cause.
- **The metaphor privileges spatial over temporal causation** -- causes that
  operate through timing (a catalyst that triggers a chain reaction, a
  window of opportunity that closes) are awkwardly represented as
  positioning. The metaphor has no natural way to express "he caused it by
  doing it at the right moment" without converting the temporal structure
  into a spatial one.
- **Control is binary in the metaphor but graded in reality** -- you either
  control where something is or you do not. But real causation involves
  degrees of influence: partial causes, contributing factors, probabilistic
  effects. The metaphor makes it hard to say "she somewhat caused it" --
  you either placed it or you did not.
- **The metaphor obscures emergent causation** -- when multiple positioned
  elements interact to produce an effect that none of them individually
  caused, the positioning frame has no vocabulary for emergence. The whole
  is not a location that any single agent placed anything in.

## Expressions

- "She put him in his place" -- establishing someone's social position
  through causal action
- "He set the wheels in motion" -- causing a process by positioning its
  components
- "The decision placed them at a disadvantage" -- causing a negative state
  through repositioning
- "She maneuvered him into agreeing" -- causing agreement through
  strategic positioning
- "He kept her at arm's length" -- preventing intimacy by maintaining
  spatial distance
- "The policy put the country on a new course" -- causing directional
  change through placement
- "Things fell into place" -- effects arriving at their correct relative
  positions (without explicit agent)
- "She positioned herself for success" -- self-caused advantageous
  arrangement
- "He displaced the incumbent" -- causing removal by occupying another's
  position
- "The crisis threw everything out of alignment" -- loss of controlled
  relative positioning

## Origin Story

This metaphor appears in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson &
Schwartz 1991) as part of the causation cluster within the Event Structure
metaphor system. It is one of several causation metaphors that extend the
basic STATES ARE LOCATIONS mapping: if states are locations, and change is
motion between locations, then causation is control over that motion --
specifically, control over where things end up relative to each other.

The metaphor is grounded in the earliest manipulative experience. Before
a child has any concept of abstract causation, it learns that it can
cause things to happen by putting objects in specific places: the block
on top of the tower, the spoon in the bowl, the toy out of reach. The
correlation between spatial arrangement and resulting state is one of the
first causal regularities a human body discovers.
