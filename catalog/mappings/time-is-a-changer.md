## What It Brings

Time does not merely pass -- it *acts*. It alters, transforms, erodes,
heals, and destroys. TIME IS A CHANGER personifies temporal passage as an
agent who modifies everything it touches. Where TIME IS MONEY treats time as
a resource to be managed, and TIME IS A MOVING OBJECT treats it as something
that flows past us, this metaphor grants time causal power. Time is not a
medium or a quantity but a force that reshapes the world.

Key structural parallels:

- **Agent of transformation** -- time *changes* things. "Time has changed
  her" treats temporal passage as an entity that acts upon people. The
  metaphor lets us attribute causality to duration itself, rather than to
  the specific physical, biological, or social processes that actually
  produce change.
- **Healer** -- "Time heals all wounds." Here the changer is benevolent,
  a physician who restores what was damaged. The metaphor converts the
  gradual psychological process of adaptation into an external agent's
  deliberate care.
- **Destroyer** -- "Time destroys everything in the end." The changer as
  entropy personified. Buildings crumble, memories fade, empires fall -- and
  the metaphor assigns all of this to a single agent rather than to the
  distinct mechanisms of decay.
- **Revealer** -- "Time will tell." The changer discloses hidden truths.
  What is concealed now will be exposed by time's action, as if time were
  an investigator uncovering evidence.
- **Sculptor** -- "Time has sculpted the landscape." The changer as artist,
  carving features into the world through patient, deliberate work. This
  variant aestheticizes entropy, making geological erosion feel intentional.

## Where It Breaks

- **Time does not cause anything** -- this is the deepest problem. Rust
  causes iron to decay, grief causes emotional change, erosion causes
  landscapes to shift. Time is the dimension in which these processes
  occur, not the agent performing them. The metaphor collapses hundreds of
  distinct causal mechanisms into a single personified force, which feels
  explanatory but explains nothing. Saying "time heals" obscures the
  specific psychological work -- reappraisal, habituation, new attachment
  formation -- that actually produces recovery.
- **The metaphor discourages action** -- if time is the agent of change,
  then waiting is a strategy. "Give it time" can be wise advice about
  patience, but it can also be a counsel of passivity that delays necessary
  intervention. The metaphor provides cover for inaction by attributing
  future improvement to an automatic process.
- **Selective attribution** -- we invoke time as changer mainly for
  negative transformations (aging, decay, loss) and for healing. We rarely
  say "time made her a better pianist" -- we attribute positive skill
  development to effort, practice, and talent. The metaphor is
  asymmetrically applied: time gets credit for healing and blame for
  deterioration, while human agency gets credit for achievement.
- **The changer is inconsistent** -- time "heals" and time "destroys."
  These are contradictory roles for the same agent. The metaphor works by
  cherry-picking: we invoke the healer when consoling, the destroyer when
  mourning, and never notice we are describing opposite behaviors by the
  same entity.
- **Cultural variation** -- not all languages personify time as a changer.
  Some frame temporal change as a quality of objects themselves (things
  ripen, things age) rather than as the action of an external agent. The
  English tendency to externalize causality onto time is a linguistic habit,
  not a universal cognitive structure.

## Expressions

- "Time heals all wounds" -- temporal passage as therapeutic agent
- "Time has changed her" -- personified time acting on a person
- "The ravages of time" -- time as destroyer, leaving damage behind
- "Time will tell" -- time as revealer of hidden truth
- "Time has not been kind to him" -- time as agent capable of cruelty
  or mercy
- "Time erodes everything" -- geological patience applied to all
  phenomena
- "Give it time" -- prescription of patience, with time as the healer
  who needs no help
- "Time waits for no one" -- the changer who acts on its own schedule
- "Time has a way of sorting things out" -- time as organizer with
  implicit wisdom
- "Weathered by time" -- the changer's cumulative effect made visible

## Origin Story

TIME IS A CHANGER appears in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson &
Schwartz, 1991) and the Osaka University Conceptual Metaphor archive. It
belongs to a cluster of TIME metaphors that collectively structure how
English speakers experience duration: TIME IS MONEY (resource), TIME IS A
MOVING OBJECT (flow), TIME IS A LANDSCAPE WE MOVE THROUGH (journey), and
TIME IS A CHANGER (agent).

The personification of time as an agent has deep literary roots: Greek
Chronos devouring his children, Shakespeare's "Devouring Time" in Sonnet
19, and the medieval figure of Father Time with his scythe. The conceptual
metaphor underlying these images is old enough that it feels like a
description of reality rather than a figure of speech.
