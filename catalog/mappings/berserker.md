## What It Brings

The berserkers were Norse warriors who fought in a trance-like fury,
reportedly biting their shields, howling, and becoming impervious to
pain. The word likely derives from "bear-shirt" (ber-serkr) -- warriors
who wore animal skins and channeled the ferocity of bears or wolves.
When modern English speakers say someone "went berserk," the Norse
warrior has vanished entirely from their awareness. What remains is
a pure mapping: loss of rational control replaced by destructive,
undirected fury.

The dead metaphor preserves specific structural features of the
original berserker state:

- **Sudden onset** -- berserker rage was not a gradual escalation but a
  transformation. The warrior crossed a threshold from human to
  something else. "Going berserk" in modern usage preserves this
  suddenness: a calm person snaps, a stable system catastrophically
  fails, a meeting devolves in an instant. The metaphor encodes a
  phase transition, not a slide.
- **Loss of discrimination** -- the berserker attacked friend and foe
  alike. The sagas describe berserkers as dangerous to their own side.
  When we say someone "went berserk," we mean they lost the capacity
  to distinguish appropriate from inappropriate targets. The metaphor
  maps onto any situation where a force designed for directed action
  becomes indiscriminate: an immune system attacking the body's own
  cells, a security system locking out legitimate users, a manager
  firing everyone in the department.
- **Superhuman intensity** -- the berserker state was credited with
  extraordinary strength and endurance. "Going berserk" implies not
  just irrationality but an intensity that exceeds normal parameters.
  A berserk system does not merely malfunction; it actively and
  energetically destroys things at a rate that surprises everyone.
- **Aftermath of exhaustion** -- the sagas report that berserkers
  collapsed into extreme weakness after their fury passed. The
  metaphor imports this crash: after someone goes berserk, there is
  a period of depleted, enfeebled quiet. The system that thrashed
  wildly now sits idle. The person who raged sits stunned.

## Where It Breaks

- **The original berserkers chose it** -- the Norse berserker state
  was cultivated, possibly through ritual, possibly through substances.
  It was a military technique, not a mental illness. But "going
  berserk" in modern usage implies involuntary loss of control --
  something that happens to you, not something you do. The metaphor
  strips away the intentionality that defined the original practice.
- **Berserker rage was effective** -- berserkers won battles. Their
  fury was terrifying and strategically useful. But the modern metaphor
  treats going berserk as pure dysfunction. Nobody says "the server
  went berserk" and means it performed exceptionally well. The
  metaphor has inverted the valence: what was once martial excellence
  is now systems failure.
- **The metaphor erases the sacred dimension** -- berserker rage was
  connected to Odin, the god of war, fury, and wisdom. The
  transformation was understood as divine possession, a state between
  human and divine. Modern usage has no trace of this -- "going
  berserk" is purely secular, purely negative. The sacred frame that
  gave the original its meaning has been completely shed.
- **"Berserk" has become a synonym for "very angry"** -- in casual
  usage, "she went berserk" can mean nothing more than "she was very
  upset." The metaphor has drifted so far from its source that it no
  longer reliably maps onto loss of rational control at all. It has
  become a generic intensifier, which is the final stage of
  metaphorical death.

## Expressions

- "The server went berserk and started dropping connections" -- a
  system behaving erratically and destructively, with no human
  awareness of Norse warriors
- "He went berserk when he saw the bill" -- extreme anger, now
  several metaphorical layers removed from bear-shirt warriors
- "Berserk mode" -- in gaming, a state of increased power at the
  cost of control, one of the few modern usages that preserves the
  original trade-off
- "The algorithm went berserk on the training data" -- a machine
  learning model behaving erratically, applying the metaphor to
  software with no awareness of its etymology
- "Going postal" -- a parallel construction that shows how "going
  berserk" has become a template for "going [adjective that implies
  violent loss of control]"

## Origin Story

Old Norse *berserkr* appears in sagas from the 13th century onward,
though the warriors it describes are set in earlier periods. Snorri
Sturluson's *Ynglinga Saga* (c. 1225) provides one of the earliest
literary descriptions: "His men rushed forward without armor, were as
mad as dogs or wolves, bit their shields, and were strong as bears or
wild oxen." The word entered English in the early 19th century through
Sir Walter Scott and other Romantic-era translators of Norse literature.

By the mid-20th century, "berserk" had fully detached from its Norse
source. Most English speakers today cannot identify the etymology. The
word functions as a pure adjective meaning "wildly out of control,"
with the bear-shirt warrior as invisible as the Latin roots in most
English vocabulary. This makes it a textbook dead metaphor: the vehicle
has been completely absorbed into ordinary language, and the tenor --
divine warrior fury -- has been forgotten.
