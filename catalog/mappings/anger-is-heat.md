## What It Brings

Anger is an increase in body temperature. When you get angry, you get hot.
This is among the most physiologically grounded of all conceptual metaphors
-- anger literally elevates skin temperature, increases blood flow to the
face, and produces the sensation of internal heat. The metaphor does not
merely describe anger in thermal terms; it reflects a genuine
psychophysiological correlation that grounds the mapping in bodily
experience.

Key structural parallels:

- **Anger intensity as temperature** -- mild irritation is "warm" while
  rage is "white-hot." The metaphor provides a continuous scale from cool
  composure to explosive fury, calibrated by degrees. "Getting heated" is
  the transitional zone. "Boiling" is the danger point. The temperature
  scale gives anger a measurable, intuitive gradient.
- **Loss of control as overheating** -- when heat exceeds a threshold,
  physical systems fail. Metal warps. Liquids boil over. Circuits burn out.
  The metaphor maps these failures onto loss of emotional control: you
  "blow up," "explode," or "have a meltdown." The implication is that
  anger, like heat, is manageable below a certain threshold and
  catastrophic above it.
- **Cooling as de-escalation** -- calming down is "cooling off." You tell
  an angry person to "chill out" or to "take a cold shower." Anger
  management is thermal management. The metaphor implies that anger
  dissipates naturally over time if no additional heat is applied, just as
  a hot object returns to ambient temperature.
- **Heat source as provocation** -- the thing that makes you angry is
  what "heats you up." People and events are "inflammatory." Insults are
  "incendiary." The metaphor externalizes the cause of anger as a heat
  source applied to an otherwise temperate person.
- **Containment and explosion** -- in its most developed form (ANGER IS A
  HEATED FLUID IN A CONTAINER, a related complex metaphor), the heat must
  be contained or it erupts. "I could barely contain my anger." "He was
  about to blow." This extension combines ANGER IS HEAT with the container
  image schema.

## Where It Breaks

- **Heat is a physical property; anger is an evaluation** -- the metaphor
  treats anger as a substance that accumulates and dissipates mechanically.
  But anger involves appraisal: you are angry *about* something, and the
  anger can be resolved by addressing the grievance, not just by waiting
  for cooling. The thermal model obscures the cognitive and moral
  dimensions of anger.
- **The explosion model excuses behavior** -- "I just snapped" or "I
  blew up" frames loss of control as a physics problem rather than a
  choice. The metaphor suggests that once heat exceeds a threshold,
  explosion is inevitable -- a framing that has been criticized by anger
  management researchers as enabling violent behavior by making it seem
  like a natural law rather than a failure of regulation.
- **Cultural variation in the heat mapping** -- while ANGER IS HEAT is
  widespread, it is not universal. Kovecses (2000) documents that some
  cultures emphasize different embodied aspects of anger. In some Chinese
  conceptualizations, anger rises as *qi* (gas/energy) rather than heat
  per se. In Zulu, anger is mapped onto the heart swelling rather than
  temperature rising. The metaphor feels universal because the English
  version dominates the literature.
- **Cold anger disappears** -- the metaphor has no good account of
  calculated, deliberate anger -- the kind that is sustained without heat.
  "Cold fury" exists as an expression but it contradicts the dominant
  mapping, making it feel paradoxical rather than descriptive. Strategic,
  patient anger does not fit the thermal model.

## Expressions

- "She was fuming" -- anger as visible heat emission
- "He made my blood boil" -- anger as internal fluid reaching boiling point
- "Don't get so heated" -- emotional escalation as temperature increase
- "A heated argument" -- mutual anger as shared thermal elevation
- "He blew his top" -- anger exceeding containment capacity
- "She has a fiery temper" -- dispositional anger as a persistent heat source
- "I need to cool off" -- de-escalation as thermal dissipation
- "That's an inflammatory remark" -- a provocation as something that
  ignites anger
- "Simmer down" -- reducing anger from boiling to a lower temperature
- "He was burning with rage" -- intense anger as combustion

## Origin Story

ANGER IS HEAT is one of the most extensively studied conceptual metaphors
in the literature. Lakoff and Kovecses (1987) provided the first detailed
analysis in their landmark paper "The Cognitive Model of Anger Inherent in
American English," demonstrating that the metaphor is not a poetic
flourish but the organizing structure of the English anger vocabulary.
The Master Metaphor List (1991) catalogs it alongside the more specific
ANGER IS A HEATED FLUID IN A CONTAINER. Kovecses later expanded the
analysis cross-linguistically in *Metaphor and Emotion* (2000), showing
that while heat is the dominant source domain for anger across many
languages, the specific elaborations vary culturally. The physiological
grounding -- anger genuinely elevates body temperature and skin
conductance -- makes this one of the strongest cases for the embodiment
thesis in conceptual metaphor theory.
