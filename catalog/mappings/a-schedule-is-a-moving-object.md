## What It Brings

The deadline is approaching. The meeting is coming up. The schedule
slipped. This metaphor maps the properties of a moving object onto
a schedule -- a temporal plan with dates, milestones, and deadlines
-- so that the schedule moves through time toward or away from the
stationary observer. It is an instance of the broader TIME IS A MOVING
OBJECT system, specialized to the domain of planned temporal sequences.

Key structural parallels:

- **The schedule approaches** -- "The deadline is approaching." "The
  launch date is coming up fast." "Exams are bearing down on us." The
  schedule, and especially its critical dates, are objects moving toward
  the observer along a temporal path. The observer stands still; the
  schedule comes to them. This creates a felt sense of inevitability:
  the deadline will arrive whether you are ready or not.
- **The schedule has speed** -- "The deadline is racing toward us."
  "Time is flying." "The project timeline is accelerating." Moving
  objects have velocity, and so do schedules. A fast-approaching deadline
  feels different from a distant one, and the metaphor maps this
  difference onto the speed of an incoming object. Urgency is velocity.
- **The schedule can slip** -- "The schedule slipped by two weeks." "The
  timeline has drifted." "The release date keeps sliding." If a schedule
  is a moving object, it can lose traction, change speed, or shift
  position. Slippage is the schedule-object failing to maintain its
  expected trajectory -- it moves away from the observer instead of
  toward them, or it moves laterally off its expected path.
- **The schedule can be pushed or pulled** -- "We pushed the meeting
  back." "They moved the deadline up." "The launch was pulled forward."
  Because the schedule is an object, agents can apply force to it. Moving
  a date "forward" or "back" treats the schedule as something with a
  spatial position that can be physically relocated.
- **The schedule arrives** -- "The day has arrived." "D-day is here."
  "The moment has come." The culmination of the schedule's movement is
  its arrival at the observer's position. At that point, the planned
  event occurs. The metaphor gives temporal events a dramatic structure:
  they were distant, they approached, and now they are here.

## Where It Breaks

- **Schedules are human constructions, not natural objects** -- a moving
  object follows physical laws; a schedule follows human decisions. The
  metaphor makes schedules feel like they have their own momentum and
  trajectory independent of human choice. "The deadline is approaching"
  obscures the fact that someone set the deadline and someone could
  change it. The moving-object frame makes arbitrary dates feel like
  forces of nature.
- **The observer is not really stationary** -- the metaphor places the
  person as a fixed point while the schedule moves toward them. But
  people are also moving through time, doing work, making progress. The
  metaphor's one-directional frame (schedule approaches, person waits)
  hides the active, agentic side of working toward a deadline. It
  makes people feel passive in the face of approaching time.
- **"Slipping" implies a failure of the schedule, not of the planner**
  -- when a schedule "slips," the metaphor places the blame on the
  object (the schedule lost traction) rather than on the people who
  failed to execute. This is a subtle but consequential framing: it
  externalizes responsibility for missed deadlines by treating the
  schedule as an agent that misbehaved.
- **Forward and backward are ambiguous** -- "pushing the meeting back"
  means delaying it, but "moving the meeting forward" is genuinely
  ambiguous (does forward mean sooner or later?). The spatial logic of
  moving objects does not map cleanly onto the temporal axis because
  the direction of the schedule's motion conflicts with the direction
  of our own temporal orientation. This ambiguity is well-documented
  in the psycholinguistic literature (Boroditsky, McGlone & Harding).
- **The metaphor cannot represent parallel schedules** -- a moving object
  follows one trajectory. But real scheduling involves multiple
  overlapping timelines, dependencies, and concurrent tracks. The
  single-object model handles one deadline well but fails to represent
  the complexity of multi-stream project management.

## Expressions

- "The deadline is approaching" -- a future date as an object moving
  toward the observer
- "The schedule slipped" -- delay as loss of traction by a moving object
- "We pushed the meeting back" -- delaying as applying force to move
  an object away
- "The launch date is coming up fast" -- urgency as high velocity of
  approach
- "The day has finally arrived" -- a scheduled event reaching the
  observer's position
- "We're running up against the deadline" -- approaching collision with
  an oncoming object
- "The timeline is drifting" -- gradual schedule delay as lateral drift
- "Move the meeting forward" -- rescheduling as repositioning an object
  (ambiguous direction)
- "The project is behind schedule" -- the schedule has passed and the
  work has not kept up
- "Time crept up on us" -- a deadline arriving without adequate warning

## Origin Story

The Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson, and Schwartz 1991) catalogs
A SCHEDULE IS A MOVING OBJECT as a specific instance of the TIME IS A
MOVING OBJECT system. In this system, future events are objects
approaching along a temporal path, and the experiencer is a stationary
observer facing the future. This contrasts with the complementary
TIME IS A LANDSCAPE system, where the experiencer moves forward through
a stationary temporal landscape (as in "we're approaching the deadline"
-- here the deadline is fixed and we move toward it).

The schedule variant is particularly interesting because it blends
temporal metaphor with the pragmatics of planning and coordination.
Schedules are not natural temporal phenomena; they are social
artifacts. Yet the moving-object metaphor naturalizes them, making
deadlines feel like approaching weather rather than human decisions.
This naturalization has real consequences in organizational life:
"the schedule slipped" is a far more common expression than "we
failed to meet the schedule," and the difference in agency attribution
matters.

Boroditsky (2000) and McGlone and Harding (1998) have shown that the
ambiguity of "moving forward" in temporal expressions is a direct
consequence of the competition between the moving-time and
moving-observer frames. When someone says "the meeting has been moved
forward two days," roughly half of English speakers interpret this as
earlier and half as later, depending on which metaphorical frame they
are currently activating.
