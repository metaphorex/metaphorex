## What It Brings

When you act, you move yourself. This metaphor refines ACTION IS MOTION by
specifying that the agent is the source of the motion -- action is not just
movement, but self-generated movement. The distinction matters: in ACTION IS
MOTION, the mover could be pushed, carried, or pulled. In ACTION IS
SELF-PROPELLED MOTION, the agent walks, runs, or drives under their own
power. This foregrounds autonomy and intention. The acting person is not a
projectile but a walker who chooses direction and pace.

Key structural parallels:

- **Agency as locomotion** -- "She's going places." "He's making his own
  way." "They're moving under their own steam." The agent does not merely
  move; they propel themselves. This mapping is the conceptual foundation
  for autonomy in the Event Structure system -- what separates acting from
  being acted upon.
- **Intention as direction** -- "She's heading toward a promotion." "He
  set out to change things." "They're on a path of their own choosing."
  Because the motion is self-propelled, the direction is chosen. This
  makes intention a matter of orientation: purposeful action points
  somewhere; aimless action wanders.
- **Effort as exertion** -- "She's pushing herself." "He's running himself
  ragged." "They're trudging through the work." Self-propelled motion
  requires energy from the mover. The metaphor maps the effort of action
  onto the physical strain of walking, running, or climbing. Hard work is
  uphill; easy work is downhill or coasting.
- **Pace as rate of action** -- "She's sprinting toward the deadline."
  "He's taking it one step at a time." "They're crawling along." The
  agent controls their speed, which maps onto the tempo of activity. Fast
  walkers are productive; slow walkers are cautious or lazy.
- **Stopping as choosing to rest** -- "She's taking a breather." "He
  paused to regroup." "They decided to stop and reassess." Because the
  motion is self-propelled, stopping is a choice, not a failure. This
  distinguishes ACTION IS SELF-PROPELLED MOTION from simple ACTION IS
  MOTION, where stopping tends to read as breakdown.
- **Obstacles as terrain** -- "She navigated the bureaucracy." "He found
  a way around the problem." "They're in uncharted territory." The
  self-propelled agent encounters landscape features and must route
  around them, climb over them, or find paths through them. This maps
  problem-solving onto wayfinding.

## Where It Breaks

- **Not all action is self-generated** -- the metaphor assumes the agent
  is the source of motion, but much human action is responsive, coerced,
  or structurally constrained. A worker on an assembly line is not
  "going places" under their own power; they are carried along by an
  institutional process. The metaphor makes it hard to talk about action
  that is real but not autonomous.
- **The individual walker model obscures collective action** -- self-
  propelled motion is paradigmatically individual. One person walks. When
  groups act, the metaphor must awkwardly extend to marching in formation,
  traveling together, or moving as a unit. The rich dynamics of collective
  action -- negotiation, dissent, emergence -- are lost.
- **The metaphor romanticizes autonomy** -- by mapping action onto
  self-propelled motion, the metaphor makes independent, self-directed
  activity the prototype and all other forms of action deficient by
  comparison. Being "carried along" or "swept up" in events sounds passive
  even when it describes legitimate participation in social movements,
  traditions, or institutions.
- **Direction implies a single path** -- the self-propelled walker moves
  in one direction at a time. But real action often involves maintaining
  multiple commitments simultaneously, oscillating between priorities, or
  deliberately keeping options open. The metaphor makes multitasking feel
  like going in circles.
- **Physical exertion is not a reliable model for cognitive effort** --
  the metaphor maps mental work onto bodily strain, which gives thinking
  a cost structure (fatigue, exhaustion, "running on empty"). But
  cognitive effort does not deplete in the same way as muscular effort,
  and the fatigue framing can make intellectual work feel more draining
  than it actually is.

## Expressions

- "She's going places" -- ambitious action as self-directed movement
  toward a destination
- "He's making his own way" -- independent action as self-propelled
  travel
- "They're moving under their own steam" -- autonomous action as
  self-powered locomotion
- "She set out to change things" -- initiating action as beginning a
  self-directed journey
- "He's pushing himself hard" -- effortful action as strenuous
  self-propulsion
- "She navigated the red tape" -- problem-solving as wayfinding through
  difficult terrain
- "He's running himself ragged" -- overexertion as self-propelled motion
  to the point of wear
- "They're forging ahead" -- determined action as driving forward through
  resistance
- "She took a step back" -- pausing for perspective as reversing
  self-propelled motion
- "He's finding his own way" -- developing personal approach as choosing
  a self-directed path

## Origin Story

ACTION IS SELF-PROPELLED MOTION is a core mapping in Lakoff and Johnson's
Event Structure metaphor system, documented in the Master Metaphor List
(1991) and elaborated in *Philosophy in the Flesh* (1999). It specializes
the more general ACTION IS MOTION by adding the crucial element of
self-generated force. In the Event Structure system, this mapping does
essential work: it distinguishes actions (self-propelled) from events that
happen to you (externally propelled). A person who acts is a walker; a
person who is affected is a moved object. This distinction maps the
philosophical concept of agency onto the embodied experience of the
difference between walking and being pushed -- a distinction available
to any mobile creature, making it one of the most deeply grounded
metaphors in the system.
