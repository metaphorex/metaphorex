## What It Brings

In Hindu and Buddhist philosophy, karma is the principle that actions
produce consequences that shape future existence -- across lifetimes, not
just within one. Good actions generate good karma; harmful actions generate
bad karma. The mechanism is not punishment or reward by a deity but a
natural law of moral causation, as impersonal as gravity. The structural
insight: the universe has a ledger, and it balances.

- **Moral accounting** -- karma imports the logic of double-entry
  bookkeeping into ethics. Every action creates a debit or a credit.
  The books must balance, even if settlement is deferred. In colloquial
  English, "karma" functions as shorthand for cosmic justice: the
  confidence that cheaters will eventually be caught, that generosity
  will eventually be repaid, that the moral universe bends toward
  equilibrium.
- **Delayed consequences** -- karma does not require immediate payback.
  The original doctrine spans multiple lifetimes; the colloquial version
  spans at least a narrative arc. "Karma will get them" expresses faith
  that bad behavior incurs a debt that must eventually be paid, even if
  the when is unknown. This temporal gap is the metaphor's distinctive
  contribution -- it provides patience in the face of apparent injustice.
- **Impersonal mechanism** -- karma is not administered by a judge. There
  is no trial, no appeal, no sentencing. Consequences arise from the
  action itself, the way burns arise from touching fire. This makes the
  metaphor structurally different from divine judgment: it is a law of
  nature, not a law of courts. In everyday use, "that's karma" implies
  that no agent of justice was necessary -- the universe handled it.

## Where It Breaks

- **The universe does not keep a ledger** -- the deepest problem with
  colloquial karma is that it asserts a causal mechanism that does not
  exist in the physical world. Bad things happen to good people; corrupt
  individuals prosper and die peacefully. The metaphor survives because
  humans are prone to just-world reasoning and because examples of
  apparent karmic justice are memorable while counterexamples are
  explained away as "karma hasn't caught up yet."
- **The original doctrine is not about this lifetime** -- Hindu and
  Buddhist karma operates across cycles of rebirth. An action in one
  life produces consequences in a future life. The Western colloquial
  version compresses this into a single lifetime or even a single
  interaction ("instant karma"), which strips away the cosmological
  framework that makes the original concept coherent. The colloquial
  version wants cosmic justice on a human timescale.
- **It can blame the victim** -- if karma is a universal law, then
  suffering must be the consequence of prior bad action. This logic,
  taken seriously, implies that people who suffer deserve their suffering.
  Within the original philosophical framework, this implication is
  acknowledged and debated extensively. In casual Western usage, the
  victim-blaming potential is rarely confronted. "That's karma" said
  about someone else's misfortune is a moral judgment disguised as a
  metaphysical observation.
- **Moral categories are not fungible** -- karma-as-ledger implies that
  good actions in one domain can offset bad actions in another, like
  carbon credits for the soul. But moral life does not work this way.
  Being generous to friends does not offset being cruel to strangers.
  The accounting metaphor smuggles in a fungibility that ethics resists.
- **It discourages intervention** -- if karma will handle it, why should
  I? The metaphor can produce passivity in the face of injustice, since
  acting against wrongdoing becomes unnecessary if the universe will
  settle accounts on its own. This is the political cost of cosmic
  justice: it can drain the urgency from human justice.

## Expressions

- "That's karma" -- the standard colloquial usage, always retrospective,
  applied when someone who behaved badly experiences misfortune
- "Karma's a bitch" -- intensified version, implying that karmic payback
  is disproportionate or particularly fitting
- "Instant karma" -- John Lennon's coinage, now standard for immediate
  consequences of bad behavior, stripping the original doctrine of its
  temporal depth
- "Good karma" -- accumulated moral credit, used in contexts from
  workplace culture ("you'll build good karma with the team") to internet
  forums (Reddit's "karma" points)
- "Karmic debt" -- obligations arising from past behavior, sometimes used
  in business contexts for technical debt accrued through ethical shortcuts
- "What goes around comes around" -- the folk proverb that functions as
  karma's English-language equivalent

## Origin Story

The Sanskrit word *karma* means simply "action" or "deed." Its
philosophical development begins in the Upanishads (c. 800-200 BCE),
where it becomes linked to the doctrine of samsara (the cycle of rebirth).
The Bhagavad Gita elaborates karma into a sophisticated ethical framework
distinguishing between action, attachment to results, and the path of
selfless duty (karma yoga).

Buddhism adopted and modified the concept, emphasizing intention
(*cetana*) as the determinant of karmic weight. Jainism developed its
own karmic theory, treating karma as a physical substance that adheres
to the soul.

The word entered English in the early 19th century through Orientalist
scholarship but remained specialized until the counterculture of the
1960s brought Hindu and Buddhist concepts into popular Western vocabulary.
By the 2000s, "karma" was fully dead as a metaphor in colloquial English.
Reddit named its reputation system "karma" in 2005 without needing to
explain the reference. The word now means "deserved consequences" with no
active connection to rebirth, dharma, or the philosophical traditions
that produced it.
