## What It Brings

When we evaluate alternatives -- ideas, partners, careers, explanations --
we *shop around*. We *browse* options. We look for the *best deal*. We
are *in the market for* a new approach. The metaphor takes the structured
activity of retail shopping -- entering a marketplace, comparing goods,
evaluating value, making a selection -- and maps it onto any process of
searching among alternatives and choosing one.

Key structural parallels:

- **Alternatives as merchandise** -- the options under consideration are
  goods displayed for selection. You *look at what's out there*, *see
  what's available*, and *check out* the possibilities. Each alternative
  is an item on a shelf, available for inspection and comparison.
- **Seeking as browsing** -- the process of searching is structured as
  moving through a store or marketplace. You *shop around*, *look
  around*, and *window-shop*. The metaphor gives searching a spatial
  structure: alternatives are distributed in a space that you move
  through.
- **Evaluating as pricing** -- comparing alternatives means assessing
  their relative value. You ask "is it *worth* it?" and consider the
  *cost* of each option. The shopping frame imports a value calculus:
  every alternative has a price, and the rational seeker maximizes value.
- **Choosing as buying** -- the moment of decision is a purchase. You
  *buy into* an idea, *invest in* a relationship, or decide you are
  *not buying* an argument. The metaphor makes commitment transactional:
  you exchange something (time, effort, opportunity cost) for your
  choice.
- **Satisfaction as getting a good deal** -- a successful search ends
  with the feeling that you got something valuable for what you paid.
  A disappointing choice feels like being *ripped off* or *sold short*.

## Where It Breaks

- **Not all seeking has a marketplace** -- the shopping metaphor assumes
  a pre-existing array of options laid out for comparison. But many
  important searches (scientific discovery, artistic creation, existential
  meaning) require creating the alternatives, not just selecting among
  them. You cannot shop for a scientific breakthrough because the
  merchandise does not yet exist.
- **The metaphor commodifies the unchosen** -- in shopping, what you
  don't buy remains on the shelf for someone else. But in life decisions,
  unchosen paths disappear. You cannot go back to the store and pick the
  other career. The shopping frame makes decisions feel more reversible
  than they are.
- **Value is not always commensurable** -- shopping assumes a common
  currency for comparison (price). But comparing a career in medicine
  against one in music requires comparing incommensurable values. The
  shopping metaphor forces a false commensurability, as if everything
  could be reduced to a single scale of worth.
- **The metaphor makes the seeker a consumer** -- the person seeking
  becomes a customer, which implies passivity. Customers choose among
  what is offered; they do not make what they need. The shopping frame
  discourages the possibility that the right answer might be to build
  something new rather than select from existing options.
- **Comparison paralysis maps onto buyer's anxiety** -- the metaphor
  imports decision fatigue from consumer culture. "Too many choices"
  becomes a problem because the shopping frame treats every option as
  requiring evaluation. In practice, many good decisions are made by
  satisficing, not by exhaustive comparison shopping.

## Expressions

- "I'm shopping around for a new doctor" -- seeking professional
  services as retail browsing
- "She's in the market for a new job" -- career seeking as marketplace
  participation
- "Let me see what's out there" -- exploring alternatives as browsing
  merchandise
- "I'm not buying that argument" -- intellectual rejection as refusing
  to purchase
- "He bought into the idea completely" -- belief adoption as completing
  a transaction
- "That theory is a tough sell" -- persuasion as salesmanship
- "Comparison shopping for colleges" -- educational evaluation as
  consumer comparison
- "What are my options?" -- alternatives as available merchandise
- "She sold me on the plan" -- persuasion as closing a sale
- "You get what you pay for" -- quality of outcome proportional to
  investment, as in retail

## Origin Story

The Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson & Schwartz 1991) catalogs
COMPARING AND SEEKING IS SHOPPING as part of the broader family of
economic metaphors in English. The metaphor reflects the deep penetration
of market logic into everyday reasoning in capitalist cultures: the
marketplace provides a ready-made structure for any situation involving
choice among alternatives. The metaphor is culturally specific in ways
that many conceptual metaphors are not -- it presupposes a consumer
economy with abundant choice, which is historically recent and not
universal.
