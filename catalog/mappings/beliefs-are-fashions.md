## What It Brings

Beliefs come into style and go out of style. They are adopted because
they are trendy and abandoned when they become passe. This metaphor
maps the dynamics of fashion -- trend cycles, social imitation,
obsolescence, and revival -- onto the domain of epistemic commitment,
making the history of what people believe look like a succession of
styles rather than a reasoned response to evidence. The Master Metaphor
List (Lakoff, Espenson & Schwartz 1991) catalogs it alongside other
belief metaphors that give convictions different ontological statuses.

Key structural parallels:

- **Currency and trendiness** -- "That belief is very fashionable right
  now." "Atheism is in vogue in certain circles." "Believing in progress
  is so last century." The metaphor gives beliefs a temporal profile
  defined by social adoption rather than by truth. A belief is current
  the way a style is current: because enough people hold it at this
  moment.
- **Trendsetters and followers** -- "She was among the first to adopt
  that position." "He just follows whatever the intellectual crowd
  believes." "They're always behind the times." The fashion metaphor
  creates a social hierarchy of belief adoption: some people lead,
  most follow, and some are left behind holding outdated convictions
  like outdated clothes.
- **Obsolescence without refutation** -- "That worldview is dated."
  "Nobody believes that anymore." "Those ideas are old-fashioned."
  Beliefs do not need to be disproven to be abandoned; they just need
  to look old. The fashion metaphor provides a mechanism for belief
  change that has nothing to do with evidence or argument -- beliefs
  are dropped because they feel stale.
- **Fads and crazes** -- "It's just the latest intellectual craze."
  "Mindfulness is a belief fad." The metaphor can trivialize beliefs
  by associating them with the superficiality of fashion trends --
  something adopted for its novelty value rather than its truth.
- **Revival and comeback** -- "There's been a revival of belief in
  traditional values." "Stoicism is making a comeback." Just as
  fashion cycles bring back old styles, the metaphor accommodates
  belief revivals without requiring new evidence. Something can be
  believable again simply because enough time has passed since it
  was last fashionable.

## Where It Breaks

- **Fashion is genuinely arbitrary; belief is not entirely so** -- the
  fashion metaphor implies that belief change is driven by taste,
  boredom, and social dynamics rather than by evidence, argument, or
  experience. While there is real truth in the sociology-of-knowledge
  critique (beliefs do gain and lose adherents for social reasons),
  the metaphor overstates arbitrariness. Some beliefs are abandoned
  because they are false, not because they are unfashionable.
- **The metaphor erases the role of evidence** -- in fashion, there
  is no external criterion that makes one style better than another.
  The metaphor imports this relativism into the domain of belief,
  making it seem as though all belief change is mere social fashion.
  This makes it difficult to distinguish between beliefs abandoned
  for good epistemic reasons and beliefs abandoned because of social
  pressure.
- **Fashion has no cumulation** -- this season's style does not build
  on last season's. The fashion metaphor cannot represent epistemic
  progress -- only epistemic succession. If beliefs are fashions,
  then we do not know more or better than we used to; we simply
  believe differently.
- **The metaphor is always pejorative** -- calling a belief
  "fashionable" is almost never a compliment. The metaphor is used
  primarily as a dismissal: to say a belief is fashionable is to say
  it is held for the wrong reasons. This built-in rhetorical bias
  limits the metaphor's usefulness as an analytical tool, since it
  always implies the speaker's contempt for the belief under
  discussion.
- **Adoption is not imitation** -- fashion spreads through imitation
  and social signaling. But beliefs also spread because they explain
  things, solve problems, or provide comfort. The fashion metaphor
  treats all belief adoption as following a trend, erasing the
  functional and epistemic reasons people have for holding the
  convictions they hold.

## Expressions

- "That belief is very fashionable right now" -- widespread conviction
  as current style
- "Nobody believes that anymore -- it's outdated" -- abandoned belief
  as last season's fashion
- "He just follows whatever everyone else thinks" -- belief adoption
  as trend-following
- "That worldview is so last century" -- an older belief system as a
  dated style
- "There's been a revival of belief in traditional values" -- returning
  to older beliefs as a fashion comeback
- "It's just the latest intellectual craze" -- dismissing a belief as
  a passing fad
- "She was among the first to take that position" -- early belief
  adoption as fashion leadership
- "Those views are old-fashioned" -- conservative beliefs as outdated
  clothing
- "Believing in progress is out of style" -- declining belief as
  declining fashionability

## Origin Story

The Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson & Schwartz 1991) records
BELIEFS ARE FASHIONS as one of a cluster of belief metaphors alongside
BELIEFS ARE POSSESSIONS, BELIEFS ARE LOCATIONS, BELIEFS ARE GUIDES,
BELIEFS ARE LOVE OBJECTS, and BELIEFS ARE BEINGS WITH A LIFE CYCLE.
The cluster is structurally parallel to the better-known ideas cluster
in Chapter 10 of *Metaphors We Live By* (1980), where IDEAS ARE
FASHIONS appears with the same mapping logic but applied to ideas
rather than to the epistemic commitment of believing.

The distinction matters: the ideas cluster is about intellectual
products (theories, concepts, hypotheses), while the beliefs cluster is
about the act of holding something to be true. BELIEFS ARE FASHIONS
emphasizes the social dynamics of conviction -- why people believe what
they believe -- rather than the content of what is believed.
