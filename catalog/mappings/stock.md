## What It Brings

Old English stocc -- a tree trunk or block of wood. Medieval debt
recording used split tally sticks: a piece of wood was notched with the
amount owed, then split lengthwise. The creditor kept the larger piece,
called "the stock." The debtor kept the shorter piece, called "the stub"
(or "foil"). Stock certificates, stockholders, stock markets, and stock
exchanges all trace to recording debts on sticks of wood.

- **Ownership as a physical token** -- the tally stick made an abstract
  relationship (I owe you) into a physical object you could hold, store,
  and transfer. The stock was proof of credit -- literally a piece of
  wood that entitled you to repayment. The metaphor imports the idea
  that ownership must be materialized in a transferable token. Modern
  stock certificates, share registries, and digital ledgers all
  recapitulate this structure: an abstract claim made concrete through
  a record that can change hands.
- **The split creates accountability** -- the genius of the tally stick
  was that both halves had to match. The notches on the stock had to
  align with the notches on the stub when held together. Fraud required
  altering two pieces of wood held by two different people. The metaphor
  imports this bilateral verification into financial instruments: a
  stock is not just a claim but a claim with a counterparty, and the
  system works because both sides hold matching records.
- **Stored value, ready for use** -- "stock" also meant a store of goods
  kept for future use (livestock, stockpile, stock of provisions). The
  tree-trunk origin carries the sense of something solid and substantial
  that you keep in reserve. "Taking stock" means assessing what you
  have stored. The financial meaning layers on top: stock is stored
  wealth, ready to be deployed. The wood-trunk metaphor makes financial
  instruments feel solid and grounded even when they represent pure
  abstraction.

## Where It Breaks

- **The material disappeared entirely** -- the tally stick was a
  physical anti-fraud device. Its value lay in its materiality: you
  could not forge a split that matched. Modern stock is entirely
  dematerialized -- electronic entries in databases with no physical
  counterpart. The metaphor died so completely that "stock" now means
  the opposite of what it originally was: not a tangible piece of wood
  but an intangible entry in a ledger. The word retained "ownership
  claim" but lost "physical object," which was the entire point of the
  original technology.
- **The bilateral verification is gone** -- tally sticks worked because
  both halves had to match. Modern stock trading involves chains of
  intermediaries (brokers, clearinghouses, depositories) that make
  bilateral verification impossible. The 2021 GameStop episode revealed
  that the modern stock system sometimes cannot determine who actually
  owns what -- a problem the tally stick solved by definition. The dead
  metaphor preserves the word "stock" while abandoning the structural
  feature that made it trustworthy.
- **"Stub" survives as a diminutive** -- the debtor's half of the tally
  was smaller and less important: the "stub." Today, "ticket stub,"
  "pay stub," and "stub" in programming (a placeholder) all carry the
  sense of an incomplete remainder -- the less valuable half of
  something that was split. The tally-stick metaphor system is
  remarkably well preserved: "stock" means the valuable part and "stub"
  means the leftover, even though nobody remembers why.
- **The London fire burned the metaphor's origin** -- in 1834, the
  British Parliament decided to burn its accumulated tally sticks (which
  had been used for tax collection until 1826). The fire in the furnaces
  of the House of Lords got out of control and burned down the Palace of
  Westminster. The physical origin of the stock market was literally
  destroyed in a fire caused by disposing of obsolete tally sticks.
  Charles Dickens called it "the most absurd instance of official
  routine." The destruction of the original sticks is a fitting coda
  to the death of the metaphor.

## Expressions

- "Stock market" -- the marketplace for trading ownership claims,
  where the tally stick became a financial instrument
- "Stockholder" -- one who holds the creditor's half of the tally,
  now meaning any owner of corporate shares
- "Stock exchange" -- a venue for trading ownership tokens, where
  the wooden sticks became electronic records
- "Stock option" -- the right to buy shares at a set price, where
  the tally-stick origin is buried under layers of financial
  abstraction
- "Taking stock" -- assessing what you have in store, preserving the
  inventory meaning rather than the financial one
- "Laughingstock" -- an object of ridicule, from a different sense of
  "stock" (a post to which offenders were fastened), showing how the
  tree-trunk root branched into multiple dead metaphors
- "Lock, stock, and barrel" -- the complete firearm (lock mechanism,
  wooden stock, barrel), where "stock" retains its wood-block meaning

## Origin Story

Old English stocc meant "tree trunk, block of wood, post." The word
is cognate with German Stock (stick, cane) and shares a root with
"stick" and "stake." The tally-stick usage emerged in medieval England
as a bookkeeping technology: wooden sticks notched with amounts and
split to create matching records for creditor and debtor.

The Exchequer (itself a dead metaphor -- from the checkered cloth used
for counting) adopted tally sticks as the official method of tax
collection. Taxpayers received the stub as a receipt; the Crown kept the
stock as proof of payment. These Exchequer tallies were transferable:
a creditor could sell their stock to a third party, creating a secondary
market in government debt recorded on sticks of wood.

The "stock" in the London Stock Exchange (founded 1801, though informal
trading began in 1698 at Jonathan's Coffee House) refers directly to
these government debt tallies. "Government stock" meant government debt
instruments, and the place where they were traded became the "stock
exchange." The transition from physical sticks to paper certificates
to electronic records happened gradually, but the word never changed.

The tally-stick system was not formally abolished until 1826, and the
accumulated sticks were stored in the Houses of Parliament until the
fateful 1834 bonfire. The fire that destroyed the Palace of Westminster
-- and prompted the construction of the current Houses of Parliament,
including the iconic Elizabeth Tower (Big Ben) -- was caused by
burning the physical objects that gave the stock market its name.
