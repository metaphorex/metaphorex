## What It Brings

On November 18, 1978, over 900 members of the Peoples Temple died in
Jonestown, Guyana, after drinking cyanide-laced Flavor Aid (popularly
misremembered as Kool-Aid) at the direction of cult leader Jim Jones.
The metaphor maps the act of consuming a lethal drink on the orders of a
charismatic authority onto the uncritical adoption of a technology,
methodology, or corporate culture. It is the darkest origin story of any
tech metaphor, and that darkness is precisely what gives it rhetorical
force.

Key structural parallels:

- **Authority-driven consumption** -- in Jonestown, people drank because
  Jones told them to. In tech, engineers adopt frameworks, languages,
  and methodologies because leadership mandates them or because a
  charismatic figure (a CTO, a conference speaker, a thought leader)
  evangelizes them. The metaphor maps the cult leader's authority onto
  the authority of whoever is promoting the technology, and the
  follower's compliance onto the engineer's adoption.
- **Collective reinforcement** -- Jonestown worked because everyone
  drank together. Doubt is harder to sustain when everyone around you
  is committed. In tech, the same dynamic operates: if the whole team
  uses Kubernetes, questioning Kubernetes feels like heresy. The
  metaphor maps social pressure within a closed community onto
  conformity within an engineering organization.
- **Irreversibility as commitment** -- drinking poison is irreversible.
  Adopting a technology is not literally irreversible, but migration costs
  can make it feel that way. Once you've rewritten everything in the new
  framework, switching again is enormously expensive. The metaphor maps
  physical irreversibility onto economic lock-in, and the emotional
  weight of the original makes the engineering situation feel more
  consequential.
- **The insider/outsider divide** -- cult members see themselves as
  enlightened; outsiders see them as deluded. Engineers who have "drunk
  the Kool-Aid" on a technology see themselves as informed advocates;
  skeptics see them as uncritical followers. The metaphor maps the
  epistemic asymmetry of cult membership onto the epistemic asymmetry
  of technology adoption: the question of who is seeing clearly is
  precisely what is in dispute.

## Where It Breaks

- **The metaphor equates enthusiasm with delusion** -- calling someone a
  Kool-Aid drinker implies their belief is irrational. But sometimes the
  technology is genuinely good. Early advocates of Git, React, or
  containerization were called Kool-Aid drinkers by skeptics who turned
  out to be wrong. The metaphor provides no way to distinguish warranted
  enthusiasm from cult-like devotion, which makes it a weapon against
  any strong conviction.
- **Jonestown was not voluntary for everyone** -- many Jonestown victims
  were coerced, including children who had no choice. The metaphor
  sanitizes this by implying that "drinking the Kool-Aid" is a voluntary
  act of gullibility. In doing so, it subtly blames victims of
  authoritarian systems for their own victimization. When applied to
  tech, this maps onto engineers who adopt mandated technologies not
  because they are credulous but because they have no organizational
  power to resist.
- **The death metaphor is wildly disproportionate** -- comparing the
  adoption of a JavaScript framework to a mass death event is, on its
  face, grotesque. The metaphor works because it is hyperbolic, but the
  hyperbole can trivialize the original event. Each casual use of
  "drinking the Kool-Aid" in a stand-up meeting makes the Jonestown
  massacre slightly more abstract, slightly more available as a
  punchline.
- **It was Flavor Aid** -- the drink at Jonestown was actually Flavor
  Aid, a cheaper competitor to Kool-Aid. The misattribution is itself
  metaphorically interesting: the brand that stuck in cultural memory
  is the more famous one, not the accurate one. Kraft (which owns
  Kool-Aid) has never been thrilled about the association. The factual
  error embedded in the metaphor is a small lesson in how metaphors
  reshape the facts they claim to reference.

## Expressions

- "They drank the Kool-Aid" -- the past tense, describing a team or
  individual who has already committed uncritically to a technology
- "Don't drink the Kool-Aid" -- the warning, cautioning against
  uncritical adoption, usually directed at someone excited about a
  new framework or methodology
- "He's been drinking the Kool-Aid" -- the progressive form, implying
  ongoing consumption and deepening commitment
- "The Kool-Aid is strong at that company" -- describing a corporate
  culture of uncritical internal promotion, mapping the drink's potency
  onto the culture's persuasive power
- "I drank the Kool-Aid and I'm not sorry" -- the ironic self-aware
  version, acknowledging the metaphor while asserting that the
  enthusiasm is justified

## Origin Story

The Jonestown massacre of November 18, 1978, killed 918 people,
including 304 children, making it the largest mass death of American
civilians by deliberate act until September 11, 2001. The phrase
"drinking the Kool-Aid" entered American English almost immediately, but
its meaning shifted over time. In the 1980s, it carried the horror of
the original event. By the 2000s, it had softened into a generic
metaphor for uncritical adoption, losing most of its original weight.

In tech culture, the phrase became common in the 2000s and 2010s during
the framework wars (Ruby on Rails vs. Django, Angular vs. React, monolith
vs. microservices). It served as a rhetorical weapon for skeptics: if
you couldn't argue on technical merits, you could dismiss your opponent's
position as cult-like devotion. The metaphor's power comes from its
darkness, but its casual deployment suggests that most speakers have
lost contact with that darkness.
