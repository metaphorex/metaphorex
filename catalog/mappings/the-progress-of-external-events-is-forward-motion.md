## What It Brings

When events in the world unfold as expected -- when negotiations advance,
when seasons turn, when a project moves from planning to completion --
we describe them as moving forward. THE PROGRESS OF EXTERNAL EVENTS IS
FORWARD MOTION is a component of the Event Structure metaphorical system
that specifically maps the unfolding of events outside the self onto the
forward movement of an entity along a path. Unlike the closely related
ACTION IS MOTION (where a volitional agent moves), this metaphor applies
to events that happen around us rather than things we do: the economy
recovers, winter approaches, a trial proceeds, technology advances.

Key structural parallels:

- **Events as moving entities** -- "The deadline is approaching." "Summer
  is coming." "The election is still a long way off." Events themselves
  are conceptualized as things that move toward us or past us along a
  temporal path. Progress in the world is literal forward motion through
  space.
- **Rate of progress as speed** -- "The talks are moving slowly."
  "Technology is racing ahead." "The recovery has stalled." How fast
  events unfold is how fast the moving entity travels. Rapid progress
  is speed; arrested progress is stopping or stalling.
- **Normal progress as unimpeded motion** -- "Things are moving along."
  "Events are unfolding." "The process is on track." When external
  events proceed as expected, the motion is smooth and forward. The
  metaphor treats the expected course of events as a default trajectory
  that only needs explanation when something interrupts it.
- **Regression as backward motion** -- "The peace process has gone
  backward." "The economy is sliding back." "We've lost ground." When
  events reverse direction or return to a prior state, this is
  understood as motion in the wrong direction -- away from the implied
  destination of normal progress.
- **Stagnation as lack of motion** -- "The investigation has stalled."
  "Nothing is moving on the housing front." "The situation is at a
  standstill." When events fail to progress, the moving entity has
  stopped. The metaphor makes stasis inherently negative: it is the
  absence of the forward motion that events ought to have.

## Where It Breaks

- **Not all change is progress** -- the metaphor treats forward motion
  as the default direction of events, implying that temporal succession
  is advancement. But many sequences of events are not progressive:
  ecological degradation, institutional decay, and the slow erosion of
  social trust all involve change without forward motion. The metaphor
  makes it difficult to describe a situation that is changing but not
  going anywhere.
- **Forward is not always good** -- the metaphor inherits the
  orientational bias of the GOOD IS FORWARD mapping. But events can
  "advance" toward catastrophe. A war can "progress" toward greater
  destruction. The metaphor has no built-in distinction between forward
  motion toward a desirable outcome and forward motion toward disaster,
  because it conflates temporal unfolding with spatial advancement.
- **External events do not have inherent direction** -- the metaphor
  imposes a path on events that may have no natural trajectory. A
  political crisis does not have a forward direction; it has multiple
  possible resolutions, none of which is more "forward" than the others.
  The metaphor picks one expected outcome and calls motion toward it
  "progress," concealing the fact that this is a choice, not a feature
  of the events themselves.
- **The metaphor obscures the observer's role** -- by treating events
  as entities that move on their own, the metaphor hides the fact that
  "progress" is always relative to someone's expectations and values.
  The economy "moving forward" means different things to different
  people. The metaphor presents one perspective's preferred direction
  as the objective direction of events.
- **Cyclical events break the model** -- seasons, economic cycles,
  political pendulums, and ecological succession are inherently
  cyclical. The forward-motion metaphor cannot comfortably represent
  events that return to their starting point as part of their normal
  behavior, because return is backward motion and backward motion is
  regression.

## Expressions

- "The negotiations are moving forward" -- diplomatic progress as
  forward motion (Master Metaphor List, Lakoff, Espenson & Schwartz,
  1991)
- "Technology is advancing rapidly" -- technological change as fast
  forward motion (common usage; documented in Kovecses, *Metaphor:
  A Practical Introduction*, 2002)
- "The economy has stalled" -- economic stagnation as cessation of
  motion (common usage)
- "The peace process has gone backward" -- regression as reversed
  direction (common journalistic usage)
- "Events are unfolding" -- temporal progression as spatial expansion
  and motion (conventional English)
- "The deadline is approaching" -- a future event as an entity moving
  toward the observer (Lakoff, "The Contemporary Theory of Metaphor,"
  1993)
- "The project is on track" -- expected progress as motion along the
  correct path (common project management usage)
- "We've lost ground on climate policy" -- regression as backward
  spatial displacement (common political usage)

## Origin Story

THE PROGRESS OF EXTERNAL EVENTS IS FORWARD MOTION is documented in
the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson & Schwartz, 1991) as part
of the broader Event Structure metaphorical system. Lakoff identified
it in "The Contemporary Theory of Metaphor" (1993) as one of the
mappings that give temporal events spatial structure. It is closely
related to the TIME IS MOTION family of metaphors but is specifically
about event progress rather than the passage of time itself. The
metaphor interacts with two complementary sub-metaphors cataloged
separately in this collection: EXTERNAL EVENTS AFFECTING PROGRESS ARE
FORCES AFFECTING FORWARD MOTION (when outside forces help or hinder
the moving entity) and INTERACTION BETWEEN PROGRESS AND EXTERNAL
EVENTS IS MOTION ALONG A PATH (when the experiencer and external
events are co-located on the path).

The metaphor is deeply embedded in Western notions of progress and
modernity. The Enlightenment concept of historical progress -- the idea
that history moves forward toward greater freedom, knowledge, and
prosperity -- depends on this metaphor. Critics of progress narratives,
from Nietzsche to postcolonial theorists, are in effect challenging the
mapping itself: the assumption that temporal succession entails spatial
advancement.
