## What It Brings

To broadcast is to scatter seeds widely across a field by hand -- a broad
cast of the arm. The agricultural term was adopted for radio transmission
in the early 1920s, mapping the undirected dispersal of seeds onto the
undirected dispersal of radio signals. Both go out in all directions, land
where they land, and reach whoever happens to be in range.

- **Undirected dispersal** -- the core structural import is the absence
  of targeting. A farmer broadcasting seed does not aim at individual
  furrows. A radio station broadcasting a signal does not choose
  individual receivers. The metaphor encodes a fundamental property of
  the medium: one-to-many communication where the sender has no control
  over -- and often no knowledge of -- who receives the message. This
  structural parallel is what made the word stick over alternatives like
  "transmit" or "send."
- **Abundance as strategy** -- broadcast sowing works by excess. You
  scatter more seeds than will germinate because you cannot control where
  each one lands. Mass media broadcasting follows the same logic: you
  produce content for millions knowing that most will not watch, listen,
  or care. Waste is built into the model. The metaphor encodes this
  tolerance for inefficiency as a feature, not a bug.
- **The sower's indifference** -- in the agricultural source, the farmer
  does not care which specific patch of soil receives which specific
  seed. Broadcasting treats the field as undifferentiated ground.
  Mass media inherited this indifference: a broadcast treats the
  audience as an undifferentiated mass. The demographic segmentation
  that advertisers later imposed was a correction to the metaphor's
  built-in assumption of homogeneity.

## Where It Breaks

- **Seeds grow; signals do not** -- the agricultural metaphor implies
  that what is scattered will take root, grow, and produce something.
  Broadcast signals do not germinate. They are received or not, consumed
  or not, but they do not develop in the receiver. The growth implication
  is entirely absent from the modern usage, but it lingers in phrases
  like "planting an idea" and "fertile ground for propaganda" -- adjacent
  metaphors that the agricultural origin of "broadcast" makes available
  but does not require.
- **Soil matters; airwaves do not discriminate** -- in agriculture,
  broadcast sowing depends on soil quality. Seeds landing on rock, clay,
  or sand fail differently. The parable of the sower (Matthew 13) is
  built on this variability. In radio and television broadcasting, the
  signal reaches all receivers with equal fidelity (within range). The
  metaphor's source domain encodes a sensitivity to receiving conditions
  that the target domain does not share -- unless you read "soil quality"
  as audience receptivity, which stretches the mapping past its useful
  point.
- **The metaphor inverted its own value judgment** -- "narrowcast," coined
  in the 1970s, was created as the antonym of "broadcast" to describe
  targeted media. But in agriculture, narrow sowing (placing seeds
  individually in rows) is more efficient than broadcasting. The
  metaphor died so completely that its agricultural value hierarchy
  inverted: in farming, narrowcasting (precision sowing) is the superior
  technique; in media, broadcasting was the prestige model and
  narrowcasting the niche alternative. The dead metaphor enabled a
  cultural inversion of its own source domain's economics.
- **Digital "broadcasting" is not broadcasting** -- streaming a video to
  selected subscribers, pushing notifications to opted-in users,
  publishing a podcast -- none of these are broadcast in the agricultural
  sense (undirected, scattered, wasteful). Yet the word persists. A
  "broadcast message" in a chat application goes to a defined list, not
  to an open field. The metaphor has been stretched so far past its
  structural core that it now means little more than "send to many."

## Expressions

- "Broadcast television" -- the original radio/TV usage, now a
  retronym distinguishing over-the-air transmission from cable and
  streaming
- "Broadcast message" -- a message sent to all members of a group,
  where "all" has replaced "anyone in range" as the defining feature
- "Don't broadcast it" -- colloquial warning against sharing information
  widely, where the agricultural scattering maps onto social
  indiscretion
- "Narrowcast" -- the antonym coined to describe targeted media, which
  only makes sense if you know the agricultural metaphor it contrasts
  with
- "Broadcaster" -- a person or organization that transmits to mass
  audiences, where the agent noun has entirely replaced the agricultural
  meaning (nobody calls a seed-scattering farmer a "broadcaster")
- "Network broadcast" -- in computing, a packet sent to all nodes on a
  network segment, the most structurally faithful surviving usage

## Origin Story

"Broadcast" as an agricultural term dates to at least the 18th century,
describing the technique of scattering seed by hand in a wide arc --
literally a "broad cast" of the arm, as opposed to drilling seeds into
individual rows. The technique was standard before the invention of the
seed drill and remained common for grass, clover, and other crops where
precision placement was unnecessary.

The metaphorical transfer to radio happened rapidly in the early 1920s.
KDKA in Pittsburgh, generally credited as the first commercial radio
station (1920), needed vocabulary for what it was doing: sending signals
indiscriminately to whoever had a receiver. "Broadcast" captured the
undirected, one-to-many structure precisely. By 1922, the radio usage was
dominant. By 1930, most English speakers had never used "broadcast" in its
agricultural sense.

The metaphor's death was accelerated by urbanization: as fewer people
farmed, fewer people had the experiential referent. By mid-century,
"broadcast" was a communications term that happened to have an
agricultural etymology, like "broadcast" itself had been broadcast -- sent
out so widely that its origin was lost in the scattering.
