## What It Brings

The Divine Child is Jung's archetype of futurity, potential, and radical
new beginnings. In "The Psychology of the Child Archetype" (CW9.1), Jung
identifies two defining motifs: *abandonment* (the child is exposed,
orphaned, cast out) and *invincibility* (the child survives against
impossible odds and grows into something transformative). Moses in the
bulrushes, the infant Heracles strangling serpents, the Christ child in
the manger surrounded by threats. The archetype's structural power lies
in the paradox that the most vulnerable thing in the system is also the
carrier of its future.

Key structural parallels:

- **The greenfield project** -- the new initiative that carries all the
  organization's hopes for renewal. It starts with nothing (abandonment:
  no users, no revenue, no track record) and must survive the established
  system's indifference or hostility. The ones that survive become the
  company's next core product. Most die in infancy.
- **Beginner's mind as strategic advantage** -- the divine child sees
  what the experienced cannot because it has no framework to constrain
  perception. In organizations: the junior engineer who asks "why do we
  do it this way?" and exposes an assumption so old it has become
  invisible. The child's ignorance is diagnostic.
- **The abandoned startup** -- Jung's abandonment motif maps directly
  onto the startup that loses its funding, its executive sponsor, or
  its institutional support. The archetype predicts that abandonment is
  not just a risk but a structural feature: the new thing *must* be
  separated from its origin to develop its own identity.
- **Invincibility through smallness** -- the child survives because it
  is too small for the established powers to take seriously. In business:
  the disruptive startup that incumbents ignore until it is too late.
  Christensen's "innovator's dilemma" is the Divine Child myth retold
  in business-school language.
- **The future as a person** -- the archetype personifies possibility
  itself. In organizational rhetoric: "this project is the future of
  the company," "this technology is in its infancy." We do not just
  plan for the future; we nurture it, protect it, invest in it -- all
  language borrowed from the care of children.

## Where It Breaks

- **Survivorship bias disguised as destiny** -- the archetype tells the
  story of the child who *survived*. It does not tell the story of the
  thousands of exposed infants who simply died. Applying this archetype
  to startups or new initiatives romanticizes the survivors and erases
  the structural conditions (funding, timing, privilege) that determined
  who lived and who didn't.
- **Infantilizes the new** -- calling something a "divine child" or
  treating new projects as infants implies they need protection rather
  than scrutiny. Some new initiatives should be killed early. The
  archetype creates emotional resistance to the rational evaluation of
  new things by wrapping them in the innocence of childhood.
- **The "specialness" trap** -- the divine child is always exceptional,
  always destined. In organizations, this becomes the pet project that
  gets infinite runway because leadership has emotionally invested in
  its narrative rather than its metrics. The archetype provides
  mythological cover for sunk-cost fallacies.
- **Ignores the infrastructure of emergence** -- the myth focuses on
  the child's innate specialness, not on the conditions that enable
  survival. In reality, greenfield projects succeed because of
  resourcing, sponsorship, market timing, and team composition -- not
  because of intrinsic destiny. The archetype obscures the structural
  factors behind emergence.
- **Cultural specificity of the "miraculous birth"** -- Jung's examples
  cluster around Greco-Roman and Christian narratives (Heracles, Dionysus,
  Christ). The motif of the special child exists cross-culturally but
  the specific framing of divine parentage and miraculous survival
  carries assumptions about individual exceptionalism that not all
  cultures share.

## Expressions

- "Greenfield project" -- the new initiative with no legacy constraints;
  the organizational divine child, born into possibility
- "In its infancy" -- describing a technology or market as a child,
  implying both vulnerability and potential
- "Skunkworks" -- the abandoned/hidden project that develops outside
  institutional oversight, recapitulating the abandonment motif
- "Disruptive innovation" -- Christensen's framework is the divine child
  myth in business language: the small, weak entrant that the powerful
  ignore until it transforms the market
- "Fresh eyes" -- the newcomer's perspective valued precisely because
  it lacks the expert's assumptions; beginner's mind as organizational
  resource
- "Born in a garage" -- the origin myth of HP, Apple, Amazon; the
  humble birth that retroactively signals destiny
- "MVP" (minimum viable product) -- the deliberately vulnerable first
  version, exposed to the market to see if it survives; ritualized
  abandonment

## Origin Story

Jung's "The Psychology of the Child Archetype" (CW9.1, 1941) is written
with Karl Kerenyi, who provides the mythological parallels while Jung
develops the psychological interpretation. Jung argues that the child
archetype represents the self's potential for wholeness -- it appears in
dreams and myths when the psyche is about to undergo transformation.
The child is both the beginning and the goal.

The archetype's commercial resonance is most visible in startup culture,
where "born in a garage" origin stories deliberately echo the humble-
birth-of-the-hero pattern. The Silicon Valley founding myth is the
Divine Child narrative secularized and venture-funded.
