## What It Brings

Mary Shelley published *Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus* in 1818,
and by the twentieth century "Frankenstein" had become the default metaphor
for any technology that turns on its creator. The source is so thoroughly
dead that most people misapply it -- calling the monster "Frankenstein"
rather than the scientist -- which only proves how far the metaphor has
drifted from its literary origin. What survives is a pure structural
template: someone creates something, loses control of it, and suffers the
consequences.

Key structural parallels:

- **The creator's hubris** -- Victor Frankenstein does not ask whether he
  should animate dead tissue; he only asks whether he can. The metaphor
  maps this onto technologists who pursue capability without considering
  consequence. "Playing God" and "Frankensteinian" both import this
  structure: the sin is not the creation itself but the failure to
  consider what comes after.
- **The creation exceeds the creator** -- Frankenstein's creature is
  stronger, more durable, and ultimately more resourceful than Victor.
  The metaphor maps this onto any technology that outgrows its designer's
  ability to control it: nuclear weapons, self-replicating software,
  autonomous AI systems. The structural import is that *creation is
  irreversible* -- once the creature exists, it cannot be uncreated.
- **The creator's abandonment** -- Victor flees from his creation the
  moment it opens its eyes. The metaphor maps this onto the pattern of
  technologists who build systems and then disclaim responsibility for
  their effects. "Move fast and break things" is a Frankensteinian motto:
  create, release, and let someone else deal with the consequences.
- **The creature's grievance** -- in Shelley's novel, the creature is
  articulate and morally aware. It becomes destructive not because it is
  inherently evil but because it is rejected by its creator and by
  society. This is the most sophisticated structural import: the metaphor
  suggests that dangerous technology is not born dangerous but becomes
  dangerous through neglect, misunderstanding, and social failure.

## Where It Breaks

- **Most technology is not autonomous** -- Frankenstein's creature acts on
  its own volition, with its own desires and plans. Most dangerous
  technologies do not. A nuclear reactor does not decide to melt down;
  a social media algorithm does not choose to radicalize users. The
  Frankenstein metaphor imports agency where there is none, which directs
  fear toward the technology itself rather than toward the humans who
  designed, deployed, and failed to regulate it.
- **The metaphor implies a single creator** -- Victor Frankenstein works
  alone in his laboratory. Modern technology is created by teams,
  corporations, and ecosystems. There is no single Dr. Frankenstein to
  blame for climate change, algorithmic bias, or antibiotic resistance.
  The metaphor's individualism obscures systemic responsibility.
- **"Frankenstein" has become synonymous with "monster"** -- the popular
  conflation of creator and creature is not just a literary error; it
  reveals a structural limitation. The metaphor has lost its most
  interesting element -- the relationship between creator and creation --
  and collapsed into a simple label for anything scary and artificial.
  "Frankenfood" (genetically modified organisms) uses the prefix to mean
  "unnatural and dangerous," discarding the novel's moral complexity
  entirely.
- **The novel is a tragedy of compassion, not a warning about
  technology** -- Shelley's actual argument is that the creature's violence
  results from Victor's refusal to love and take responsibility for it.
  The popular metaphor inverts this: it treats creation itself as the sin,
  not abandonment. This makes "Frankenstein" a conservative metaphor that
  counsels against innovation, when the novel actually counsels against
  irresponsibility.
- **The binary of control/no-control is too simple** -- the Frankenstein
  frame offers only two states: the creator controls the creation, or the
  creation runs amok. Real technology risk involves degrees of control,
  partial predictability, probabilistic failure, and distributed
  governance. The metaphor has no vocabulary for "mostly under control
  with occasional edge-case failures," which is where most real technology
  risk actually lives.

## Expressions

- "Frankenstein's monster" -- labeling any technology perceived as
  dangerous and out of control, from GMOs to AI
- "Frankenfood" -- genetically modified organisms, using the prefix to
  import fear of the unnatural
- "Playing God" -- the Frankensteinian sin of creating life or life-like
  systems without adequate humility
- "We've created a monster" -- the moment of realization that a technology
  has exceeded its creator's control
- "The genie is out of the bottle" -- a parallel metaphor often used in
  conjunction with Frankenstein to express irreversibility
- "Don't be Dr. Frankenstein" -- cautionary invocation in technology
  ethics discussions

## Origin Story

Mary Shelley wrote *Frankenstein* at age eighteen during the famous
summer of 1816 at the Villa Diodati, where Byron challenged his guests
to write ghost stories. Shelley drew on contemporary experiments in
galvanism -- Luigi Galvani's demonstrations that electricity could make
dead frog legs twitch -- and on the Romantic era's anxieties about
industrial technology. The novel's subtitle, "The Modern Prometheus,"
explicitly connects it to the Greek myth of stealing divine power, a
connection that has remained active in technology discourse for two
centuries. The Frankenstein metaphor has been applied to every major
technological anxiety since: electricity, nuclear power, genetic
engineering, cloning, nanotechnology, and artificial intelligence. Its
durability suggests that the creator-loses-control-of-creation structure
maps onto a deep and recurring human concern.
