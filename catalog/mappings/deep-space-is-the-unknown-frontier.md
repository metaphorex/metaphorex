## What It Brings

"Space: the final frontier." Star Trek's opening narration (1966) made
explicit what science fiction had always implied: deep space is the
American frontier relocated beyond Earth's atmosphere. The metaphor
takes the structure of terrestrial exploration -- uncharted territory,
pioneer settlements, encounters with the unknown, the expanding edge
of civilization -- and maps it onto space, and from there onto any
domain of radical uncertainty.

Key structural parallels:

- **The frontier as boundary of the known** -- Frederick Jackson
  Turner's 1893 "frontier thesis" defined American identity through
  the existence of an advancing edge between civilization and
  wilderness. Deep space inherits this role: beyond the frontier lies
  the unknown, and moving into it is inherently virtuous. The metaphor
  maps onto how we talk about scientific research ("the frontier of
  knowledge"), technological development ("the bleeding edge"), and
  business innovation ("uncharted territory").
- **The explorer as hero** -- on a frontier, individual courage and
  resourcefulness matter more than institutional process. Kirk, Picard,
  and every SF space captain inherit the mythos of Lewis and Clark,
  Magellan, and Columbus. The metaphor privileges individual agency
  and daring over collective planning -- when applied to science and
  business, it frames innovators as explorers rather than as members
  of systems.
- **Terra nullius -- the empty land** -- frontiers are narratively
  empty before the explorer arrives. Deep space literalizes this: it
  is actually empty. But when the metaphor is re-applied to
  intellectual or commercial domains, it imports the assumption that
  the territory is unclaimed, erasing prior inhabitants (indigenous
  knowledge, existing competitors, earlier researchers).
- **Expansion as destiny** -- the frontier must be crossed. Staying
  behind is stagnation. This maps onto growth ideology in business
  ("expand or die"), the imperative to publish in academia ("push the
  boundaries"), and the techno-optimist conviction that humanity's
  destiny is interplanetary.

## Where It Breaks

- **Space is not land.** A frontier implies habitable territory on the
  other side. Deep space is radiation-soaked vacuum. The metaphor
  conceals the fact that space is actively hostile to human life, not
  merely unknown. Treating space as frontier implies it will eventually
  be "settled" -- an assumption that may be physically wrong.
- **The frontier metaphor carries colonial violence.** The American
  frontier was not empty; it was inhabited. Turner's thesis celebrated
  westward expansion while erasing genocide. When deep space is framed
  as frontier, the metaphor imports an ideology of rightful conquest.
  Applied to business or intellectual domains, it naturalizes aggressive
  expansion and territorial thinking.
- **There is no "other side."** A terrestrial frontier has a known
  world behind it and a knowable world ahead. Deep space is
  effectively infinite and mostly empty. The frontier metaphor implies
  a destination; space exploration has none. This makes "the final
  frontier" paradoxical: a frontier with no far side is not a
  frontier but an abyss.
- **The metaphor individualizes what is fundamentally institutional.**
  Space exploration requires massive state or corporate investment,
  not lone pioneers. No one walks into space the way a homesteader
  walked into Kansas. The frontier metaphor systematically understates
  the institutional, financial, and technological infrastructure
  required, making space exploration seem like an act of will rather
  than an act of engineering.

## Expressions

- "The final frontier" -- Star Trek's defining phrase, now used for any
  domain presented as the last remaining challenge (the brain, the
  deep ocean, consciousness)
- "Boldly go" -- the aspirational version, applied to any venture into
  unknown territory
- "Uncharted territory" -- standard business and policy language for
  novel situations, directly importing the exploration frame
- "Space cowboy" -- the explicit fusion of frontier mythology with
  space travel, used for maverick entrepreneurs (Steve Miller, Elon
  Musk coverage)
- "New world" -- applied to virtual reality, the internet, and
  emerging markets, recycling colonial exploration language
- "Pushing the boundaries" -- the frontier as something to be moved
  outward, standard in science and engineering discourse
- "Pioneer" -- used for early adopters, first movers, and founding
  researchers, carrying all the frontier's heroic associations

## Origin Story

The deep-space-as-frontier metaphor predates Star Trek but was
cemented by it. Early space-age rhetoric deliberately invoked frontier
mythology: Kennedy's "New Frontier" speech (1960) linked space
exploration to American expansionist identity. Science fiction
amplified this by casting space travel as a Western: *Star Trek* was
pitched to NBC as "Wagon Train to the stars." The metaphor was
already present in pulp SF (E. E. Smith's *Lensman* series, 1930s-1950s),
but television gave it mass cultural penetration. By the 1990s, the
frontier metaphor migrated from space to the internet -- "cyberspace"
and "the electronic frontier" (the EFF was founded in 1990) extended
the same spatial metaphor to a non-spatial domain.
