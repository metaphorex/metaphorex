## What It Brings

When something changes state, it turns. This metaphor extends the basic
CHANGE IS MOTION mapping by specifying what kind of motion corresponds to
what kind of change: not just any movement, but a change in direction. If
you are heading north and you turn east, you have changed your trajectory
-- and that turning maps onto a shift in condition, quality, or fortune.
The metaphor captures change that is not just progression along a path but
a departure from the current trajectory.

Key structural parallels:

- **State change as turning** -- "The economy took a turn for the worse."
  "Her health turned a corner." "Things took an unexpected turn." A change
  of state is a change of heading. The entity was going one way (in one
  condition) and now it is going another way (in a different condition).
  The turn is the moment of transition -- the pivot between before and
  after.
- **Improvement as turning upward** -- "Things are looking up." "The
  company turned around." "His fortunes took an upward turn." Combined
  with orientational metaphors (GOOD IS UP), a positive change of state
  is a turn in the upward direction. Recovery is a U-turn from decline.
  The spatial geometry gives evaluative structure to the change.
- **Deterioration as turning downward** -- "The situation took a
  downturn." "His career went south." "Things went downhill from there."
  Negative change is a turn toward a bad direction -- typically down,
  backward, or off the established path. The metaphor maps the normative
  quality of the change onto the spatial quality of the new direction.
- **Unexpected change as a sharp turn** -- "Events took a sharp turn."
  "Nobody saw the twist coming." "That was a sudden turn of events."
  Surprise is the sharpness of the angle: a gentle curve is a gradual,
  expected transition, while a sharp turn is an abrupt, unforeseen one.
  The metaphor gives surprise a geometry.
- **Maintaining state as staying the course** -- "We're staying on track."
  "Don't change course." "Keep heading in the same direction." If change
  of state is change of direction, then continuity is a straight line.
  Perseverance is the refusal to turn. The metaphor rewards consistency
  and frames deviation as something requiring justification.

## Where It Breaks

- **Not all state changes have a prior trajectory** -- the metaphor
  assumes something was already moving in a direction before the change.
  But some state changes have no meaningful "before" direction. A person
  who falls ill was not "heading toward" health in any experientially
  meaningful way -- they were simply in a state. The metaphor imposes a
  narrative of trajectory on situations that may have none.
- **The metaphor linearizes multidimensional change** -- direction in
  physical space is defined relative to a coordinate system. But states
  of affairs are not one-dimensional. A company might simultaneously
  improve financially, deteriorate culturally, and hold steady
  operationally. The metaphor's demand for a single direction of change
  collapses these dimensions into one line.
- **Turning implies a single pivot point** -- the metaphor codes state
  change as a moment of turning, which suggests a discrete event. But
  many state changes are gradual and have no identifiable pivot. The
  economy does not "turn" the way a car turns; it drifts through
  overlapping micro-changes. The metaphor manufactures a turning point
  where often there is only a slow curve.
- **Direction is relative to an observer** -- "going south" as
  deterioration is a cultural convention, not a universal mapping.
  The metaphor's directional evaluations (up is good, down is bad,
  forward is progress) are grounded in orientational metaphors specific
  to certain cultural and linguistic traditions. In spatial navigation,
  south is just a direction; in the metaphor, it is a judgment.
- **The metaphor rewards linear persistence** -- because staying on
  course is good and turning is marked, the metaphor creates a bias
  against adaptation. "Changing course" carries an implicit admission
  of error, when in reality changing direction may be the wisest response
  to new information. The metaphor makes flexibility look like failure.

## Expressions

- "The economy took a turn for the worse" -- negative state change as a
  turn toward a bad direction
- "Her health turned a corner" -- recovery as rounding a bend toward
  improvement
- "Things took an unexpected turn" -- surprising state change as a sudden
  change of heading
- "The company turned around" -- recovery from decline as a reversal of
  direction
- "His career went south" -- deterioration as motion toward a negatively
  valued direction
- "Don't change course now" -- advice against state change as advice
  against altering direction
- "We need to change direction" -- advocating for state change as
  advocating a new heading
- "A twist of fate" -- an unexpected state change as a sharp bend in
  a path
- "Things went downhill from there" -- progressive deterioration as
  descent along a slope
- "She steered the conversation in a new direction" -- causing state
  change as redirecting a vehicle

## Origin Story

This metaphor appears in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson &
Schwartz 1991) as a specification within the Event Structure metaphor
system. It elaborates on the basic CHANGE IS MOTION mapping by specifying
the kind of motion: change of state corresponds not to motion in general
but to a particular kind of motion -- a change in direction. This makes it
a more specific mapping than CHANGE IS MOTION, applying particularly to
cases where the change involves a qualitative shift (getting better,
getting worse, entering a crisis) rather than mere progression along a
stable trajectory.

The experiential basis is straightforward: a child walking or being
carried experiences changes in direction as qualitatively different from
continued forward motion. A turn is felt in the body -- the shift in
momentum, the new visual field, the reorientation. This kinesthetic
experience provides the grounding for understanding abstract state
changes as directional shifts.
