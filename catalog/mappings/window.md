## What It Brings

Old Norse vindauga -- "wind eye." An opening in a wall was understood as
the building's eye, and what it perceived was wind. The compound fuses
two sensory metaphors: the opening sees (it is an eye) and what it sees
is tactile (wind, not light). This is not how we think about windows
today. We think of glass, transparency, and vision. The original metaphor
was about ventilation and the building as a sensing body.

- **The building as a body** -- calling an opening an "eye" maps the
  structure of a face onto the structure of a wall. Eyes are organs of
  perception set into a containing surface (the face); windows are
  openings for perception set into a containing surface (the wall).
  The metaphor imports the idea that buildings have sensory organs --
  that a house without windows is blind. This anthropomorphism of
  architecture persists in expressions like "the eyes of the building"
  even though the specific word "window" no longer triggers the
  association.
- **Wind as the primary perception** -- modern windows are about
  light and sight. The Norse compound specifies wind, not light. In
  Scandinavian longhouses, the opening in the wall or roof served
  primarily for ventilation and smoke escape. The "wind eye" perceived
  airflow, not visual scenes. The metaphor records a material reality:
  in a world without glass, an opening in a wall was primarily a hole
  for air, and closing it meant blocking the wind, not the view.
- **The word displaced its competitor** -- Old English had eagthyrl,
  literally "eye-hole," for the same architectural feature. The Norse
  vindauga replaced it completely after the Danelaw period. Both words
  used the eye metaphor, but the Norse version specified what the eye
  perceived (wind), while the English version named only the shape
  (hole). The more specific metaphor won.

## Where It Breaks

- **Glass killed the wind** -- the invention of affordable window glass
  in the late medieval period severed the "wind" half of the compound.
  A glazed window does not perceive wind; it blocks it. The word
  retained the form vindauga > window while the referent transformed
  from an opening that admits wind to a barrier that excludes it. The
  metaphor did not merely die -- the physical object it described
  became the opposite of what the word meant. A window is now an
  anti-wind-eye.
- **The second death: GUI windows** -- in the 1970s and 1980s, "window"
  was adopted for rectangular regions on a computer screen. Xerox PARC
  and later Apple and Microsoft mapped the architectural window (a
  bounded opening that frames a view of another space) onto the GUI
  element (a bounded rectangle that frames a view of another
  application or document). This second metaphorical mapping ignored
  the Norse etymology entirely -- it was a metaphor built on the dead
  metaphor's modern meaning, not its original one. A GUI window has no
  wind and no eye.
- **"Window of opportunity" reveals the temporal stretching** -- when
  "window" is used metaphorically for a limited time period ("window
  of opportunity," "launch window," "transfer window"), it maps the
  architectural sense of a bounded opening onto temporal boundaries.
  You can see through a window but not through the wall; you can act
  during the window but not before or after. This metaphorical
  extension works only because the architectural meaning (bounded
  opening) is established -- the Norse "wind eye" would make "window
  of opportunity" incomprehensible.
- **The architectural metaphor hides what windows cost** -- windows
  are structurally expensive. They weaken walls, leak heat, and
  require maintenance. In the original Norse context, a wind-eye was
  a deliberate vulnerability: you cut a hole in your shelter because
  you needed air, knowing you were sacrificing protection. Modern
  usage treats windows as unambiguous goods ("more windows" is always
  better in real estate marketing). The dead metaphor hides the
  engineering tradeoff that the original term encoded: every eye you
  cut into a wall is a wound you choose to inflict.

## Expressions

- "Window" -- the base architectural term, two full metaphorical
  deaths from "wind eye"
- "Window shopping" -- looking without buying, where the glass barrier
  (absent from the original wind-eye) is the defining feature
- "Window of opportunity" -- a bounded time period for action, mapping
  the spatial opening onto a temporal opening
- "Window seat" -- a seat beside a window, where proximity to the
  opening confers the privilege of the view
- "Window dressing" -- superficial presentation to make something look
  better, where the window is a display surface rather than a
  perceptual organ
- "Overton window" -- the range of politically acceptable positions,
  mapping the bounded frame of a window onto the bounded range of
  discourse

## Origin Story

Old Norse vindauga is a transparent compound: vindr (wind) + auga (eye).
The word entered English during the period of Norse settlement in Britain
(9th-11th centuries), where it gradually displaced the native Old English
eagthyrl ("eye-hole" or "eye-opening"). The displacement was complete by
the 13th century -- eagthyrl disappeared entirely from the language.

The original referent was a hole in a wall or roof, sometimes covered
with translucent animal membrane or oiled cloth, but often simply open.
Glass windows existed in Roman Britain but were rare and expensive.
Medieval English windows were largely unglazed until the 14th-15th
centuries, meaning "wind eye" was literally accurate for several
centuries of English usage: the window really was an eye for the wind.

The metaphor's first death came with widespread glazing. By the 16th
century, "window" meant a glass-fitted opening, and the wind connection
was lost. The second death came in the 1980s with the GUI usage, which
mapped the architectural meaning (bounded view-frame) onto screen
regions. Microsoft named its entire operating system after the dead
metaphor in 1985, ensuring that billions of people would use the word
"window" daily without thinking about wind, eyes, or walls.

The deepest archaeological layer is the eye metaphor itself. Both the
Norse vindauga and the displaced English eagthyrl used "eye" for an
opening in a wall. This is not accidental: the eye-as-opening mapping
runs throughout Indo-European languages. The building is a body; the
opening is its organ of perception. When we look out a window, the
dead metaphor insists, the building is also looking out through us.
