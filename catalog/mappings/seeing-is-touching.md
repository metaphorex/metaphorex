## What It Brings

The eyes reach out and make contact. Vision is not passive reception but
active touching -- the gaze extends from the body, lands on things, and
handles them. This metaphor reverses the physical reality of light entering
the eye and replaces it with something much older: the extramission theory
of vision, where the eyes emit rays that touch the world. The theory is
scientifically wrong but metaphorically productive. It gives vision agency,
direction, and physical force.

Key structural parallels:

- **The gaze as a reaching hand** -- "Her eyes landed on the painting."
  "His gaze fell on the letter." "She cast her eyes around the room." The
  eyes project outward and make contact with surfaces, just as a hand
  reaches out and touches. Looking at something is putting your eyes on it.
- **Visual attention as grip** -- "I can't take my eyes off her." "His
  eyes were glued to the screen." "She held him with her gaze." Once the
  eyes touch something, they can grip it, hold it, or be stuck to it.
  Sustained attention is a sustained grasp -- you cannot let go of what
  you are looking at.
- **Scanning as manual exploration** -- "His eyes picked out every detail."
  "She ran her eyes over the document." "He swept the room with his gaze."
  Visual scanning maps onto the hand moving over a surface, feeling for
  features. The eyes do not merely receive; they actively explore, probe,
  and select.
- **The gaze as physical force** -- "She felt his eyes on her." "His stare
  bore into her." "Stop eyeing me." Being looked at is being touched, and
  unwanted looking is unwanted touching. The metaphor gives the gaze a
  physical pressure that the looked-at person can feel.
- **Visual sharpness as tactile precision** -- "A keen eye." "A piercing
  gaze." "Sharp-eyed." The metaphor maps the acuity of vision onto the
  sharpness of a point -- the eye is an instrument that can cut, pierce,
  or poke. Fine visual discrimination is fine-pointed touching.

## Where It Breaks

- **Vision is actually reception, not projection** -- light enters the eye;
  nothing leaves it. The metaphor inverts the direction of causation. This
  is not merely a scientific quibble: the projection model makes vision feel
  like an act of will ("I'll look at X"), which understates how much vision
  is shaped by involuntary processes (peripheral detection, saccades, change
  blindness).
- **Touch requires proximity; vision does not** -- the metaphor collapses
  the distance between seer and seen. You can see a star but you cannot
  touch it. The touching frame makes vision feel intimate and proximal, which
  is powerful for describing close observation but misleading for remote
  perception. Surveillance at a distance is not well described as touching.
- **The metaphor enables the "male gaze" problem** -- if seeing is touching,
  then looking at someone is a form of physical contact, and looking without
  consent is touching without consent. The metaphor has been rightly
  criticized for naturalizing the objectifying gaze: it makes visual
  attention feel like possession, which reinforces power dynamics where the
  looker acts upon the looked-at.
- **Not all vision is focused** -- the touching model works for directed
  gaze but not for peripheral vision, ambient awareness, or the unfocused
  stare. The hand-eye parallel privileges foveal, intentional looking and
  makes diffuse visual experience hard to articulate. You cannot "touch" with
  your peripheral vision.
- **The metaphor obscures the role of the seen object** -- in actual touch,
  the object is passive. But seen objects emit or reflect light; they
  actively present themselves to vision. A bright color "catches your eye"
  -- agency runs from object to seer, the opposite direction from the
  touching model.

## Expressions

- "I can't take my eyes off her" -- sustained visual attention as inability
  to release a grip
- "His eyes picked out every detail" -- visual scanning as manual selection
- "Her gaze fell on the painting" -- directed attention as the eye landing
  on a surface
- "He ran his eyes over the document" -- scanning as moving a hand across
  a surface
- "She felt his eyes on her" -- being seen as being physically touched
- "A piercing gaze" -- visual intensity as sharp-pointed contact
- "His stare bore into her" -- intense looking as drilling or penetrating
- "She cast her eyes around the room" -- looking as throwing one's gaze
  outward
- "His eyes were glued to the screen" -- attention as adhesion, inability
  to detach
- "Stop eyeing me" -- unwanted looking as unwanted touching

## Origin Story

Lakoff and Johnson discuss SEEING IS TOUCHING in *Metaphors We Live By* as
part of a broader analysis of how sensory experience structures abstract
thought. The metaphor draws on the ancient extramission theory of vision --
the idea that the eyes send out rays that touch objects in the world. Euclid,
Ptolemy, and Galen all held versions of this theory. It was not replaced by
the intromission model (light entering the eye) until Alhazen's *Book of
Optics* in the eleventh century, and even then, the metaphorical habits
persisted.

The persistence is revealing. Despite centuries of knowing that vision is
reception, English speakers still talk as if the eyes project outward. This
is Lakoff and Johnson's point: metaphors are not beliefs about how the world
works. They are conceptual structures that organize experience regardless of
what we know scientifically. We know the eyes do not touch. We speak as if
they do.
