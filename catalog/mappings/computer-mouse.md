## What It Brings

A small creature that scurries across a surface, tethered by its tail.
The metaphor mapped the physical appearance of a palm-sized device with
a trailing cord onto the most familiar small, tailed creature. It gave
an alien new technology an instantly familiar name.

- **Shape as naming** -- the original mouse was a wooden block with a
  single button and a cord emerging from the rear. The cord looked like
  a tail. The device was small and fit in the hand. "Mouse" was a shape
  description, not a behavioral analogy. This is unusual among computing
  metaphors: most map function (virus replicates, firewall blocks), but
  mouse mapped form.
- **Domestication of the machine** -- calling the input device a mouse
  made the computer approachable. A mouse is small, harmless, even cute.
  The name invited users to pick it up, move it around, play with it.
  This was significant in 1968, when computers were room-sized machines
  operated by specialists. Giving a peripheral a pet name signaled that
  ordinary people could use computers.
- **Embodied interaction** -- the mouse made the hand an extension of
  the cursor. You do not operate a mouse; you move it as if it were an
  extension of your own pointing gesture. The animal name reinforced
  this intimacy: a mouse is something you hold gently, something that
  responds to small movements. The metaphor helped users forget they
  were interacting with electronics.

## Where It Breaks

- **The tail is gone** -- Engelbart's naming rationale was the cord.
  Wireless mice have no cord. The defining visual feature that
  motivated the metaphor has been physically eliminated, and nobody
  noticed because the metaphor was already dead. This is a rare case
  where the referent's essential feature disappeared while the name
  persisted unchanged. It is as if we kept calling cars "horseless
  carriages" after every carriage had disappeared.
- **The CAT connection is forgotten** -- Engelbart's team called the
  on-screen cursor the "CAT" (reportedly from "Computer-Aided
  Tracking," though the backronym may be apocryphal). The mouse appeared
  to chase the cat across the screen. This playful ecological metaphor
  -- predator and prey, input device and cursor -- was lost almost
  immediately. Nobody today thinks of the cursor as a cat being chased
  by a mouse. The relational structure of the metaphor died while the
  surface label survived.
- **Mice are not tools** -- the animal metaphor implies the device has
  its own nature, its own tendencies. Real mice are unpredictable,
  skittish, and have agency. A computer mouse is entirely passive --
  it does nothing until moved by a human hand. The creature metaphor
  attributes a vitality to the device that it does not possess. This
  mismatch is invisible because the metaphor is so dead that "mouse"
  is simply a word, not an image.
- **Plural confusion as fossil** -- the uncertainty about whether the
  plural is "mice" or "mouses" (both are used) is a linguistic fossil
  of the dead metaphor. If "mouse" were purely a technical term,
  "mouses" would be standard (as with "mongooses," not "mongeese").
  The fact that "mice" persists as a common plural shows the animal
  metaphor is not entirely decomposed -- it still exerts gravitational
  force on the morphology even though it exerts none on the imagery.

## Expressions

- "Mouse click" -- the primary interaction verb, entirely opaque as
  metaphor; mice do not click
- "Mouse pointer" -- the on-screen cursor, a term that preserves no
  trace of the CAT origin
- "Mouse pad" -- the surface, named after the device rather than the
  animal; a pad for a mouse rather than a nest
- "Scroll mouse" -- the variant with a scroll wheel, adding a
  mechanical metaphor (scroll as rolled parchment) on top of the
  animal metaphor
- "Mice" vs. "mouses" -- the plural debate, a living fossil of the
  metaphor's animal origin
- "Mousing around" -- casual exploration of an interface, the only
  expression that preserves the animal's behavioral quality of
  scurrying and investigating

## Origin Story

Douglas Engelbart and Bill English built the first mouse at the Stanford
Research Institute in 1964. It was a wooden shell with a single button
and two perpendicular wheels underneath that tracked movement. A cord
ran from the rear of the device to the computer.

Engelbart named it in a 1968 paper and demonstrated it publicly at what
became known as "The Mother of All Demos" (December 9, 1968, at the
Fall Joint Computer Conference in San Francisco). In the demo, he used
the mouse alongside a chord keyset to navigate a hypertext system --
decades before the World Wide Web.

The name "mouse" was informal and was expected to be replaced by a
proper technical term once the device reached production. It never was.
Xerox PARC adopted the mouse for the Alto in 1973. Apple adopted it
for the Macintosh in 1984. By then, the name was fixed. The animal
metaphor had died, and no one thought to rename it.

The irony of the metaphor's death is its completeness. A wireless,
laser-tracking, ergonomic peripheral with five buttons and a scroll
wheel bears no physical resemblance to a mouse. The tail is gone, the
size has changed, the shape has evolved. Yet the name persists because
it was dead before any of those changes happened. You cannot kill a
metaphor that is already dead.
