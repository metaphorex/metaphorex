## What It Brings

In Aphorisms XXXIX and XLI of the *Novum Organum* (1620), Bacon
identifies the first and most fundamental class of cognitive error:
"The Idols of the Tribe have their foundation in human nature itself,
and in the tribe or race of men. For it is a false assertion that the
sense of man is the measure of things." The mind is a "false mirror,
which, receiving rays irregularly, distorts and discolors the nature
of things by mingling its own nature with it."

Key structural parallels:

- **Idol as false god** -- Bacon chose "idol" (Latin *idolum*, from
  Greek *eidolon* = image, phantom) deliberately. Cognitive errors
  are not mere mistakes; they are false images that the mind worships
  as truth. The religious framing implies that overcoming bias requires
  something like conversion -- a fundamental reorientation of how one
  relates to one's own perceptions.
- **The tribe is the species** -- unlike the other three idol categories
  (individual, social, institutional), tribal idols afflict all humans
  by virtue of being human. The errors are built into the hardware.
  No one is exempt.
- **The false mirror** -- the mind does not passively receive reality
  but actively distorts it. This is Bacon's version of what cognitive
  science would later call "motivated reasoning," "confirmation bias,"
  and "pattern-seeking." The mirror metaphor captures both the
  reflective pretension (we think we are seeing the world) and the
  systematic distortion (we are seeing ourselves).
- **Ordering impulse** -- Bacon notes that the human mind "supposes a
  greater degree of order and regularity in things than it actually
  finds." We see patterns in noise, impose narratives on randomness,
  and mistake correlation for causation. This anticipates the
  clustering illusion, apophenia, and the narrative fallacy by
  centuries.

## Where It Breaks

- **Bacon frames biases as removable obstacles; they are not.** The
  idol metaphor implies that cognitive biases are false gods that can
  be smashed, like Moses destroying the golden calf. Modern cognitive
  science shows that biases are structural features of human cognition
  -- heuristics that often work well and cannot be simply eliminated.
  You can mitigate confirmation bias through methodology; you cannot
  excise it from the mind.
- **The religious framing moralizes cognitive error.** Calling a bias
  an "idol" makes it quasi-sinful -- something to be ashamed of and
  repented. But cognitive biases are not moral failings. Framing them
  as idolatry encourages guilt where it should encourage design (of
  better institutions, better methods, better checks).
- **Bacon himself was not immune.** He rejected Copernican
  heliocentrism, preferring the geocentric model partly on traditional
  grounds -- a textbook case of his own tribal idols (status quo bias,
  authority bias) operating on the man who named them. Awareness of
  bias does not prevent bias, a fact the idol metaphor obscures by
  implying that naming the false god is sufficient to stop worshipping
  it.
- **The category is too broad.** "Biases inherent in human nature"
  encompasses everything from optical illusions to confirmation bias
  to tribalism. Modern cognitive science has identified hundreds of
  distinct biases with different mechanisms, different contexts, and
  different interventions. Lumping them all into one idol-category
  obscures these differences.

## Expressions

- "Cognitive bias" -- the modern successor term, stripped of the
  religious framing but covering the same territory
- "Confirmation bias" -- the specific tribal idol of seeking evidence
  that supports existing beliefs, identified by Bacon in Aphorism XLVI
  three centuries before Wason's experiments
- "The availability heuristic" -- overweighting vivid or recent
  information, another instance of the false mirror distorting
  perception
- "Anchoring" -- the mind's tendency to fixate on initial information,
  a tribal idol that operates regardless of expertise or training
- "Apophenia" -- perceiving meaningful patterns in random data, the
  ordering impulse Bacon described as seeing "more order than it
  actually finds"
- "Motivated reasoning" -- the false mirror at work: the mind shapes
  evidence to fit conclusions rather than the reverse
- "Debiasing" -- the modern methodological project of correcting for
  tribal idols, pursued through statistical controls, blinding, and
  pre-registration

## Origin Story

The Four Idols are among the earliest systematic attempts to taxonomize
cognitive error. Bacon organized them by scope: the Tribe (universal
human), the Cave (individual), the Marketplace (social-linguistic),
and the Theatre (institutional-philosophical). The tribal idols are
first because Bacon considers them most fundamental -- they are the
errors you cannot escape by changing your education, your language,
or your intellectual tradition, because they come with the species.

The connection to modern cognitive bias research is direct. Kahneman
and Tversky's heuristics-and-biases program (1970s onward) is
effectively an empirical investigation of Bacon's tribal idols,
replacing Bacon's literary descriptions with experimental
demonstrations. Kahneman himself acknowledged the ancestry.
