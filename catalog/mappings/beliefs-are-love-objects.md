## What It Brings

People fall in love with beliefs, become devoted to them, are
unfaithful to them, and sometimes break up with them painfully. This
metaphor maps the emotional dynamics of romantic love -- attraction,
devotion, jealousy, infidelity, heartbreak -- onto the domain of
epistemic commitment, treating the relationship between a person and
their convictions as a love affair. The Master Metaphor List (Lakoff,
Espenson & Schwartz 1991) catalogs it as part of the belief metaphor
cluster.

Key structural parallels:

- **Attraction and falling** -- "I was immediately drawn to the idea."
  "He fell for that philosophy." "She was seduced by the argument."
  The initial adoption of a belief is romantic attraction: something
  about the belief is compelling, alluring, hard to resist. The
  metaphor implies that belief adoption is not purely rational but
  involves a kind of captivation.
- **Devotion and commitment** -- "She is devoted to her principles."
  "He is wedded to that theory." "They are committed to the cause."
  Sustained belief is the equivalent of romantic commitment: loyalty,
  fidelity, the decision to stay with one belief rather than wander
  to another. The metaphor makes believing feel like a relationship
  that requires ongoing investment.
- **Infidelity and betrayal** -- "He flirted with Marxism for a
  while." "She was unfaithful to her principles." "He betrayed his
  beliefs." The metaphor constructs belief change as romantic
  infidelity: to consider alternative beliefs is to have a wandering
  eye, and to adopt them is to cheat on the beliefs you were
  committed to.
- **Heartbreak and loss** -- "He was heartbroken when he lost his
  faith." "She couldn't let go of that belief." "Abandoning that
  ideology was like losing a lover." The end of a belief is a
  breakup. The metaphor captures the genuine emotional devastation
  that accompanies loss of faith, loss of political conviction, or
  the collapse of a worldview one was deeply invested in.
- **Jealousy and possessiveness** -- "He guards his beliefs
  jealously." "She's possessive about her ideology -- she can't
  stand anyone criticizing it." The metaphor maps the possessiveness
  of romantic love onto epistemic commitment: beliefs are mine, and
  criticism of them feels like a threat to an intimate relationship.

## Where It Breaks

- **Beliefs are not reciprocal** -- love relationships involve two
  agents with mutual feelings. But beliefs do not love you back.
  The love-object metaphor treats a one-directional relationship
  (person to proposition) as though it were bidirectional, which
  can inflate the emotional significance of conviction beyond what
  is warranted. Your belief does not care whether you are faithful
  to it.
- **The metaphor pathologizes open-mindedness** -- if belief is like
  romantic commitment, then entertaining alternatives is infidelity
  and changing your mind is betrayal. The metaphor makes intellectual
  flexibility look morally suspect, when in fact openness to
  evidence and willingness to revise one's beliefs are epistemic
  virtues.
- **Attraction is not evidence** -- the metaphor maps the
  compelling quality of romantic attraction onto the persuasiveness
  of beliefs. But being drawn to a belief, finding it seductive, or
  falling for an argument are not the same as finding it well
  supported by evidence. The love-object metaphor conflates
  emotional appeal with epistemic merit.
- **The metaphor makes belief change unnecessarily traumatic** --
  by framing belief revision as heartbreak, the metaphor raises
  the emotional cost of changing one's mind. This can make people
  cling to false beliefs because abandoning them feels like losing
  a relationship, not like updating a model of the world.
- **The metaphor is asymmetric across belief types** -- it works
  well for deeply held convictions (religious faith, political
  ideology, moral principles) but poorly for mundane beliefs
  ("I believe it will rain tomorrow"). Nobody is devoted to their
  weather predictions. The love-object metaphor only applies to
  beliefs that are identity-constituting, which limits its scope
  while implying universality.

## Expressions

- "He's wedded to that theory" -- deep commitment to a belief as
  marriage
- "She was seduced by the argument" -- finding a belief compelling
  as romantic seduction
- "He fell for that philosophy" -- adopting a belief as falling in
  love
- "She is devoted to her principles" -- sustained conviction as
  romantic devotion
- "He flirted with Marxism" -- briefly considering a belief as
  romantic flirtation
- "She was unfaithful to her principles" -- acting against one's
  beliefs as romantic infidelity
- "He was heartbroken when he lost his faith" -- loss of belief as
  romantic loss
- "She can't let go of that belief" -- difficulty abandoning a
  conviction as difficulty ending a relationship
- "He guards his beliefs jealously" -- defending beliefs against
  criticism as romantic jealousy
- "They have an intimate relationship with the truth" -- deep
  epistemic engagement as romantic intimacy

## Origin Story

The Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson & Schwartz 1991) records
BELIEFS ARE LOVE OBJECTS as the most emotionally charged of the
belief metaphor cluster: BELIEFS ARE POSSESSIONS, BELIEFS ARE
LOCATIONS, BELIEFS ARE GUIDES, BELIEFS ARE FASHIONS, BELIEFS ARE
BEINGS WITH A LIFE CYCLE, and BELIEFS ARE LOVE OBJECTS. While the
other metaphors in the cluster emphasize cognitive or social aspects
of belief (ownership, navigation, fashion), the love-object metaphor
foregrounds the emotional dimension of epistemic commitment -- the
fact that people care deeply about what they believe, sometimes more
than they care about whether it is true.

The metaphor helps explain phenomena that purely rationalist accounts
of belief cannot: why people get angry when their beliefs are
challenged, why losing one's faith can feel like bereavement, and
why belief change is often resisted long past the point where the
evidence warrants it. The love-object framing makes these phenomena
intelligible by mapping them onto the familiar emotional dynamics of
romantic attachment.
