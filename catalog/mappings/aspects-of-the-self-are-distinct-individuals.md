## What It Brings

The single self is a society. When we experience internal conflict, we
conceptualize it as a disagreement between distinct people inhabiting the
same body. One part of you wants to stay in bed; another part knows you
should get up. Your "better self" disapproves of your "worst impulses."
The metaphor takes the unity of consciousness and fractures it into a
cast of characters, each with their own desires, voices, and moral
standing.

Key structural parallels:

- **Internal conflict as interpersonal conflict** -- "Part of me wants to
  go, but another part of me resists." The metaphor converts a single
  person's ambivalence into a dialogue (or argument) between two or more
  agents. This makes inner conflict legible by borrowing the structure of
  social disagreement, which we already know how to narrate.
- **Moral hierarchy among selves** -- not all inner agents are equal. The
  "higher self" outranks the "lower self." Your "conscience" is a stern
  authority figure while your "desires" are unruly subordinates. The
  metaphor imports social hierarchy into the psyche, so that self-control
  becomes a matter of the right person being in charge.
- **Conversation and persuasion** -- "I told myself to calm down." "I had
  to convince myself it was worth it." "I argued with myself for hours."
  Internal deliberation is rendered as verbal exchange between people.
  This makes it possible to talk about reasoning as something that
  happens *between* agents rather than *within* a single mind.
- **Betrayal and alliance** -- "I let myself down." "I was my own worst
  enemy." "I need to be true to myself." The social vocabulary of loyalty
  and betrayal maps onto the relationship between the "real" self and its
  wayward aspects. Failing to follow through on a resolution is a form
  of self-betrayal, as though a promise was broken between two people.
- **Possession and dispossession** -- "I wasn't myself." "I lost control
  of myself." "Something came over me." In extreme cases, the metaphor
  shifts from internal plurality to external takeover -- the self is
  displaced by an alien agent. This extension borders on the metaphor of
  possession by spirits, which may be its historical ancestor.

## Where It Breaks

- **The self is not actually plural** -- there is no homunculus making
  decisions inside your head, much less two of them arguing. The metaphor
  reifies phenomenological complexity into a folk model of multiple agents
  that has no neurological basis. While it helps people narrate inner
  experience, it can also lead to the false belief that the psyche
  contains discrete, stable sub-personalities.
- **Who is the "real" self?** -- the metaphor requires one aspect to be
  more authentically "you" than the others, but it provides no principled
  way to decide which one. Is the real self the disciplined one or the
  spontaneous one? The generous one or the selfish one? The metaphor
  imports the problem of authenticity without solving it, and different
  cultural contexts will nominate different candidates for the "true" self.
- **The metaphor can pathologize normal ambivalence** -- framing mixed
  feelings as a war between internal people makes ordinary indecision
  sound like a disorder. "I'm of two minds" is normal human complexity,
  but the metaphor's logic pushes toward resolution: one self must win,
  the other must be suppressed. This framing is hostile to the
  psychological reality that holding contradictory feelings simultaneously
  is often healthy.
- **The social model oversimplifies causation** -- in actual interpersonal
  conflict, each party has their own independent history and motivations.
  But the "parts" of the self are not independent; they share a single
  brain, a single body, a single history. The metaphor's separation is
  too clean -- it hides the fact that the conflicting impulses emerge from
  the same integrated system.

## Expressions

- "Part of me wants to stay" -- the self divided into sub-agents with
  competing desires
- "I told myself to stop worrying" -- internal deliberation as one person
  instructing another
- "My better self prevailed" -- moral conflict as a contest between ranked
  internal persons
- "I was at war with myself" -- internal conflict borrowing the full
  apparatus of the war metaphor
- "I need to get in touch with myself" -- aspects of self as separate
  persons requiring social contact
- "I wasn't myself yesterday" -- aberrant behavior as temporary replacement
  of the true self
- "She's her own worst enemy" -- self-sabotage as interpersonal hostility
  directed inward
- "I had to convince myself" -- self-persuasion as persuasion of another
  person
- "My conscience wouldn't let me" -- the moral faculty as an authoritative
  person who grants or withholds permission

## Origin Story

The metaphor appears in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson &
Schwartz 1991) under the "Self" section. Lakoff discusses it more
extensively in *Philosophy in the Flesh* (1999), where he identifies
several variants: the Subject-Self metaphor (a subject experiences a self
as a distinct entity), the divided self, and the self as a society of
inner persons. The philosophical lineage is much older -- Plato's tripartite
soul (reason, spirit, appetite) in the *Republic* is an early systematic
deployment of the same mapping. Freud's id, ego, and superego formalized
it as psychology. The metaphor's persistence across millennia of Western
thought suggests it is grounded in a universal feature of human
self-awareness: the phenomenological experience of inner conflict.
