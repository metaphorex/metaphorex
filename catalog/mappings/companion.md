## What It Brings

Latin com + panis -- "with bread." A companion is literally someone you
break bread with. The word encodes a specific theory of social bonding:
that relationships are forged through the shared, bodily act of eating
together. Not through shared beliefs, shared blood, or shared geography,
but through shared sustenance. The same root gives us "company,"
"accompany," and the French copain (buddy) -- an entire vocabulary of
togetherness built on a loaf of bread.

- **Shared sustenance as social glue** -- the core structural import
  is that eating together constitutes a relationship. The metaphor
  does not merely describe a social activity; it defines the
  relationship by the activity. A companion is not someone you happen
  to eat with; a companion is someone whose status as your companion
  is constituted by eating with you. The bread is not incidental to
  the bond -- it is the bond. This maps a material, biological
  necessity (feeding) onto an abstract social category (friendship,
  alliance, fellowship).
- **Proximity and duration** -- bread is not a snack you grab in
  passing. In the ancient Mediterranean, breaking bread implied
  sitting down, staying, sharing time. The metaphor imports temporal
  and spatial requirements for companionship: you must be present,
  you must remain, you must share something essential. This structural
  mapping survived the death of the metaphor -- we still intuit that
  true companionship requires sustained co-presence, not just
  occasional contact.
- **Reciprocity and equality** -- breaking bread is bilateral. Both
  parties eat. The metaphor imports a symmetry that other relationship
  terms lack. A "follower" is asymmetric. A "subject" is hierarchical.
  A "companion" is someone who shares the same bread at the same
  table -- the food metaphor encodes equality as a structural feature
  of the relationship. This may explain why "companion" feels warmer
  than "associate" or "colleague," which lack the embodied reciprocity.

## Where It Breaks

- **Modern companionship requires no bread** -- the metaphor died
  because the literal requirement evaporated. You can be someone's
  companion without ever sharing a meal. Digital companions, travel
  companions, companion animals -- none of these involve bread. The
  metaphor's death reveals that we kept the social structure
  (sustained co-presence, shared experience) while discarding the
  specific material substrate (food). The bread was the scaffolding;
  the social bond was the building.
- **The metaphor smuggles in exclusion** -- if companionship is
  constituted by breaking bread together, then those who cannot share
  your table are not your companions. Dietary laws, class-based
  dining, caste restrictions on commensality -- the bread metaphor
  maps all of these onto social boundaries. "Companion" sounds
  inclusive, but its etymology encodes the most ancient form of
  social gatekeeping: who you will and will not eat with. The dead
  metaphor hides this exclusionary structure under a warm word.
- **"Companion" has been commercialized beyond recognition** --
  "companion planting" (agriculture), "companion piece" (art),
  "companion app" (software), "companion volume" (publishing). In
  none of these does the bread metaphor apply. The word has been
  stretched so far from its source that it now means little more
  than "thing that goes alongside another thing." The social
  richness of the original -- the shared meal, the reciprocity,
  the implied equality -- is absent from these technical usages.
  The metaphor did not just die; it was hollowed out.
- **The resurrection is uncomfortable** -- if you take the etymology
  seriously, "companion animal" means "an animal you break bread
  with." This is actually closer to the truth than the dead metaphor
  intends: people do share meals with their pets, and the act of
  feeding is central to the human-animal bond. But "companion care"
  (eldercare) becomes "bread-sharing care," which misrepresents
  the relationship. The dead metaphor works better in some modern
  contexts than others, which is unusual -- most dead metaphors
  either map well everywhere or nowhere.

## Expressions

- "Companion" -- the base term, now meaning any close associate,
  with the bread origin entirely invisible
- "Company" -- from the same root, now meaning a group of people
  or a business entity, three full metaphorical deaths from the
  original bread-sharing
- "Accompany" -- to go with someone, where "with bread" has become
  simply "with"
- "Boon companion" -- a close drinking and eating partner, where
  "boon" (from French bon) reinforces the conviviality that the
  base word originally encoded
- "Companion animal" -- a pet, where the bread metaphor accidentally
  captures the centrality of feeding in human-animal bonds
- "Keep someone company" -- to be present with someone, where the
  bread has been replaced by mere presence

## Origin Story

The Latin companio (genitive companionis) appears in the Salic law of
the Franks (6th century CE) and is generally understood as a calque of
the Gothic gahlaiba, meaning "with-bread" or "messmate." The parallel
construction in two unrelated language families suggests the concept
was culturally widespread in early medieval Europe: the person you eat
bread with is the person you are bonded to.

The word entered English via Old French compaignon in the 13th century,
already losing its food connotation. By Chaucer's time (14th century),
"companion" meant a fellow traveler or associate with no necessary
reference to meals. The Canterbury Tales pilgrims are "compaignye" --
traveling companions who do share meals, but the word has already
shifted to emphasize the journey over the bread.

The commercial extension ("company" as a business entity) emerged in
the 16th and 17th centuries, mapping the bread-fellowship onto
commercial partnership. The East India Company was, etymologically, a
bread-sharing group. By the 18th century, "company" could mean a
military unit, a theatrical troupe, or a trading firm -- each further
from the table.

The deepest layer of the dead metaphor is religious. "Breaking bread"
remains a live metaphor in Christian liturgy, where the Eucharist
enacts precisely what "companion" once literally meant: communion (com
+ munis, shared duty) through shared bread. The sacred version
preserved what the secular version forgot.
