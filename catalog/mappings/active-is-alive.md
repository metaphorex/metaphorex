## What It Brings

Activity is life; inactivity is death. We understand whether something is
functioning, operating, or participating by mapping it onto the most
fundamental biological distinction we know -- alive versus dead. An active
process is a living thing. A stopped process is a dead one.

Key structural parallels:

- **Activity as vitality** -- something that is working, changing, or
  producing effects is "alive." A live wire carries current. A live issue
  demands attention. A living language has speakers. The mapping equates
  doing with being, so that anything which acts must, in some sense, live.
- **Inactivity as death** -- when something ceases to function, we say it
  "died." A dead battery produces no current. A dead letter reaches no
  recipient. A dead end leads nowhere. The cessation of activity is not
  merely a pause but a termination of existence.
- **Resuscitation as reactivation** -- bringing something back into
  operation is "reviving" or "resurrecting" it. You revive a stalled
  project, breathe life into a proposal, resurrect an abandoned plan. The
  metaphor implies that restarting something requires the same kind of
  dramatic intervention as restoring biological life.
- **Dormancy as sleep** -- the metaphor has a middle state between alive
  and dead. A dormant volcano, a sleeping process, a hibernating account --
  these are inactive but not dead, preserving the possibility of future
  activity without current vitality.

The metaphor is so deeply embedded that we rarely notice it. Calling a
city "vibrant" or a market "dead" feels like literal description rather
than metaphorical mapping. This invisibility is its power: we genuinely
think of activity as a form of life.

## Where It Breaks

- **Dead things do not restart; inactive things do** -- the metaphor
  borrows finality from biological death, but most inactive systems can be
  restarted trivially. A "dead" server can be rebooted. A "dead" project
  can be refunded. The metaphor overstates the difficulty of reactivation
  by importing the irreversibility of death.
- **Life implies agency; activity does not** -- a living thing acts on its
  own behalf, but many active systems have no agency at all. A spinning
  hard drive is "alive" only in the metaphorical sense. The mapping can
  lead to animist reasoning, attributing intention to purely mechanical
  processes.
- **The metaphor obscures useful inactivity** -- biological death is bad
  for the organism, so the metaphor codes all inactivity as negative. But
  many systems benefit from being inactive: fallow fields, cooling-off
  periods, paused negotiations. The alive/dead binary has no room for
  deliberate, productive stillness.
- **Gradations collapse** -- biological life is largely binary (with edge
  cases), but activity exists on a continuous spectrum. A company with
  declining revenue is not "dying" in any precise sense, yet the metaphor
  pushes us toward that dramatic framing, triggering panic responses
  appropriate to mortal threats.

## Expressions

- "That's a dead issue" -- an inactive topic treated as biologically
  terminated
- "The project is alive and well" -- continued activity as health and
  vitality
- "Let's breathe some life into this proposal" -- reactivation as
  resuscitation
- "A living wage" -- a wage sufficient to sustain activity (life)
- "Dead code" -- software instructions that never execute, named as
  though they have died
- "The party really came alive" -- increased activity as the onset of
  life
- "A lively debate" -- vigorous exchange as biological vitality
- "That tradition is dead" -- a ceased practice as an organism that has
  expired

## Origin Story

ACTIVE IS ALIVE appears in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson &
Schwartz 1991) as one of the general mappings that structure how we talk
about states and processes. It is grounded in the primary correlation
between animacy and movement that infants detect before they can speak:
things that move on their own are alive, things that are still are not.
This perceptual correlation, documented in developmental psychology, makes
the metaphor feel so natural that it barely registers as figurative.
