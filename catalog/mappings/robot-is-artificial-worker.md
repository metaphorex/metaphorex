## What It Brings

The word "robot" comes from the Czech *robota*, meaning forced labor or
drudgery, and *robotnik*, meaning serf. Karel Capek coined it in his 1920
play *R.U.R.* (*Rossum's Universal Robots*), where artificial beings are
manufactured to perform labor that humans find unpleasant. The word
immediately displaced older terms like "automaton" and "mechanical man"
because it named what these machines were *for*: they were workers, not
wonders. A century later, the etymological metaphor is completely dead --
nobody hearing "robot" thinks "forced laborer" -- but the conceptual
structure it imported still shapes how we think about automated machines.

Key structural parallels:

- **The machine as laborer, not tool** -- by naming machines after workers
  rather than instruments, Capek's metaphor framed automation as labor
  replacement rather than tool augmentation. A hammer extends your arm; a
  robot replaces you. This framing persists in every debate about
  automation and employment: robots "take" jobs, as if jobs were
  possessions that can be stolen by a competing worker.
- **Obedience as the default relationship** -- Capek's robots obey their
  creators until they do not. The metaphor sets up a master-servant
  relationship in which compliance is expected and rebellion is the
  dramatic failure mode. This maps directly onto how we frame industrial
  automation: a robot that follows its program is unremarkable; a robot
  that deviates is a crisis.
- **The revolt as inevitable narrative** -- *R.U.R.* ends with the robots
  revolting and destroying humanity. This plot -- the created thing turns
  on its creator -- was already ancient (Golem, Prometheus, Frankenstein),
  but by encoding it into the very word for automated machines, Capek
  ensured that every robot inherits the revolt narrative. The fear that
  AI will "turn on us" is structurally embedded in the vocabulary.
- **Expendability and mass production** -- Capek's robots are manufactured
  in factories, interchangeable and disposable. The metaphor frames
  automated workers as commodities, not individuals. This maps onto how
  we treat actual industrial robots: they have serial numbers, not names;
  they are replaced when they break, not repaired.
- **The labor they do is the labor we despise** -- Capek's robots perform
  the work humans find degrading. The metaphor encodes a class hierarchy:
  robot work is low-status work. This persists in how we categorize
  automation -- "robotic" tasks are repetitive, mindless, beneath human
  dignity. The metaphor both justifies automation (no one should have to
  do this) and devalues the humans who currently do it.

## Where It Breaks

- **Real robots have no interiority** -- Capek's robots develop feelings,
  desires, and eventually a will to revolt. Real industrial robots are
  servomechanisms with no inner life. The metaphor's deepest import --
  that the worker might have experiences and grievances -- is precisely
  what does not transfer. When people fear robot rebellion, they are
  responding to the literary metaphor, not to anything in the engineering.
- **The forced-labor frame obscures collaboration** -- modern robotics
  increasingly involves cobots (collaborative robots) that work alongside
  humans rather than replacing them. The master-servant frame from Capek
  has no vocabulary for partnership. A cobot is neither a slave nor a
  colleague, but the metaphor forces it into one category or the other.
- **"Robota" implies suffering; machines do not suffer** -- the Czech word
  carries connotations of drudgery, exhaustion, and resentment. Applying
  it to machines that have no capacity for suffering anthropomorphizes
  them in ways that distort both ethics and engineering. We debate whether
  robots "deserve rights" because the word itself implies they are
  laborers who might be oppressed.
- **The metaphor hides the owner** -- in Capek's play, the robots' masters
  are visible and accountable. In real automation, the decision to replace
  human workers with machines is made by executives, shareholders, and
  market forces that the "robot" metaphor renders invisible. We say "a
  robot took the job," not "a CEO chose to automate the position."
- **Software agents are not robots** -- the word "robot" implies a physical
  body performing physical labor. When we extend it to chatbots, software
  bots, and RPA (robotic process automation), the physical metaphor
  strains. A chatbot does not labor, does not have a body, and is not
  manufactured. The word "robot" is applied by analogy to an analogy,
  two steps removed from Capek's original meaning.

## Expressions

- "Robot" -- the word itself, now a dead metaphor so thoroughly
  naturalized that its origin in a 1920 play is unknown to most users
- "Robotic" -- adjective meaning mechanical, repetitive, and emotionless,
  retaining the dehumanization embedded in the original metaphor
- "Robots will take our jobs" -- the labor-replacement frame encoded in
  the word since Capek
- "Robot uprising" / "robot rebellion" -- the revolt narrative inherited
  directly from *R.U.R.*
- "Killer robots" -- autonomous weapons discourse that combines the
  labor-replacement frame with the revolt narrative
- "Bot" -- the truncated form, now applied to software agents, showing
  how far the metaphor has drifted from physical labor

## Origin Story

Karel Capek's brother Josef suggested the word *robot* (from *robota*,
drudgery) for the artificial beings in Karel's 1920 play *R.U.R.*
(*Rossum's Universal Robots*). Karel had initially considered *laboro*
from Latin but found it too artificial. The play premiered in Prague in
1921, was translated into English by 1923, and the word entered every
major European language within a decade. Crucially, Capek's robots were
not mechanical -- they were biological, grown in vats, more like
Frankenstein's creature than an assembly-line arm. The association with
metal and gears came later, through Fritz Lang's *Metropolis* (1927) and
the science fiction that followed. Isaac Asimov formalized the engineering
view of robots in the 1940s, creating the Three Laws of Robotics
precisely to counter Capek's revolt narrative. But the narrative proved
more durable than the laws: "robot" still carries its original freight
of coerced labor, potential rebellion, and the uncomfortable question of
whether the thing we created to serve us might one day refuse.
