## What It Brings

What comes first causes what comes after. This metaphor maps the structure
of temporal sequence -- earlier and later, before and after -- onto the
structure of causal reasoning. It is one of the most fundamental and
invisible metaphors in the Event Structure system because it feels less
like a metaphor and more like a logical truth. But Lakoff and Johnson
argue that the identification of temporal priority with causal priority
is a metaphorical mapping, not a necessary relation: correlation in time
is being used to structure our understanding of causation.

Key structural parallels:

- **Earlier events as causes** -- "The recession followed the banking
  crisis." "War came after the assassination." "First the leak, then the
  flood." The temporal order of events is read as causal order. What
  happened first is treated as the reason for what happened next. This
  mapping is so pervasive that the Latin phrase *post hoc ergo propter
  hoc* (after this, therefore because of this) was named as a formal
  fallacy precisely because the mapping is cognitively irresistible.
- **Later events as effects** -- "That led to disaster." "The consequence
  followed swiftly." "The aftermath was devastating." Effects are things
  that come after. The metaphor gives causal reasoning a directional
  arrow that mirrors the arrow of time: causes point forward, effects
  look backward.
- **Causal chains as timelines** -- "The sequence of events that led to
  the collapse." "Trace it back to the beginning." "One thing after
  another." Causal explanation takes the form of narrative: a temporal
  ordering of events where each step follows (temporally and causally)
  from the last. This is why causal explanation and storytelling share
  the same structure.
- **Immediacy as directness** -- "The blow immediately caused him to
  fall." "Right after the announcement, the market crashed." When
  temporal proximity is tight, the causal connection feels stronger.
  Distant causes (the policy enacted ten years ago that led to today's
  crisis) feel less causally potent than proximate ones, even when they
  are more important.
- **Precedence as priority** -- "The underlying cause." "The root of the
  problem." "It goes back to the original decision." The earliest event
  in the temporal chain is treated as the most important cause. Temporal
  primacy maps onto causal primacy, which is why origin stories feel like
  explanations.

## Where It Breaks

- **Temporal precedence is not sufficient for causation** -- the rooster
  crows before dawn but does not cause it. The metaphor makes *post hoc*
  reasoning feel natural because it equates "came before" with "brought
  about." Every superstition exploits this mapping: the ritual preceded
  the success, so the ritual caused it.
- **Simultaneous causation is invisible** -- the metaphor requires a
  before and after. But many causal relationships are synchronic: the
  gravitational field of the earth simultaneously causes the apple to
  fall. Supply and demand simultaneously determine price. The temporal
  precedence mapping has no way to represent causes that operate at the
  same time as their effects.
- **Backward causation and feedback loops break the arrow** -- in systems
  with feedback, effects modify their own causes. The thermostat's output
  (heat) changes its input (temperature reading), which changes its
  output. The temporal precedence metaphor cannot represent circular
  causation because time does not run in circles. This is a deep
  limitation for understanding any self-regulating system.
- **The metaphor privileges narrative over structure** -- because causal
  reasoning is mapped onto temporal sequence, explanation takes the form
  of story ("first this happened, then that happened"). But many of the
  most important causal explanations are structural, not narrative: the
  bridge collapsed because of its geometry, not because of a sequence of
  events. The temporal mapping makes structural causation hard to think
  about.
- **Distant causes feel weak** -- the further back in time a cause is,
  the less causally potent it feels under this metaphor. But temporal
  distance and causal importance are unrelated. A constitutional decision
  made two centuries ago may be the most important cause of a present
  crisis, yet the metaphor makes last week's triggering event feel like
  the "real" cause.

## Expressions

- "One thing led to another" -- temporal sequence as causal chain
- "In the wake of the disaster" -- later events following earlier ones
  like a ship's wake
- "The events leading up to the war" -- temporal predecessors as causal
  contributors
- "That was the first domino to fall" -- temporal priority as causal
  initiation
- "Trace it back to the source" -- finding the earliest cause by
  reversing the timeline
- "The root cause" -- the temporally first cause as the foundational one
- "What followed was chaos" -- temporal succession as causal consequence
- "It all started when..." -- narrative origin as causal origin
- "The chain of events" -- temporal sequence as linked causal sequence
- "After that, everything changed" -- temporal boundary as causal
  turning point

## Origin Story

The mapping of temporal precedence onto causal precedence is documented
in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson, and Schwartz, 1991) as
part of the broader Event Structure metaphor system. The metaphor was
implicitly recognized long before cognitive linguistics gave it a name:
David Hume's analysis of causation in *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739)
identified temporal priority as one of the three elements of the causal
impression (along with contiguity and constant conjunction). What Lakoff
and Johnson add is the claim that this identification is not a discovery
about the nature of causation but a metaphorical mapping -- we understand
causation through time because embodied experience teaches us that pushes
precede movements and actions precede results.

The metaphor is especially important in legal and historical reasoning,
where establishing a timeline is treated as establishing causation. The
legal concept of *proximate cause* explicitly relies on temporal-causal
mapping: the cause closest in time to the harm is typically assigned
responsibility, even when more distant causes may be more important.
