## What It Brings

A double etymology converging on a single internet behavior. The word
"troll" in online usage derives from a fishing technique -- trolling,
meaning to drag bait through water hoping something bites. But the
Scandinavian folklore creature -- a menacing figure lurking under bridges
where travelers must pass -- has colonized the popular imagination so
thoroughly that almost nobody now thinks of the fishing origin.

- **Fishing as provocation** -- the original internet meaning was precise:
  trolling a forum meant posting provocative bait (a deliberately wrong
  statement, a loaded question, a bad-faith argument) and waiting for
  someone to bite. The fisherman knows the bait is artificial. The fish
  does not. The structural mapping is tight: the troll knows the
  provocation is insincere; the respondent does not.
- **The monster under the bridge** -- the folklore creature maps
  differently but powerfully. A troll lurks where people must pass (a
  forum, a comment section, a social media platform), demanding a toll
  (attention, emotional energy, engagement) from anyone who wants to
  cross. The bridge troll is territorial, ugly, and unavoidable. This
  mapping captures the *experience* of encountering a troll better than
  the fishing metaphor does.
- **Etymology collapsed into caricature** -- modern usage has fully
  merged the fishing and folklore meanings. A "troll" is now simply a
  person who behaves badly online. The fishing technique's structural
  insight (deliberate, strategic baiting) has been lost; the word now
  covers everything from sophisticated political disinformation
  operatives to teenagers posting rude comments. The metaphor died by
  becoming too useful.

## Where It Breaks

- **The fishing metaphor requires intent; modern usage doesn't** -- a
  fisherman trolling is engaged in a deliberate, skilled activity.
  The bait is chosen carefully, the line is managed, the goal is
  specific. Much of what gets called "trolling" today has no strategic
  intent at all -- it is impulsive hostility, not calculated provocation.
  The metaphor's structural core (deliberate deception for a reaction)
  has been diluted into a general term for online unpleasantness.
- **The bridge troll can be avoided; the internet troll cannot** -- in
  folklore, you can choose another path, or you can answer the troll's
  riddle and pass. Online, trolls are distributed across every platform,
  every comment section, every reply chain. There is no alternate bridge.
  "Don't feed the trolls" is advice derived from the fishing metaphor
  (don't take the bait), but it fails in practice because platforms are
  algorithmically designed to surface engagement, and troll content
  generates engagement.
- **The folklore troll is solitary; internet trolls are networked** --
  mythological trolls are isolated creatures: one bridge, one troll.
  Internet trolls operate in coordinated groups, troll farms, brigades,
  and bot networks. The solitary-creature metaphor cannot account for
  organized trolling campaigns, state-sponsored disinformation, or
  coordinated harassment. The word "troll" makes institutional
  manipulation sound like individual mischief.
- **"Don't feed the trolls" misattributes the problem** -- this advice,
  derived from the fishing metaphor (if no fish bites, the fisherman
  moves on), assumes that trolling is economically rational: it will
  stop when it becomes unrewarding. But much trolling is intrinsically
  motivated (the troll enjoys the act itself) or externally funded (troll
  farms are paid per post, not per response). The fishing metaphor
  suggests a solution that addresses neither motivation.

## Expressions

- "Don't feed the trolls" -- the canonical advice, fishing-derived,
  assuming trolling stops when unrewarded
- "Trolling" (verb) -- posting provocative content to elicit reactions,
  now often used without awareness of the fishing origin
- "Troll farm" -- an organized operation producing trolling content at
  scale, where "farm" adds yet another agricultural metaphor layer
- "Concern troll" -- someone who feigns sympathy or concern while
  actually undermining the position they claim to support, a subspecies
  that preserves the fishing metaphor's emphasis on deceptive intent
- "Patent troll" -- a company that acquires patents not to produce
  anything but to extract licensing fees through litigation, where the
  bridge-troll mapping (demanding a toll from those who must pass) is
  structurally precise
- "Under the bridge" -- a reference to the troll's habitat in folklore,
  used to describe the hidden, anonymous spaces where trolling thrives

## Origin Story

The fishing verb "to troll" (or "to trawl," from the French *troller*,
to wander or to drag) described the technique of slowly dragging a baited
line through water from a moving boat. The technique is ancient; the word
entered English by the 15th century.

The Scandinavian folklore creature -- *troll* in Old Norse -- is a
separate word entirely, referring to a class of supernatural beings
ranging from enormous, stupid mountain dwellers to small, cunning
forest creatures. The bridge troll, demanding a toll from travelers,
is the subspecies that entered international popular culture, primarily
through the fairy tale "Three Billy Goats Gruff."

Internet trolling emerged in the early 1990s on Usenet. The earliest
documented uses (circa 1992) clearly reference the fishing metaphor:
"trolling for newbies" meant posting deliberately wrong information to
see who would take the bait. The fishing etymology was explicit and
widely understood in early internet culture.

By the 2000s, the folklore meaning had overtaken the fishing meaning.
The word "troll" increasingly referred to the person (the monster) rather
than the activity (the fishing technique). Image boards and forums
developed troll iconography based on the folklore creature: ugly, green,
club-wielding. The fishing origin became obscure. By the 2010s, "troll"
had entered mainstream journalism as a generic term for anyone who
behaves badly online, and the metaphor was fully dead.
