## What It Brings

A pipeline is a physical pipe -- steel or concrete -- that carries oil,
water, or gas from a source to a destination. The first long-distance
oil pipeline was built in Pennsylvania in 1879. The word migrated from
infrastructure engineering to any system where things flow sequentially
through stages: sales pipelines, talent pipelines, CI/CD pipelines,
legislative pipelines. The metaphor is so dead in business and software
contexts that most users have no awareness of the physical pipe.

- **Unidirectional flow through stages** -- physical pipelines enforce
  direction. Oil enters at the wellhead and exits at the refinery. The
  metaphor imports the assumption that whatever is being processed moves
  one way through a fixed sequence of stages. "Sales pipeline" frames
  potential deals as flowing from lead to prospect to negotiation to
  close. The directionality feels natural because the dead metaphor
  supplies the physics: things flow downhill through pipes, and so
  deals flow forward through stages.
- **Capacity is finite and measurable** -- a physical pipeline has a
  diameter and a flow rate. The metaphor imports the idea that any
  sequential process has a measurable throughput. "How full is your
  pipeline?" is a standard business question that makes sense only
  because the dead metaphor supplies the image of a pipe that can be
  more or less full. Pipeline "capacity" and pipeline "velocity" are
  fluid-dynamics terms applied to abstract processes.
- **What enters must eventually exit** -- a physical pipeline does not
  consume its contents. Everything that goes in comes out, possibly
  transformed (crude oil in, refined products out). The metaphor
  imports the expectation of complete throughput: if a lead enters
  the sales pipeline, it should eventually exit as either a closed
  deal or a documented loss. The pipe metaphor makes "leakage" from
  the pipeline feel like a failure -- something that should not happen.

## Where It Breaks

- **Real pipelines leak, and everyone knows it** -- physical pipelines
  corrode, crack at joints, and suffer catastrophic failures. The
  metaphor hides this: a "leaky pipeline" in business is treated as
  a problem to be fixed, but in physical infrastructure, some leakage
  is engineered tolerance. The dead metaphor imports the idealized
  pipe (zero leakage) rather than the real pipe (managed leakage),
  creating unrealistic expectations for process throughput. When
  organizations report "pipeline leakage" in hiring or sales, they
  treat normal attrition as a defect rather than a feature of any
  system involving human decision-making.
- **Pipelines carry homogeneous fluid; business carries heterogeneous
  items** -- oil in a pipeline is uniform. Every barrel is
  interchangeable. But deals in a sales pipeline, candidates in a
  hiring pipeline, and features in a development pipeline are all
  unique. Treating them as interchangeable units flowing through
  standard stages obscures the fact that each item may need different
  handling. The dead metaphor encourages standardization of process
  at the expense of recognizing individual variation.
- **The pipeline metaphor hides the human at each stage** -- a
  physical pipeline is automated: pumps and gravity move the fluid.
  But a "talent pipeline" or "legislative pipeline" has a human
  gatekeeper at every stage. The dead metaphor naturalizes sequential
  processing and obscures the fact that each stage involves judgment,
  discretion, and potential bias. "She's in the pipeline" treats a
  person as fluid moving through infrastructure, erasing her agency
  and the gatekeepers' power.
- **Pipelines do not branch or merge easily** -- physical pipelines
  are expensive to reroute. The metaphor imports rigidity into
  processes that could be more flexible. Once an organization builds
  a "pipeline," changing the sequence of stages feels like replumbing
  rather than simply changing a process. The dead metaphor literally
  concretizes (makes into concrete pipes) what should be a flexible
  workflow, resisting reorganization by making the current structure
  feel physical and permanent.
- **The infrastructure-industrial overtone** -- pipelines are
  associated with extractive industries (oil, gas, mining). The dead
  metaphor imports an extractive framing onto processes that might
  benefit from a different image. A "talent pipeline" frames people
  as a resource to be extracted and processed, not cultivated or
  attracted. A "content pipeline" frames creative work as industrial
  throughput. The extractive connotation is invisible but shapes how
  organizations think about the things moving through their pipes.

## Expressions

- "Sales pipeline" -- the sequence of stages from lead to closed deal,
  where potential revenue flows like oil through stages
- "Talent pipeline" -- the process of recruiting candidates through
  hiring stages, where people are treated as fluid
- "In the pipeline" -- currently being processed through sequential
  stages, where the speaker has forgotten the pipe entirely
- "Pipeline velocity" -- the speed at which items move through stages,
  borrowing flow-rate language from fluid dynamics
- "Pipeline leakage" -- loss of items between stages, where normal
  attrition is framed as infrastructure failure
- "Fill the pipeline" -- add more items to the early stages, where
  the pipe is being topped up like a reservoir
- "CI/CD pipeline" -- automated build-test-deploy sequence, where the
  fluid metaphor has died completely into "sequence of automated steps"

## Origin Story

"Pipeline" entered English in the 1850s as a literal compound: a line
of pipes. The first major oil pipeline (a 2-inch wrought-iron pipe
running 6 miles) was built in Pennsylvania in 1865 by Samuel Van Syckel,
replacing the expensive and unreliable system of horse-drawn barrel
wagons. By 1879, long-distance pipelines were carrying oil hundreds of
miles, and the word had become standard infrastructure vocabulary.

The metaphorical transfer began in the early 20th century. "In the
pipeline" appeared by the 1940s to mean "in process, being prepared."
The business usage ("sales pipeline") emerged in the 1950s and 1960s
as management science adopted engineering metaphors for organizational
processes. The software usage came later: Doug McIlroy's Unix pipes
(1973) used the plumbing metaphor explicitly, and "CI/CD pipeline"
became standard DevOps vocabulary in the 2010s.

The dead metaphor is now so pervasive that "pipeline" appears in
contexts with no connection to fluid flow. A "legislative pipeline"
has nothing to do with pipes; it simply means "bills being processed
in sequence." The word has become a pure synonym for "sequential
process," with the physical pipe entirely forgotten. Yet the fluid
implications persist: people speak of pipeline "flow," "capacity,"
"throughput," and "leakage" without realizing they are using
engineering vocabulary for social processes.
