## What It Brings

To cause something is to control what someone has. This metaphor is the
"object case" of the Event Structure metaphor system's model of causation,
the complement of the location case. Where the location case frames
causation as controlling where an entity is, the object case frames it as
controlling what a person possesses. If properties and states are
possessions (you "have" courage, patience, or a headache), then causing
someone to be in a state is giving them or taking from them the
corresponding possession. You give someone a headache, take away their
freedom, rob them of their dignity, or grant them permission.

Key structural parallels:

- **Causing a state as giving an object** -- "The news gave her a shock."
  "That experience gave him confidence." "The medicine gave her relief."
  The causal agent transfers a state-object to the affected person.
  Causation is a gift or delivery: the cause hands over the effect.
- **Removing a state as taking an object** -- "The scandal took away
  his credibility." "Age robbed her of her mobility." "The disease
  stripped him of his dignity." Causing someone to lose a state is
  taking their possession. The causal agent is a thief, a tax collector,
  or an authority confiscating property.
- **Preventing a state as withholding** -- "They denied her the
  opportunity." "He withheld his approval." "The committee refused to
  grant funding." Preventing an effect is refusing to hand over the
  object-state. The causal agent keeps the possession and does not
  transfer it.
- **Enabling a state as making available** -- "The scholarship offered
  her a chance at education." "The new law provides protection." "His
  generosity afforded them comfort." Enabling causation is making an
  object-state available for the other person to take. The cause does
  not force the effect but makes it accessible.
- **Causal responsibility as ownership of outcomes** -- "She owns that
  decision." "He bears the blame." "The team takes credit for the
  success." The causal agent possesses the effect they produced. If you
  caused it, it is yours -- both the credit and the blame are your
  property.

## Where It Breaks

- **The transfer model implies discrete, bounded effects** -- giving
  someone a headache frames the headache as a single object with clear
  boundaries. But most effects are diffuse, gradual, and overlapping.
  You cannot hand someone "a little bit of anxiety" the way you hand
  them half a sandwich. The object model forces discreteness onto
  continuous causal processes.
- **Giving implies the giver loses** -- when you give someone an object,
  you no longer have it. But when a cause produces an effect, the cause
  is not depleted. A contagious person who gives others their illness
  does not lose it. A teacher who gives students knowledge retains it.
  The transfer model imports a conservation law that does not hold for
  most causal relationships.
- **The model makes the affected party passive** -- in the object case,
  the affected person is a recipient: they receive the effect the way
  they receive a package. But real causal processes often require active
  participation from the affected party. Education, therapy, and skill
  development all require the "recipient" to do work. The object model
  hides this co-construction.
- **Responsibility is not ownership** -- "owning" an outcome implies
  the same kind of exclusive possession as owning a car. But causal
  responsibility is typically shared, partial, and contested. The
  object model pushes toward assigning singular ownership of effects
  to singular causes, which distorts the distributed nature of most
  real-world causation.
- **The two cases are not always interchangeable** -- Lakoff notes
  that the location case and object case are dual perspectives on the
  same causal relationship, but they are not always equally natural.
  "The therapy brought her out of depression" (location case) and
  "The therapy took away her depression" (object case) frame the same
  event differently, and the choice of framing carries different
  implications about agency, effort, and the nature of the state.

## Expressions

- "That gave me a headache" -- causing a state as transferring an object
- "She gave him the courage to try" -- enabling a state as gifting a
  possession
- "The scandal stripped him of his authority" -- removing a state as
  confiscating a possession
- "Age robbed her of her beauty" -- losing a property as being robbed
- "They denied her the opportunity" -- preventing a state as withholding
  an object
- "The new law provides protection" -- enabling a state as supplying
  goods
- "He took away her peace of mind" -- causing loss of state as theft
- "She bears the blame" -- causal responsibility as carrying a burden-
  object
- "He was granted permission" -- receiving a state as receiving a
  bestowed object
- "The discovery yielded new insights" -- producing effects as producing
  goods

## Origin Story

CAUSATION IS CONTROL OVER AN OBJECT RELATIVE TO A POSSESSOR is documented
in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson, and Schwartz, 1991) as the
object-case complement to CAUSATION IS CONTROL OVER AN ENTITY RELATIVE
TO A LOCATION. Together, these two metaphors form the complete causal
component of the Event Structure metaphor system.

The duality between the location case and the object case reflects a
fundamental duality in how English (and many other languages) structures
experience: the duality between being-somewhere and having-something.
Lakoff and Johnson develop this in *Philosophy in the Flesh* (1999),
showing that the same abstract change can be described in either
locational or possessional terms. "She fell into despair" (location) and
"despair overtook her" (possession) describe the same event through
different metaphorical lenses. The existence of both cases allows
speakers to highlight different aspects of causation: the location case
emphasizes where the affected entity ends up, while the object case
emphasizes what the affected entity gains or loses.
