## What It Brings

Abstract qualities are concrete things. Beauty, speed, intelligence,
fairness -- none of these exist as physical objects, yet we speak of them
as if they do. We can "encounter" kindness, "pursue" happiness, "grasp"
the truth, and "lose sight of" justice. The metaphor performs the most
fundamental ontological operation in human cognition: it turns properties,
which are predicated of things, into things themselves.

Key structural parallels:

- **Attributes as manipulable objects** -- once an attribute is an entity,
  you can do things to it. You can "build" confidence, "destroy" trust,
  "shape" opinion, and "sharpen" wit. The metaphor grants abstract
  qualities the physical affordances of solid objects: they can be grasped,
  moved, modified, and broken.
- **Attributes as agents** -- reified attributes can act on their own.
  "Curiosity killed the cat." "Jealousy consumed him." "Poverty breeds
  crime." When an attribute becomes an entity, it can become a causal
  agent, doing things to people rather than merely describing them. This
  is an enormous conceptual upgrade: it lets us talk about abstract
  causation using the familiar grammar of physical interaction.
- **Attributes as transferable goods** -- "She brings energy to every
  room." "He radiates confidence." "The speech lacked substance." If
  attributes are entities, they can move between locations and people.
  This grounds the common experience of interpersonal influence in a
  physics of thing-transfer.
- **Quantification** -- entities come in amounts. "A great deal of
  patience." "Not enough courage." "An excess of caution." The metaphor
  makes abstract qualities measurable by treating them as stuff that can
  be accumulated, depleted, or divided. This is the precondition for all
  scalar talk about qualities.
- **Spatial location** -- "Where did all the goodwill go?" "Creativity
  is found in unexpected places." "The answer lies in simplicity."
  Reified attributes occupy space. They can be present or absent, near
  or far, hidden or exposed. This extension makes it possible to talk
  about searching for, discovering, and locating abstract qualities.

## Where It Breaks

- **Attributes are not separable from their bearers** -- beauty does not
  exist apart from beautiful things. Courage does not float free of
  courageous people. The metaphor invents a world of free-floating
  qualities, which leads to philosophical puzzles (Plato's Forms, the
  problem of universals) that arise directly from taking the metaphor
  too literally. Treating "justice" as a thing to be found, rather than
  a quality of actions and institutions, generates centuries of
  unproductive philosophy.
- **Reification obscures process** -- "building trust" makes trust sound
  like a product, when it is actually a dynamic relationship maintained
  through ongoing behavior. The entity metaphor freezes what is fluid. It
  converts continuous processes into static things, hiding the work
  required to sustain them. Trust is not built once and then possessed; it
  is enacted daily or it dissolves.
- **Causal agency is misleading** -- "Poverty breeds crime" assigns causal
  power to an abstraction, obscuring the actual mechanisms (material
  deprivation, institutional failure, social exclusion) through which
  poverty correlates with crime. Reified attributes make bad explanations
  feel satisfying: naming the cause as an entity feels like identifying a
  culprit, even when the real causal structure is diffuse and systemic.
- **Quantification is often spurious** -- "a lot of intelligence" or "not
  enough empathy" imposes a scalar measure on qualities that may not be
  scalar. The metaphor creates the illusion that all attributes exist on a
  single dimension from less to more, which is why we end up with IQ
  scores and personality percentiles -- measurements that feel precise
  because the entity metaphor makes the underlying quality feel
  measurable.

## Expressions

- "Beauty is a rare thing" -- an attribute treated as a scarce physical
  entity
- "Curiosity killed the cat" -- an attribute as a causal agent
- "He possesses great intelligence" -- an attribute as a held object
  (overlaps with PROPERTIES ARE POSSESSIONS)
- "We need to find the truth" -- an attribute as a hidden entity to be
  discovered
- "Poverty breeds crime" -- an attribute as an organism that reproduces
- "She radiates confidence" -- an attribute as an entity that emits
  energy
- "That took a lot of courage" -- an attribute consumed as a resource
- "Justice demands action" -- an attribute as a person who issues
  commands
- "Where has all the trust gone?" -- an attribute as a movable entity
  that has departed

## Origin Story

ATTRIBUTES ARE ENTITIES is one of Lakoff and Johnson's foundational
ontological metaphors, discussed in Chapter 6 of *Metaphors We Live By*
(1980). They identify it as part of a systematic tendency to reify
abstractions: experiences are entities, activities are entities, states are
entities, and attributes are entities. The Master Metaphor List (1991)
catalogs it separately because the reification of attributes is
particularly consequential -- it is the precondition for an enormous range
of further metaphorical elaboration. Once beauty is a thing, it can be
pursued (PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS), possessed (PROPERTIES ARE
POSSESSIONS), contained (CATEGORIES ARE CONTAINERS), or personified
(ATTRIBUTES ARE PERSONS). The entity metaphor is not one metaphor among
many; it is the gateway that makes most other ontological metaphors
possible.
