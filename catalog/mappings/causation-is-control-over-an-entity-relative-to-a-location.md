## What It Brings

To cause something is to control where it goes. This metaphor is the
"location case" of the Event Structure metaphor system's model of
causation. It builds on two more basic metaphors -- STATES ARE LOCATIONS
and CHANGE IS MOTION -- and adds a causal agent who controls the motion.
If states are locations and changes are movements between locations, then
causing a change is controlling an entity's movement from one location to
another. You put someone in danger, send them into a panic, bring them to
their senses, or drive them to distraction.

Key structural parallels:

- **Causing a state as putting something in a place** -- "She put him in
  a difficult position." "The scandal placed the company in jeopardy."
  "He landed himself in trouble." The causal agent moves the affected
  entity into a state-location. Causation is spatial placement: you
  control where the thing ends up.
- **Preventing a state as blocking movement** -- "She kept him out of
  trouble." "The regulation held prices in check." "He was barred from
  participating." Preventing causation is preventing motion: the agent
  stops the entity from reaching a location-state. Barriers, walls,
  and restrictions are the tools of causal prevention.
- **Causing change as moving something between places** -- "The therapy
  brought her out of depression." "The policy moved families into
  poverty." "The discovery pushed the field into a new era." Change
  is relocation, and causing change is transporting an entity from one
  state-location to another. The agent controls the journey.
- **Degree of control as firmness of placement** -- "She firmly
  established the company in the market." "He loosely held the team
  together." "The law is deeply entrenched." How securely the entity
  is placed in its location-state reflects how strong or stable the
  causal effect is. A firm placement is a lasting effect; a loose one
  is tenuous.
- **Causal responsibility as custodial authority** -- "She's in charge
  of keeping things on track." "He's responsible for putting the project
  in this state." "Who got us into this mess?" The causal agent is a
  custodian or authority figure who controls where the entity is located.
  Causal responsibility maps onto jurisdictional authority.

## Where It Breaks

- **Not all causation involves a controlling agent** -- the metaphor
  requires someone or something that directs where the entity goes. But
  many causal processes are agentless: entropy, erosion, market forces,
  and evolutionary drift move things between states without any controller.
  The metaphor makes it difficult to conceptualize causation without
  asking "who put it there?" -- which is why people seek someone to blame
  for impersonal causal processes.
- **The location model makes states feel discrete** -- if states are
  places, you are either in one or not. "She put him in danger" implies
  a boundary between safe and dangerous. But many states are continuous
  gradients, not bounded locations. The metaphor forces categorical
  thinking about what may be a spectrum.
- **Control implies intentionality** -- the agent who places an entity
  in a location is typically acting deliberately. But many causes are
  unintentional: carelessness, negligence, and side effects all produce
  states without the agent aiming to produce them. The control metaphor
  makes unintentional causation feel like a contradiction, which is why
  "I didn't mean to put you in that position" sounds like a partial
  excuse -- the metaphor attributes more agency than was actually present.
- **The metaphor obscures self-caused states** -- "He put himself in
  danger" strains the mapping because the agent and the entity are the
  same. The spatial model works best with distinct mover and moved. When
  a person is both the controller and the controlled, the metaphor
  produces an odd image of someone picking themselves up and placing
  themselves somewhere.
- **Causal chains lose the spatial coherence** -- if A puts B in state X,
  and B puts C in state Y, the chain of custody becomes hard to track
  spatially. The location model works well for single-step causation but
  becomes unwieldy for multi-step causal chains where entities pass
  through many state-locations under different agents' control.

## Expressions

- "She put him in a difficult position" -- causing a state as placing in
  a location
- "The policy drove families into poverty" -- causing a state change as
  forced relocation
- "He got himself into trouble" -- self-causation as self-placement
- "She kept them out of harm's way" -- preventing a state as blocking
  entry to a location
- "The therapy brought her out of depression" -- reversing a state as
  extraction from a location
- "The discovery pushed the field into a new era" -- causing collective
  change as moving a group to a new location
- "He landed himself in hot water" -- arriving in a bad state as landing
  in a dangerous place
- "She was placed under suspicion" -- entering a state as being put in
  a location by an authority
- "The regulation held prices in check" -- preventing change as
  restraining movement
- "Who got us into this mess?" -- seeking the agent responsible for
  the current state-location

## Origin Story

This metaphor is the location case of Lakoff and Johnson's Event
Structure metaphor system, elaborated most fully in *Philosophy in the
Flesh* (1999). The Event Structure system is a coherent family of
metaphors that maps the physical world -- locations, movements, objects,
forces -- onto the abstract world of states, changes, causes, and
purposes. The location case treats states as locations and changes as
movements, so causation naturally becomes control over where entities
are located.

The Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson, and Schwartz, 1991)
distinguishes this location-case model of causation from the object-case
model (CAUSATION IS CONTROL OVER AN OBJECT RELATIVE TO A POSSESSOR),
where causing a change is transferring an object rather than relocating
an entity. The two cases are complementary perspectives on causation that
mirror the fundamental duality in the Event Structure system: the duality
between location (where you are) and possession (what you have).
