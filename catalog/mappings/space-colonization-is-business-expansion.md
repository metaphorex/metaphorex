## What It Brings

Science fiction has long imagined space colonization through the lens of
terrestrial colonial enterprises: trading companies, frontier towns,
resource extraction outposts. From the Weyland-Yutani Corporation in
*Alien* (1979) to the mining colonies of *The Expanse* (2011-2021),
SF consistently frames off-world expansion as a business venture. This
metaphor has migrated from fiction to reality: SpaceX, Blue Origin, and
asteroid mining startups explicitly frame their missions using the
language of market expansion, resource acquisition, and colonial
settlement.

Key structural parallels:

- **Colony as branch office** -- a colonial settlement is an outpost of
  the home civilization, governed by its laws and serving its interests.
  This maps directly onto how corporations frame international expansion:
  opening new markets, establishing regional offices, extending the
  brand's reach. Space companies use identical language -- "establishing
  a permanent presence on Mars" mirrors "establishing a permanent
  presence in the Asian market."
- **Resource extraction as revenue** -- colonial enterprises were funded
  by the promise of extractable wealth (gold, spices, furs). Space
  colonization is funded by the promise of extractable resources
  (helium-3, rare earth metals, asteroid minerals). The business case
  for space colonization is literally the colonial business case
  relocated to orbit.
- **The company town** -- many SF space settlements are owned by the
  corporation that built them. Workers live in company housing, buy
  from company stores, breathe company air. This maps onto the gig
  economy and platform dependency: when your employer controls your
  entire infrastructure, the employment relationship becomes total.
- **Manifest destiny as growth imperative** -- "expand or die" is both
  colonial ideology and corporate strategy. The metaphor frames
  planetary expansion as the natural continuation of market growth,
  making staying on Earth feel like stagnation rather than
  sustainability.

## Where It Breaks

- **Space has no indigenous population to exploit.** Terrestrial
  colonization was built on the subjugation of existing peoples. Space
  (so far) is empty. The metaphor imports an exploitative framework
  that may not apply -- but it also normalizes the exploitative mindset,
  which could matter if we ever do encounter non-human intelligence or
  when applied to relations between space settlers and Earth populations.
- **Colonial economics required cheap labor; space requires expensive
  expertise.** Historical colonies depended on enslaved or coerced
  labor. Space operations require highly trained specialists who are
  expensive and scarce. The colonial economic model (extract value
  through labor exploitation) does not translate to environments where
  every worker must be a specialist kept alive by costly infrastructure.
- **The return on investment timeline is fiction.** Colonial ventures
  (East India Company, Hudson's Bay Company) generated returns within
  years. Space colonization has no credible near-term business case --
  the cost of launching mass to orbit makes almost any space-extracted
  resource more expensive than its terrestrial equivalent. The metaphor
  imports a promise of profitability that physics does not support.
- **You cannot "settle" space the way you settle land.** Land is
  habitable by default. Space requires total environmental engineering
  -- atmosphere, radiation shielding, gravity, food production. The
  colonial metaphor makes space settlement sound like homesteading when
  it is more like submarine habitation: technically possible, miserable,
  and economically marginal.

## Expressions

- "Space colony" -- so naturalized it barely registers as metaphor,
  directly importing the colonial frame
- "Terraform" -- to make a planet Earth-like, the ultimate colonial
  act: remaking the territory to suit the colonizer
- "Off-world" -- from *Blade Runner*, used to describe any non-Earth
  operation, carrying connotations of exile and frontier opportunity
- "The billionaire space race" -- Bezos, Musk, and Branson framed as
  colonial-era explorers competing for territory and glory
- "Asteroid mining" -- the resource-extraction frame applied to space
  rocks, the modern equivalent of the colonial gold rush
- "Mars colony" -- used interchangeably with "Mars settlement" and
  "Mars base," each term importing different assumptions about
  permanence and purpose
- "New markets" -- corporate expansion language applied to literal
  new worlds

## Origin Story

The space-colonization-as-business metaphor traces to the earliest
space fiction but was made explicit by Robert Heinlein's commercial
space ventures (*The Man Who Sold the Moon*, 1950) and solidified by
the corporate villains of 1970s-1980s SF film. Weyland-Yutani in
*Alien* (1979) established the template: a corporation whose space
operations prioritize profit over human survival. *The Expanse*
updated this for the 21st century, depicting Belt miners as an
exploited colonial labor force. In reality, the metaphor became
operational when Elon Musk founded SpaceX (2002) and Jeff Bezos
founded Blue Origin (2000), both explicitly framing space access as
a business problem with colonial-era ambition.
