## What It Brings

An amorphous mass overhead -- shapeless, boundaryless, seemingly
everywhere and nowhere in particular. Network engineers drew cloud
shapes on diagrams to represent infrastructure they did not need to
detail: the phone network, the internet, someone else's problem. The
cloud symbol meant "complexity happens here, but you don't need to
understand it." That diagramming convention became the name of an
industry.

- **Abstraction as invisibility** -- a cloud obscures what is behind
  it. You see the cloud, not the sky above it. This maps precisely onto
  cloud computing's core promise: you use compute, storage, and
  networking without seeing the physical machines that provide them.
  The metaphor encodes abstraction as a visual property. What you
  cannot see, you do not need to manage.
- **Amorphous scale** -- clouds have no fixed shape, no clear edges,
  and they can be any size. This maps onto elastic computing: cloud
  resources scale up and down without fixed boundaries. You do not
  provision a specific server; you draw from the cloud. The metaphor
  makes infinite elasticity feel natural rather than engineered.
- **Overhead ubiquity** -- clouds are above you, everywhere you go.
  This maps onto the "access from anywhere" promise of cloud services.
  Your data is not on a specific machine in a specific room; it is in
  the cloud, available wherever you have a connection. The spatial
  metaphor (above, not beside) imports a sense of transcendence: the
  cloud is above the messy physical world of racks and cables.

## Where It Breaks

- **Clouds are not infrastructure** -- the most fundamental break. A
  real cloud is water vapor condensed around dust particles, shaped by
  wind and temperature, with no internal structure and no architect. A
  computing "cloud" is millions of physical servers in thousands of
  data centers, with extremely precise internal structure, maintained
  by hundreds of thousands of engineers. The metaphor's entire purpose
  is to hide this reality. "The cloud" is a deliberate mystification
  of very physical infrastructure.
- **The cloud has addresses** -- real clouds do not have locations. You
  cannot point to a specific cloud and say "my email is in that one."
  But cloud computing absolutely has locations: AWS us-east-1 is in
  Northern Virginia, eu-west-1 is in Ireland. Data sovereignty laws
  (GDPR, data residency requirements) force companies to know exactly
  where in the cloud their data lives. The amorphous metaphor actively
  interferes with understanding data jurisdiction. When a politician
  says "the data is in the cloud," the metaphor is doing real damage
  to policy reasoning.
- **Someone else's computer** -- the famous rebuttal: "there is no
  cloud; it is just someone else's computer." The metaphor obscures
  ownership, cost, and dependency. When AWS us-east-1 goes down, half
  the internet breaks. The cloud metaphor makes this event feel like
  weather (unpredictable, nobody's fault) rather than what it is: a
  single company's infrastructure failure in a specific building in
  Ashburn, Virginia. The abstraction that makes cloud computing easy
  to adopt also makes its risks easy to ignore.
- **Environmental cost vanishes** -- clouds are water. They are natural,
  clean, light. Data centers consume enormous quantities of electricity
  and water for cooling. The cloud metaphor sanitizes the environmental
  impact. Nobody thinks about carbon emissions when they "upload to the
  cloud." The ethereal image performs ideological work: it makes
  resource-intensive computation feel weightless.

## Expressions

- "In the cloud" -- the canonical location phrase, using spatial metaphor
  to describe a relationship with remote infrastructure; compare "on the
  server" (spatial, specific) versus "in the cloud" (spatial, vague)
- "Cloud-native" -- born in the cloud, as if the cloud were a homeland;
  implies applications designed for cloud infrastructure from the start
- "Cloud migration" -- moving from on-premises to cloud, using the
  journey metaphor; implies a one-way trip to a better place
- "Multi-cloud" -- using multiple cloud providers, as if there were
  multiple skies; the metaphor barely survives the pluralization
- "Cloud burst" -- a sudden spike in demand, borrowing from meteorology;
  a cloudburst is a sudden heavy rainfall, mapping volume onto compute
  demand
- "Above the cloud line" -- the boundary between local and cloud
  infrastructure, using the altitude metaphor; below the cloud line is
  your responsibility, above it is the provider's
- "There is no cloud, just someone else's computer" -- the counter-
  metaphor, a bumper sticker that performs the resurrection the dead
  metaphor needs

## Origin Story

The cloud symbol in network diagrams dates to at least the early 1970s.
Telecommunications engineers used a cloud shape to represent the
telephone network: calls went into the cloud at one end and came out at
the other, and the complexity in between was not your concern. The
symbol appeared in ARPANET diagrams and early internet architecture
documents.

The term "cloud computing" crystallized in the mid-2000s. Google's Eric
Schmidt used it at a Search Engine Strategies conference in August 2006.
Amazon Web Services launched its Elastic Compute Cloud (EC2) the same
month. But the idea and the diagram symbol were decades older. The
2006 moment was when the diagramming convention became a product
category.

The metaphor died with remarkable speed. By 2010, "cloud" was a
standard term in enterprise IT budgets. By 2015, it was a consumer
term (iCloud, Google Drive, Dropbox all "put things in the cloud").
By 2020, the metaphor was so dead that "cloud-native" could be coined
without anyone finding it strange that a computing paradigm was being
described as native to a weather phenomenon.

The counter-metaphor ("someone else's computer") emerged around 2014,
attributed to various sources. It functions as a resurrection attempt:
it tries to kill the cloud metaphor by making the physical reality
visible again. Its popularity suggests the cloud metaphor's abstraction
has become a problem, not just a convenience.
