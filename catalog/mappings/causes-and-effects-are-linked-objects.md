## What It Brings

Causes and their effects are objects physically connected to each other --
joined by chains, links, ropes, or bonds. When you pull one, the other
moves. This metaphor gives causation a tangible structure: causes and
effects are discrete things, and the causal relationship between them is
a physical attachment that transmits influence from one to the other. The
connection is visible, traceable, and potentially breakable.

Key structural parallels:

- **Causal connection as physical linkage** -- "There's a clear link between
  poverty and crime." "Smoking is connected to lung cancer." "The two events
  are tied together." The causal relation is not an abstract logical
  necessity but a concrete attachment. You can see it, trace it, and point
  to it. If two things are causally related, there is something between
  them -- a link, a tie, a bond -- that makes one move when the other does.
- **Causal chains as linked sequences** -- "A chain of events led to the
  disaster." "One thing was linked to the next." "The causal chain goes
  back decades." When multiple causes and effects are connected in sequence,
  the metaphor produces a chain: each link transmits force to the next.
  The chain has a beginning (the first cause) and an end (the final
  effect), and you can trace it link by link.
- **Causal strength as bond strength** -- "There's a strong connection
  between the two." "The link is tenuous." "The evidence weakly ties the
  suspect to the crime." Strong causation is a tight bond; weak causation
  is a loose one. The metaphor gives causal strength a physical analog:
  how hard would you have to pull to break the connection?
- **Breaking causation as severing a link** -- "We need to break the cycle
  of poverty." "She cut the connection between those two departments."
  "The link was severed." If causal connection is physical attachment,
  then ending a causal relationship is cutting, breaking, or severing.
  Intervention is a physical act on the connection itself.
- **Missing causation as disconnection** -- "The two events are unrelated."
  "I see no connection." "There's a missing link in the argument." When no
  causal relationship exists, the objects float free -- unlinked, unattached,
  independent. An unexplained gap in a causal sequence is a missing link.

## Where It Breaks

- **The metaphor makes causation binary** -- objects are either linked or
  not linked. But real causation is often probabilistic, partial, or
  graded. Smoking does not cause lung cancer the way pulling a chain moves
  the next link. The metaphor has no natural representation for "A raises
  the probability of B by 30%." It pushes toward "connected or not," which
  distorts statistical and probabilistic reasoning.
- **Links are symmetrical but causation is not** -- a physical chain
  transmits force in both directions. If A is linked to B, pulling A
  moves B and pulling B moves A. But causation is directional: the cause
  produces the effect, not the other way around. The metaphor must rely on
  context or additional language ("A leads to B") to impose asymmetry on
  an inherently symmetrical source domain.
- **The metaphor hides causal mechanisms** -- a link connects two objects
  but says nothing about how the connection works. "There's a link between
  stress and heart disease" gestures at a causal relationship without
  specifying the mechanism (cortisol levels, blood pressure, inflammatory
  response). The metaphor can create an illusion of explanation where only
  correlation has been established.
- **Chains suggest linearity** -- the chain metaphor arranges causes and
  effects in a single line. But real causal structures are often networks:
  multiple causes converge on one effect, one cause fans out to multiple
  effects, and feedback loops create circular causation. The chain is a
  one-dimensional representation of a multi-dimensional reality.
- **Linked objects are passive** -- in the metaphor, causes and effects
  just sit there, connected. The link does not change them. But in reality,
  being caused often transforms the effect: the effect is not the same
  entity after the causal process as it was before. The metaphor preserves
  the identity of the linked objects in a way that obscures transformative
  causation.

## Expressions

- "There's a link between diet and health" -- causal relationship as
  physical connection
- "A chain of events" -- sequential causation as linked objects in series
- "The missing link" -- an unexplained gap in a causal or evolutionary
  sequence
- "Break the cycle" -- ending a recurring causal pattern by severing a
  connection
- "Connected to the outcome" -- causally related as physically joined
- "A strong connection between the variables" -- causal strength as bond
  strength
- "Unrelated events" -- absence of causation as absence of physical linkage
- "Everything is tied together" -- multiple causal relationships as a
  network of bonds
- "The weakest link" -- the most vulnerable point in a causal chain
- "Sever the connection" -- ending a causal relationship by cutting

## Origin Story

This metaphor is cataloged in the Master Metaphor List (Lakoff, Espenson &
Schwartz 1991) as part of the causation cluster. It sits alongside CAUSES
ARE FORCES and CAUSATION IS CONTROL OVER RELATIVE LOCATION as one of the
major metaphorical systems through which English speakers understand causal
relationships. While CAUSES ARE FORCES emphasizes the dynamic, push-pull
aspect of causation, CAUSES AND EFFECTS ARE LINKED OBJECTS emphasizes the
structural, relational aspect -- the fact that causes and effects are bound
together in persistent connections that can be traced, strengthened, or
broken.

The metaphor is grounded in the infant's experience of physical linkage:
pulling a toy on a string, watching one block knock over another in a chain,
discovering that connected objects move together. The correlation between
physical attachment and co-movement provides the experiential basis for
understanding abstract causal connection.
