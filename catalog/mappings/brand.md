## What It Brings

Old Norse brandr -- "to burn." Livestock were branded with hot irons to
mark ownership. The brand was a wound that healed into a permanent sign:
this animal belongs to this rancher. The metaphor maps the physical
marking of property onto commercial identity. Every use of "brand" in
marketing -- brand loyalty, brand recognition, brand equity, brand
identity, brand ambassador -- is built on a dead metaphor about burning
cattle with iron.

- **Permanence through pain** -- the core structural import is that
  identity is inscribed through a violent, irreversible act. A brand
  is not painted on; it is burned in. The metaphor imports the idea
  that commercial identity should be indelible -- once established,
  it should be as hard to remove as a scar. Modern branding strategy
  explicitly pursues this: brand consultants talk about "burning the
  brand into the consumer's mind," often without realizing they are
  restating the literal etymology. The permanence-through-violence
  structure persists even when the violence is forgotten.
- **Ownership and property** -- branding cattle marked them as
  property. The brand said "this belongs to me." When the metaphor
  transferred to commerce, it carried this ownership claim: a branded
  product belongs to its maker in a way that an unbranded product does
  not. Trademark law formalizes this structure -- a brand is a legal
  claim of ownership over an identity. The livestock metaphor also
  imports the idea that branded things are commodities: cattle are
  interchangeable except for their brands, and the brand is what
  distinguishes one rancher's identical-looking cows from another's.
- **The brand as the rancher's reputation** -- a cattle brand did
  not just identify ownership; it carried the rancher's reputation.
  A respected rancher's brand meant healthy animals, fair dealing,
  reliable stock. A disreputable rancher's brand meant the opposite.
  The metaphor maps this reputational function directly onto commercial
  branding: a brand is not merely an identifier but a compressed
  promise. "Brand trust" and "brand promise" are modern marketing
  concepts that the cattle-branding metaphor encoded from the start.

## Where It Breaks

- **The branded entity has no say** -- cattle do not choose to be
  branded. The brand is imposed by the owner on the property. When
  the metaphor transferred to commercial products, this power
  structure was preserved: companies brand their products, and the
  product has no say. But when "branding" extended to people --
  "personal brand," "employer brand," "brand yourself" -- the
  metaphor became grotesque under examination. A person who "brands
  themselves" is simultaneously the rancher and the cattle, the
  burner and the burned. The dead metaphor hides the fact that
  self-branding is, etymologically, self-mutilation for the purpose
  of declaring oneself property.
- **The violence is entirely invisible** -- nobody thinks about
  burning when they say "brand." The sanitization is so complete
  that branding is now associated with creativity, design, and
  aspiration -- the opposite of its source domain. Brand agencies
  employ graphic designers, not blacksmiths. The dead metaphor
  underwent a complete emotional inversion: from pain and
  subjugation to pleasure and aspiration. This inversion is itself
  revealing -- it shows how thoroughly a dead metaphor can be
  repurposed once the source domain is forgotten.
- **Brands were designed for interchangeable goods** -- cattle
  branding exists because individual cows look alike. If every cow
  were visually unique, branding would be unnecessary. The metaphor
  imports the assumption that products are fundamentally
  interchangeable and that the brand is what creates differentiation.
  This assumption is accurate for commodities (gasoline, flour,
  salt) but misleading for differentiated products (software, art,
  professional services). When a software company invests heavily in
  "branding," the dead metaphor is encouraging it to treat its
  unique product as an interchangeable commodity that needs a burn
  mark to stand out.
- **The historical echo of human branding** -- the branding of
  livestock was not the only branding. Enslaved people were branded.
  Criminals were branded. The technology of the hot iron was applied
  to human bodies as a mark of ownership and punishment. The dead
  metaphor's sanitization does not merely forget the cattle; it
  also forgets the people. When a company talks about "branding its
  workforce" or an employee talks about their "personal brand," the
  layers of forgotten violence include not just animal husbandry but
  chattel slavery. The dead metaphor is not merely dead; it is
  buried under layers of intentional forgetting.

## Expressions

- "Brand name" -- a product identified by its commercial mark,
  where the burning iron has become a logo
- "Brand loyalty" -- consumer attachment to a particular commercial
  identity, where the livestock metaphor maps the animal's inability
  to remove its brand onto the consumer's reluctance to switch
- "Brand equity" -- the financial value of a brand's reputation,
  where the rancher's reputation has been quantified into an
  accounting line item
- "Rebrand" -- to change a commercial identity, which in the source
  domain would mean burning over the old brand with a new one
- "Personal brand" -- an individual's curated public identity,
  where the person is simultaneously brander and branded
- "Off-brand" -- inconsistent with an established identity, or
  generic and unbranded, where the absence of a burn mark means
  the absence of identity
- "Brand new" -- completely new, from the sense of something freshly
  made in a fire, where "brand" retains its burning origin
  independently of the commercial meaning

## Origin Story

Old Norse brandr meant "sword" or "burning piece of wood" -- a firebrand.
The verb form meant "to burn" or "to mark by burning." The practice of
burning ownership marks into livestock hides dates to at least ancient
Egypt (cattle brands appear in tomb paintings from 2700 BCE), but the
English word traces through Norse and Germanic roots.

In the American West, cattle branding became a systematic practice in the
18th and 19th centuries. Brand registries were maintained by counties and
states, and a rancher's brand was as legally significant as a modern
trademark. "Rustling" (stealing branded cattle) was a hanging offense in
part because it involved altering or counterfeiting a brand -- an
early form of trademark infringement enforced at gunpoint.

The metaphorical transfer to commercial products began in the 19th
century. Bass Brewery registered what is often cited as the first
trademark (a red triangle) in 1876. But the language of "branding" for
commercial identity did not become dominant until the mid-20th century,
when advertising agencies began talking about "building a brand" as a
deliberate strategy. Procter & Gamble's "brand management" system,
formalized in the 1930s, institutionalized the metaphor: each product
line had a "brand manager" who was, etymologically, the person
responsible for the burn mark.

By the late 20th century, "brand" had completed its metaphorical
journey from physical act to abstract concept. Naomi Klein's *No Logo*
(2000) critiqued the expansion of branding into every domain of culture,
but the title itself -- "no logo" -- shows that the brand had already
become a visual mark rather than a burn. The iron was forgotten; the
image remained.
