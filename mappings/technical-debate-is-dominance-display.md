## What It Brings

Reframes unproductive technical arguments as ritualized status competition
rather than rational discourse. This is genuinely useful — once you *see*
a design review as a dominance display, you can name the behavior, short-
circuit it, and redirect toward actual technical evaluation.

Key structural parallels:

- **Costly signals** — participants invest time preparing elaborate counter-
  arguments or demos not to find truth but to demonstrate capability.
- **Audience matters** — the behavior intensifies when senior engineers or
  managers are watching, just as animal displays intensify with observers.
- **Outcome is rank, not truth** — the "winner" of a penis-fencing design
  review isn't whoever has the best architecture, but whoever established
  dominance. The technical decision may be incidental.
- **Ritualized, not lethal** — like animal dominance displays, the point
  is to establish hierarchy *without* destroying the loser. Nobody gets
  fired; someone just gets to say "I told you so" for the next quarter.

## Where It Breaks

- **Not all disagreement is posturing.** The metaphor can cynically dismiss
  legitimate technical debate as mere ego. Sometimes people argue because
  the architecture genuinely matters.
- **Dominance displays are instinctive; technical debate is (sometimes)
  rational.** Mapping one onto the other risks fatalism — "this is just
  what primates do" — when the behavior is actually addressable through
  better facilitation.
- **The biological source implies inevitability.** Animal displays are
  evolved and species-wide. Technical posturing is cultural and changeable.

## Expressions

- "Let's skip past the penis-fencing phase of this design review" —
  directly names the flatworm reproductive behavior where hermaphroditic
  flatworms fence with penis-like organs; the loser bears the metabolic
  cost of reproduction. Maps precisely: both participants can "penetrate"
  (contribute), neither wants to bear the cost (maintain the other's code),
  and the whole ritual could be skipped by cooperating.
- "This meeting is just a pissing contest" — territory-marking as
  expertise-signaling (mammalian variant, less structurally specific)
- "Stop measuring and start building" — implicit reference to the
  measurement/comparison ritual

## Origin Story

The flatworm reference (penis fencing) comes from the reproductive biology
of *Pseudobiceros* flatworms, documented by Michiels and Newman (1998).
The metaphorical application to tech culture appears to be informal/oral
tradition — common in engineering teams but rarely written down, likely
because the imagery is too vivid for corporate documentation.
